Background

The previous analysis of this dataset established a macro-level capital paradox: micro-enterprises constitute the majority of registered Brazilian firms yet control a disproportionately small share of total capital stock. That finding is important, but it treats each size category as internally homogeneous. It is not.

Brazil’s official firm-size classification (Simples Nacional) is based on annual gross revenue, not capital stock. A sole proprietor earning 360,000 BRL per year is legally a “micro-enterprise” regardless of whether they hold 180,000 BRL or 10,000,000 BRL in declared capital stock. This classification logic creates a natural experiment embedded in the data: firms within the same regulatory category can have wildly different capital endowments. The spread within size tiers is as analytically interesting as the gaps between them.

A second underexplored dimension is governance. The owner_qualification field records the legal role of the controlling person: a Managing Partner who actively runs the business, a Beneficial Owner who holds equity but may be a passive investor or part of a holding-company chain, or a solo Entrepreneur operating under their own identity. These governance archetypes map onto well- established predictions from agency theory: passive beneficial ownership is associated with access to external capital, while sole entrepreneurs face the highest information asymmetries with lenders.

Research Questions

Three analytical questions guide this extended analysis:

  1. Within-tier inequality: Are micro-enterprises themselves internally unequal in capital endowments, or is the category relatively flat?
  2. Governance as a capital predictor: Does owner qualification predict capital accumulation independently of firm size, and by how much?
  3. The “capital-rich micro”: Among micro-enterprises with unusually high capital stock, what governance and legal-form features distinguish them from the capital-poor majority?

Visualization 1 — Within-Tier Inequality: Lorenz Curves by Firm Size

Show code
# Lorenz curve: cumulative firm share (x) vs cumulative capital share (y)
lorenz_data <- co |>
  group_by(size) |>
  arrange(capital_stock, .by_group = TRUE) |>
  mutate(
    cum_firms   = row_number() / n(),
    cum_capital = cumsum(capital_stock) / sum(capital_stock)
  ) |>
  ungroup()

# Gini coefficient helper
gini <- function(x) {
  x <- sort(x[!is.na(x)])
  n <- length(x)
  2 * sum((seq_len(n) / n) * (x / sum(x))) - (n + 1) / n
}

gini_labels <- co |>
  group_by(size) |>
  summarise(g = round(gini(capital_stock), 3), .groups = "drop") |>
  mutate(
    label = paste0("Gini = ", g),
    x = 0.25, y = c(0.68, 0.58, 0.48)
  )

pal_size <- c("Micro" = "#e76f51", "Small" = "#f4a261", "Medium/Large" = "#264653")

ggplot(lorenz_data, aes(x = cum_firms, y = cum_capital, colour = size)) +
  # Perfect equality diagonal
  geom_abline(intercept = 0, slope = 1,
              linetype = "dashed", colour = "grey60", linewidth = 0.6) +
  geom_line(linewidth = 1.1, alpha = 0.9) +
  # Shade area between Micro and equality line to make inequality visual
  geom_ribbon(
    data = filter(lorenz_data, size == "Micro"),
    aes(ymin = cum_capital, ymax = cum_firms),
    fill = "#e76f51", alpha = 0.08, colour = NA
  ) +
  geom_text(
    data = gini_labels,
    aes(x = x, y = y, label = label, colour = size),
    inherit.aes = FALSE,
    fontface = "bold", size = 3.8
  ) +
  annotate("text", x = 0.57, y = 0.47, label = "Perfect equality",
           colour = "grey50", size = 3.3, angle = 42) +
  scale_colour_manual(values = pal_size, name = "Firm size") +
  scale_x_continuous(labels = percent_format(), name = "Cumulative share of firms →") +
  scale_y_continuous(labels = percent_format(), name = "← Cumulative share of capital") +
  labs(
    title    = "Micro-enterprises are internally the most unequal: Gini > 0.6 within the tier",
    subtitle = "Lorenz curves by firm size. The further the curve bows below the diagonal, the more concentrated capital is\namong a small fraction of firms within that category.",
    caption  = "Source: Brazilian CNPJ Registry · TidyTuesday 2026 Wk 4 (Extended) · Zhijun He · 2026-02-25"
  ) +
  theme_minimal(base_size = 13) +
  theme(
    plot.title    = element_text(face = "bold", size = 14, colour = "#264653"),

The Lorenz curves reveal that the capital trap is not simply a gap between size categories — it is also a profound concentration within micro-enterprises. A Gini coefficient above 0.60 for micro-enterprises is striking: it means that among firms that have all been designated “micro” by the same revenue-based rule, a small minority holds the majority of declared capital. Medium/Large firms are actually the most internally equal (Gini ≈ 0.43), consistent with the idea that formal capitalization requirements for larger corporate structures compress the distribution. Small enterprises sit in between. The upshot is that “micro-enterprise” is not a coherent economic category — it is a legal label that shelters both subsistence operations and well-capitalised ventures under the same regulatory umbrella.

Across all three size tiers, a clear hierarchy emerges from governance structure alone. Firms controlled by a Beneficial Owner — typically a holding company, trust, or passive equity holder — show systematically higher capital stock than firms run by a Managing Partner, which in turn outperform sole entrepreneurs. This governance premium persists within every size category: even among micro-enterprises, a Beneficial Owner-controlled firm holds a materially higher median capital than a sole entrepreneur of identical regulatory status. The implication is significant for development policy: size classification (a revenue proxy) and governance structure (an ownership proxy) are two orthogonal dimensions of firm capitalization, and conflating them understates the heterogeneity within each tier. A micro- enterprise programme that treats all micro-enterprises alike implicitly subsidizes governance-rich firms alongside genuinely capital-constrained ones.

# Identify what proportion of "capital-rich" micros belong to each legal/governance cell
micro_profile <- co |>
  filter(size == "Micro", governance != "Other") |>
  mutate(
    legal_short = case_when(
      str_detect(legal_nature, "LLC")              ~ "LLC",
      str_detect(legal_nature, "Sole Proprietorship") ~ "Sole\nProprietary",
      str_detect(legal_nature, "Individual Limited") ~ "EIRELI",
      str_detect(legal_nature, "Simple")           ~ "Simple\nPartnership",
      str_detect(legal_nature, "Silent")           ~ "Silent\nPartnership",
      str_detect(legal_nature, "Corporation")      ~ "Corporation",
      TRUE                                          ~ "Other"
    )
  )

# Rate of "capital-rich" membership by legal × governance cell
escape_rate <- micro_profile |>
  group_by(legal_short, governance) |>
  summarise(
    n_total    = n(),
    n_rich     = sum(is_capital_rich_micro),
    escape_pct = n_rich / n_total,
    .groups    = "drop"

  filter(n_total >= 20)  # remove sparse cells

ggplot(

The final visualization identifies the escape route from the capital trap at a granular level. Among micro-enterprises, the probability of being in the top capital quartile varies substantially by the combination of legal form and governance archetype. Beneficial Owner–controlled LLCs have the highest escape rate, followed by Beneficial Owner–controlled EIRELIs. Sole Entrepreneurs in sole proprietorships — the most structurally isolated governance form — have the lowest rates of capital-rich status.

The structural interpretation is straightforward: Beneficial Ownership signals the presence of a holding-company chain or external equity investor behind what is officially registered as a micro-enterprise. These are not small businesses in the policy-relevant sense; they are subsidiaries or investment vehicles wearing micro-enterprise clothing because their revenue happens to fall below the Simples Nacional threshold, even though their capital endowment is substantial. The EIRELI (Individual Limited Liability Company) pattern is similarly suggestive: this legal form was specifically created to allow single-person companies to separate personal from business liability, and firms using it show elevated capital retention relative to informal sole proprietorships.

Synthesis

This extended analysis reframes the capital trap finding in three directions. First, the trap operates at two scales simultaneously: the between-tier gap documented previously, and a severe within-tier Gini of 0.6 inside micro-enterprises that makes the category a statistical fiction for capital analysis purposes.

Second, governance structure is an independent predictor of capital accumulation. A Beneficial Owner-controlled micro-enterprise has systematically higher capital than a sole entrepreneur with the same regulatory label. Any policy that targets “micro-enterprises” as a homogeneous group will inevitably redistribute from capital-constrained genuine small businesses toward governance-rich investment vehicles.

Third, the capital-rich micro profile is not random. It concentrates in LLC and EIRELI structures under Beneficial Ownership — a signature consistent with holding-company architecture rather than organic small-business growth. This has a direct diagnostic implication: if Brazil’s development goal is to reduce the capital gap for genuinely small entrepreneurs, the targeting instrument should incorporate governance structure alongside revenue-based size classification. Revenue thresholds alone are insufficient — and this dataset shows precisely why.

distribution. Small enterprises sit in between. The upshot is that "micro-enterprise" is not a
coherent economic category — it is a legal label that shelters both subsistence operations
and well-capitalised ventures under the same regulatory umbrella.

## Synthesis

This extended analysis reframes the capital trap finding in three directions. First, the trap
operates at two scales simultaneously: the *between-tier* gap documented previously, and a
severe *within-tier* Gini of 0.6 inside micro-enterprises that makes the category a statistical
fiction for capital analysis purposes.

Second, governance structure is an independent predictor of capital accumulation. A Beneficial
Owner-controlled micro-enterprise has systematically higher capital than a sole entrepreneur
with the same regulatory label. Any policy that targets "micro-enterprises" as a homogeneous
group will inevitably redistribute from capital-constrained genuine small businesses toward
governance-rich investment vehicles.

Third, the capital-rich micro profile is not random. It concentrates in LLC and EIRELI structures
under Beneficial Ownership — a signature consistent with holding-company architecture rather
than organic small-business growth. This has a direct diagnostic implication: if Brazil's
development goal is to reduce the capital gap for genuinely small entrepreneurs, the
targeting instrument should incorporate governance structure alongside revenue-based size
classification. Revenue thresholds alone are insufficient — and this dataset shows precisely why.
