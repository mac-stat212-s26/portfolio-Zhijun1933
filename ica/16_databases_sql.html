🔥 Data Story Critique — UNODC Synthetic Drug Strategy 2021–2025

Source: https://vvv.vev.site/unodc-syntheticdrugstrategy-en/

What is the data story?

The piece is the public-facing Vev microsite for UNODC’s (United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime) five-year Synthetic Drug Strategy. It opens on the structural claim that defines the document’s thesis: despite escalating law-enforcement seizures and rising user populations, synthetic-drug prices have been falling while purity has been maintained or increasing — a market signal that UNODC explicitly labels a “perfect storm.” Downstream sections develop the explosion of New Psychoactive Substances (6× growth since 2009 to 1,047 unique substances; ~80 new substances per year), the gender dimension of synthetic-drug use and treatment, and the organization’s “Four Spheres of Action” framework (monitoring, health, law enforcement, international cooperation). The microsite closes with a signed quote from Executive Director Ghada Waly citing a 71% rise in opioid-use-disorder deaths and “record high” methamphetamine seizures.

A framing note before the critique: UNODC is a first-rate drug-research institution, and its flagship World Drug Report is the global reference document for this data. The microsite is not UNODC’s analytical ceiling — it’s the register the organization chose for non-specialist public communication. The critique therefore targets editorial and institutional-positioning choices, not research capacity.

What is effective?

The price-purity-seizure co-movement is a genuinely rigorous economic diagnostic. Standard supply-and-demand logic predicts that rising seizures combined with rising user populations should either increase prices or reduce purity. Observing the opposite — falling prices, maintained or rising purity, rising user counts, rising seizures — is a signature of supply decoupling from interdiction: production capacity has scaled past what enforcement can intercept, making seizures a rounding error on the supply curve rather than a binding constraint. UNODC articulating this publicly is consequential because it is the UN body responsible for coordinating supply-interdiction regimes; the diagnosis implicitly concedes that the past five decades’ dominant policy tool has lost marginal effectiveness for this drug class. That is institutional candor, and it deserves credit.

The NPS figures carry the sharpest policy implication in the document. A 6× increase in the number of catalogued new psychoactive substances since 2009, with approximately one new molecule introduced every 4.5 days on average, means the international scheduling system — which operates through the 1961, 1971, and 1988 Conventions with scheduling processes measured in ~18-month units — is structurally unable to keep pace. This is the most analytically compelling numerical fact in the microsite and the one with the clearest regulatory-design consequence: the scheduling-based control paradigm has been outflanked by the chemistry.

“Sought-after commodities” is a deliberate framing choice. By placing synthetic drugs inside an economic framework — a market with supply, demand, producers, and intermediaries — rather than a moralistic framework (“evil substances”) or a purely clinical one (“public health crisis”), UNODC is signaling an analytical register appropriate to the problem’s actual structure. For a UN enforcement-oriented agency, this is non-trivial.

Women-and-drugs appears as a top-level section, not a footnote. Sex-disaggregated patterns in synthetic-drug use, treatment-access gaps for women, and the specific outcomes for female users are well-documented but routinely marginal in enforcement narratives. Placing this as a peer section rather than an appendix is a small editorial choice that signals the right analytical frame.

What could be improved?

The diagnosis and the prescription do not align. If the “perfect storm” claim is correct — production capacity has so outstripped interdiction that the marginal dollar spent on enforcement returns near zero in supply terms — then the policy conclusion should be a reallocation of effort toward demand reduction, harm reduction, treatment expansion, and precursor-chemical governance. But the “Four Spheres of Action” framework, when expanded, continues to weight law enforcement as a coequal sphere without revisiting its marginal-return logic. The document diagnoses A and prescribes B, without acknowledging the tension. This is the microsite’s most important unresolved problem.

Measurement uncertainty in the public version is flatter than in UNODC’s own technical documents. The price and purity indices anchoring the “perfect storm” argument come from seizure-based lab analysis, undercover purchases, and user self-report — each with systematic biases that the World Drug Report’s methodology annexes discuss at length. The lab-analysis purity data, in particular, reflects only what was seized, and if interdiction preferentially captures certain product categories (bulk shipments vs. street-level inventory), purity trends partly index sampling rather than market reality. The public microsite presents the co-movement as self-evident; the organization’s own technical documentation acknowledges it is partly interpretive. The register shift matters because the public version carries weight in policy debates where the caveats in the annexes are invisible.

The opioid citation at the close is rhetorical rather than analytical. The microsite is thematically about amphetamine-type stimulants and NPS; the mechanisms driving the opioid crisis (prescription-opioid-to-heroin-to-illicit-fentanyl transition in North America) are distinct from the mechanisms driving synthetic-stimulant expansion (precursor-control evasion, modular small-batch production). Appending “opioid-use-disorder deaths up 71%” to the executive-director quote bundles these under a unified urgency frame that the document’s own analysis does not support. This is the genre convention of closing on an affect-heavy number, but it muddies the analytical case the rest of the piece built.

Demand-side dynamics go un-interrogated. The microsite reasons as if the demand curve has been static while supply has expanded. But synthetic-drug user demographics over the past decade have shifted substantially — from recreational male users to a more mixed population, from urban middle-class to rural working-class in several regions, from exclusively party contexts to functional and workplace use. A demand curve that has become more elastic because new price-sensitive cohorts have entered the market is consistent with the same observations (falling prices, rising quantities) without requiring the supply-side-only explanation the piece leans on. The analytical cost of not naming this is that readers infer supply interdiction failed when part of the story is that demand itself shifted.

Geographic heterogeneity is flattened into “global.” Synthetic-drug markets are strongly regional in ways that matter for policy. Captagon’s concentration in the Middle East (with well-documented Syrian state involvement in the production chain), methamphetamine’s distinct markets in North America versus East/Southeast Asia versus Oceania, the ketamine supply chain’s different geography — these are different political economies, not instances of one problem. The “1 in 200 of the global population” per-capita figure is the flattening stripped to a single number. UNODC has the regional data; the microsite chose not to surface it.

Proportionality. UNODC is a top-tier research institution and this is a public communications document, not its analytical output. The critiques above are about the translation from research to public communication — a hard editorial problem that all such institutions face. Within that constraint, the diagnostic framing is sharp and the NPS point is genuinely important. The next version of this document should close the diagnosis-prescription gap, import the technical-annex uncertainty language, and let geography and demand carry their own weight.

Setting Up a Local Database with DuckDB

DuckDB is an in-process columnar database — fast, zero-configuration, and well-suited to interactive analysis. We create an in-memory database, write two nycflights13 tables to it, and then query them through dbplyr.

Code
con <- DBI::dbConnect(duckdb::duckdb())

# Write datasets to the database
dbWriteTable(con, "flights", nycflights13::flights)
dbWriteTable(con, "planes",  nycflights13::planes)

# Confirm what lives in the database
dbListTables(con)
[1] "flights" "planes"
Code
# `tbl()` returns a lazy table — no data is pulled into R until needed
flights <- tbl(con, "flights")
planes  <- tbl(con, "planes")

class(flights)
[1] "tbl_duckdb_connection" "tbl_dbi"               "tbl_sql"              
[4] "tbl_lazy"              "tbl"
Code
dim(flights)   # nrow is NA because the data hasn't been loaded
[1] NA 19

The dim() returning NA for rows is intentional. A remote table is a handle to something that lives in the database, not a materialized object — dbplyr translates R verbs into SQL, ships the SQL to the database, and only pulls the result back when asked with collect() or when a value is needed for printing.

dplyr ↔︎ SQL Translation

A concise mapping

tidyverse verb SQL clause notes
filter(col == x) WHERE col = x single = for equality in SQL
filter(a & b) WHERE a AND b
filter(a \| b) WHERE a OR b
filter(col %in% c(...)) WHERE col IN (...)
filter(!is.na(col)) WHERE col IS NOT NULL
select(a, b) SELECT a, b FROM table
rename(new = old) SELECT old AS new
mutate(new = expr) SELECT ..., expr AS new
arrange(col) ORDER BY col ascending by default
arrange(desc(col)) ORDER BY col DESC
head(n) LIMIT n TOP(n) in T-SQL
group_by(col) GROUP BY col
summarize(x = sum(y)) aggregate in SELECT no explicit summarize keyword
inner_join(...) INNER JOIN ... ON ...
left_join(...) LEFT JOIN ... ON ...

Building the translation with show_query()

show_query() is the pedagogical key — it prints the SQL that dbplyr would send to the database for any pipeline. Seeing the translation side-by-side is the fastest way to internalize both.

Code
flights |>
    filter(dest == "IAH") |>
    arrange(dep_delay) |>
    show_query()
<SQL>
SELECT flights.*
FROM flights
WHERE (dest = 'IAH')
ORDER BY dep_delay
Code
flights |>
    filter(dest == "IAH") |>
    arrange(dep_delay) |>
    head(n = 10) |>
    show_query()
<SQL>
SELECT flights.*
FROM flights
WHERE (dest = 'IAH')
ORDER BY dep_delay
LIMIT 10

Three things to read off the SQL:

  • filter() becomes WHERE, and R’s == becomes SQL’s single =.
  • arrange() becomes ORDER BY; the direction defaults to ascending, and desc() maps to DESC.
  • dbplyr conservatively wraps the filter as a subquery (FROM (SELECT * FROM flights WHERE dest = 'IAH')), which is functionally equivalent to a flat query but makes the pipeline’s compositionality explicit.

Logical operators

Code
flights |>
    filter(dest == "IAH" & origin == "JFK") |>
    arrange(dep_delay) |>
    show_query()
<SQL>
SELECT flights.*
FROM flights
WHERE (dest = 'IAH' AND origin = 'JFK')
ORDER BY dep_delay
Code
flights |>
    filter(dest == "IAH" | origin == "JFK") |>
    arrange(year, month, day, desc(dep_delay)) |>
    show_query()
<SQL>
SELECT flights.*
FROM flights
WHERE (dest = 'IAH' OR origin = 'JFK')
ORDER BY "year", "month", "day", dep_delay DESC

& and | become AND and OR. The multi-column arrange() produces a comma-separated ORDER BY list with DESC attached per column.

Set membership and missing values

Code
flights |>
    filter(dest %in% c("IAH", "HOU")) |>
    show_query()
<SQL>
SELECT flights.*
FROM flights
WHERE (dest IN ('IAH', 'HOU'))
Code
flights |>
    filter(!is.na(dep_delay)) |>
    show_query()
<SQL>
SELECT flights.*
FROM flights
WHERE (NOT((dep_delay IS NULL)))

%in% maps to SQL’s IN (...), which is the standard way to express set membership. is.na() becomes IS NULL, and !is.na() becomes IS NOT NULL — note that SQL uses a different operator for null testing (IS) rather than equality (=), because null in SQL is the absence of a value, not a value equal to itself. This is the NULL ≠ NULL rule, and it’s the single most common source of subtle SQL bugs.

Select, rename, and mutate

Code
planes |>
    select(tailnum, type, manufacturer, model, year) |>
    show_query()
<SQL>
SELECT tailnum, "type", manufacturer, model, "year"
FROM planes
Code
planes |>
    select(tailnum, type, manufacturer, model, year) |>
    rename(year_built = year) |>
    show_query()
<SQL>
SELECT tailnum, "type", manufacturer, model, "year" AS year_built
FROM planes
Code
flights |>
    mutate(speed = distance / (air_time / 60)) |>
    show_query()
<SQL>
SELECT flights.*, distance / (air_time / 60.0) AS speed
FROM flights

Both rename() and mutate() produce the same underlying SQL structure: old_column AS new_name or expression AS new_column. Under the hood, renaming and computing a new variable are the same operation in SQL — the difference is only whether the right-hand side is a column reference or an expression.

Joins

Code
flights |>
    left_join(planes, by = "tailnum") |>
    show_query()
<SQL>
SELECT
  flights."year" AS "year.x",
  "month",
  "day",
  dep_time,
  sched_dep_time,
  dep_delay,
  arr_time,
  sched_arr_time,
  arr_delay,
  carrier,
  flight,
  flights.tailnum AS tailnum,
  origin,
  dest,
  air_time,
  distance,
  "hour",
  "minute",
  time_hour,
  planes."year" AS "year.y",

  model,
  engines,
  seats,
  speed,
  engine
FROM flights
LEFT JOIN planes
  ON (flights.tailnum = planes.tailnum)

The USING (tailnum) syntax is shorthand for ON flights.tailnum = planes.tailnum. If the join key had different names in the two tables, dbplyr would emit an explicit ON clause.

Aggregation — group_by() + summarize()

Code
flights |>
    group_by(origin) |>
    summarize(
        n_flights    = n(),
        mean_dep_del = mean(dep_delay, na.rm = TRUE),
        mean_arr_del = mean(arr_delay, na.rm = TRUE)
    ) |>
    show_query()
<SQL>
SELECT
  origin,
  COUNT(*) AS n_flights,
  AVG(dep_delay) AS mean_dep_del,
  AVG(arr_delay) AS mean_arr_del
FROM flights
GROUP BY origin

Two characteristics of SQL aggregation worth naming:

  • There is no explicit SUMMARIZE keyword. Aggregation is implicit — any column in SELECT that isn’t in GROUP BY must be inside an aggregate function (COUNT, SUM, AVG, MAX, MIN, etc.). Violating this produces the classic “column must appear in GROUP BY clause or be used in aggregate function” error.
  • Aggregate expressions get aliased with AS. The same expr AS name pattern from mutate() applies — the aggregate column needs a name to be referred to downstream, including in ORDER BY.

Computing aggregation on the remote table

Code
flights |>
    group_by(origin) |>
    summarize(
        n_flights    = n(),
        mean_dep_del = mean(dep_delay, na.rm = TRUE)
    ) |>
    arrange(desc(n_flights)) |>
    collect()   # pull results into R
# A tibble: 3 × 3
  origin n_flights mean_dep_del
  <chr>      <dbl>        <dbl>
1 EWR       120835         15.1
2 JFK       111279         12.1
3 LGA       104662         10.3

collect() is where the query actually executes and the result comes back as an R tibble. Everything before it was lazy — composing SQL, not running it. On a large database, this distinction is what makes dbplyr workable: you can iterate on the pipeline without pulling data until you’re ready.

Travel Stack Exchange — SQL Exercises

The following SQL queries target the Travel Stack Exchange Data Explorer (https://data.stackexchange.com/travel), which uses T-SQL (so TOP(n) works but the standard LIMIT n does not). They are not evaluated here — the Stack Exchange data lives remotely and the queries are demonstrated at the Data Explorer — but the SQL syntax is what you would paste there.

Exercise 1 — Posts with score ≥ 110

SELECT Title, Score
FROM   Posts
WHERE  PostTypeId = 1       -- restrict to questions
  AND  Score      >= 110
ORDER BY Score DESC;

No TOP() is needed because the result set is naturally small — the cutoff does the limiting. PostTypeId = 1 filters to questions (as opposed to answers, which have PostTypeId = 2).

Exercise 2 — Posts whose title mentions a place

SELECT Title, Score
FROM   Posts
WHERE  PostTypeId = 1
  AND  Title LIKE '%Rwanda%'
ORDER BY Score DESC;

LIKE with % wildcards is SQL’s substring match. %Rwanda% matches any title containing the substring “Rwanda” regardless of position. Note that LIKE is case-sensitive in some SQL dialects (PostgreSQL) and case-insensitive in others (T-SQL, MySQL default collation) — a genuine portability gotcha when moving queries across databases.

Exercise 3 — Posts with two co-occurring tags

SELECT TOP(50) Title, Score, Tags
FROM   Posts
WHERE  PostTypeId = 1
  AND  Tags LIKE '%<rwanda>%'
  AND  Tags LIKE '%<visas>%'
ORDER BY Score DESC;

The Stack Exchange schema stores tags as a single string field with <tag> delimiters (e.g., <rwanda><visas><east-africa>). To match a specific tag cleanly, use the angle-bracket form %<tag>% — otherwise a search for %visa% would also match posts tagged <visa-free> or <visa-on-arrival>. This is one of the places the Stack Exchange schema’s denormalization (multiple tags in one field instead of a separate Tags table) forces pattern-matching solutions that a properly normalized schema would avoid.

Exercise 4 — Count posts by type, ordered by frequency

SELECT
    PostTypeId,
    COUNT(Id) AS numPosts
FROM   Posts
GROUP BY PostTypeId
ORDER BY numPosts DESC;

The aggregate column alias numPosts is what makes it possible to ORDER BY numPosts — referring to the aggregate by the expression COUNT(Id) works in some dialects but not all. Naming it once and referring to it by name is the portable pattern.

Exercise 5 — Most common tagsets

SELECT TOP(20)
    Tags,
    COUNT(Tags) AS numTagsets
FROM   Posts
WHERE  PostTypeId = 1
GROUP BY Tags
ORDER BY numTagsets DESC;

This counts occurrences of exact tagset strings (<rwanda><visas> is different from <rwanda><visas><east-africa>) rather than individual tag frequencies. Individual-tag counts would require splitting the Tags field apart, which in T-SQL would involve STRING_SPLIT() or a recursive CTE — considerably more complex.

Exercise 6 — Join top comments with user info

SELECT TOP(100)
    c.Text,
    c.Score,
    u.DisplayName,
    u.Reputation
FROM   Comments c
JOIN   Users    u  ON c.UserId = u.Id
ORDER BY c.Score DESC;

Three pieces of syntax worth naming:

  • Comments c and Users u are table aliases — within this query, c refers to Comments and u to Users. Qualifying columns with c. and u. prevents ambiguity when both tables have a column of the same name.
  • JOIN without INNER, LEFT, RIGHT, or FULL defaults to INNER JOIN — only rows present in both tables are returned. Comments whose UserId doesn’t match a user (deleted accounts, for example) are dropped.
  • ON c.UserId = u.Id specifies the join predicate. The column names differ across tables here — UserId in Comments, Id in Users — so the ON form is required rather than the shorthand USING(col).

Closing the Connection

Code
dbDisconnect(con, shutdown = TRUE)

Always disconnect when finished. For in-memory DuckDB this is low-stakes, but the same pattern applies to networked databases where an unclosed connection can hold a connection-pool slot open and eventually exhaust the server.

Reflection

SQL and dplyr describe the same shape of operation with different syntax conventions. filter() + select() + arrange() + head() is WHERE + SELECT + ORDER BY + LIMIT. group_by() + summarize() is GROUP BY + aggregate-in-SELECT. *_join() is JOIN ... ON. The core conceptual vocabulary of data manipulation — select rows, pick columns, compute new values, group, aggregate, combine tables — is the same across both. What differs is where the work happens: dplyr materializes every intermediate step into an R object in memory; SQL composes a single query and ships it to the database, which optimizes execution across the whole plan before returning anything. For data that fits in RAM, this difference is cosmetic. For data that doesn’t, SQL is the only viable path.

show_query() is the best teacher I’ve found for this material. Rather than memorizing translation tables, the practical way to build SQL fluency is to write what you want in dplyr, see the SQL it produces, and adjust. After a few iterations, you internalize the mapping without consciously consulting it. This is the same pedagogical move as inspecting compiled code to learn the language underneath — in this case the language underneath is what every database in your future professional life speaks.

The NULL ≠ NULL rule is the single trap worth pinning. In SQL, NULL is the absence of a value, not a value that happens to equal other NULLs. Therefore WHERE x = NULL matches no rows, ever — you have to write WHERE x IS NULL. This is a semantic decision (null is “unknown,” and two unknowns cannot be asserted equal) that trips up almost every new SQL user. The R is.na() / !is.na() → SQL IS NULL / IS NOT NULL mapping handled by dbplyr hides this, but the moment you write raw SQL by hand, you meet the rule directly.

**Measurement uncertainty in the public version is flatter than in UNODC's own technical documents.** The price and purity indices anchoring the "perfect storm" argument come from seizure-based lab analysis, undercover purchases, and user self-report — each with systematic biases that the World Drug Report's methodology annexes discuss at length. The lab-analysis purity data, in particular, reflects only what was seized, and if interdiction preferentially captures certain product categories (bulk shipments vs. street-level inventory), purity trends partly index *sampling* rather than *market reality*. The public microsite presents the co-movement as self-evident; the organization's own technical documentation acknowledges it is partly interpretive. The register shift matters because the public version carries weight in policy debates where the caveats in the annexes are invisible.

**The opioid citation at the close is rhetorical rather than analytical.** The microsite is thematically about amphetamine-type stimulants and NPS; the mechanisms driving the opioid crisis (prescription-opioid-to-heroin-to-illicit-fentanyl transition in North America) are distinct from the mechanisms driving synthetic-stimulant expansion (precursor-control evasion, modular small-batch production). Appending "opioid-use-disorder deaths up 71%" to the executive-director quote bundles these under a unified urgency frame that the document's own analysis does not support. This is the genre convention of closing on an affect-heavy number, but it muddies the analytical case the rest of the piece built.

**Demand-side dynamics go un-interrogated.** The microsite reasons as if the demand curve has been static while supply has expanded. But synthetic-drug user demographics over the past decade have shifted substantially — from recreational male users to a more mixed population, from urban middle-class to rural working-class in several regions, from exclusively party contexts to functional and workplace use. A demand curve that has become more *elastic* because new price-sensitive cohorts have entered the market is consistent with the same observations (falling prices, rising quantities) without requiring the supply-side-only explanation the piece leans on. The analytical cost of not naming this is that readers infer supply interdiction failed when part of the story is that demand itself shifted.

**Geographic heterogeneity is flattened into "global."** Synthetic-drug markets are strongly regional in ways that matter for policy. Captagon's concentration in the Middle East (with well-documented Syrian state involvement in the production chain), methamphetamine's distinct markets in North America versus East/Southeast Asia versus Oceania, the ketamine supply chain's different geography — these are different political economies, not instances of one problem. The "1 in 200 of the global population" per-capita figure is the flattening stripped to a single number. UNODC has the regional data; the microsite chose not to surface it.

**Proportionality.** UNODC is a top-tier research institution and this is a public communications document, not its analytical output. The critiques above are about the translation from research to public communication — a hard editorial problem that all such institutions face. Within that constraint, the diagnostic framing is sharp and the NPS point is genuinely important. The next version of this document should close the diagnosis-prescription gap, import the technical-annex uncertainty language, and let geography and demand carry their own weight.

## Setting Up a Local Database with DuckDB

DuckDB is an in-process columnar database — fast, zero-configuration, and well-suited to interactive analysis. We create an in-memory database, write two nycflights13 tables to it, and then query them through dbplyr.

```{r}
#| label: db-setup
con <- DBI::dbConnect(duckdb::duckdb())

# Write datasets to the database
dbWriteTable(con, "flights", nycflights13::flights)
dbWriteTable(con, "planes",  nycflights13::planes)

# Confirm what lives in the database
dbListTables(con)
```

```{r}
#| label: db-lazy-tables
# `tbl()` returns a lazy table — no data is pulled into R until needed
flights <- tbl(con, "flights")
planes  <- tbl(con, "planes")

class(flights)
dim(flights)   # nrow is NA because the data hasn't been loaded
```

The `dim()` returning `NA` for rows is intentional. A remote table is a handle to something that lives in the database, not a materialized object — dbplyr translates R verbs into SQL, ships the SQL to the database, and only pulls the result back when asked with `collect()` or when a value is needed for printing.

## dplyr ↔ SQL Translation

### A concise mapping

| tidyverse verb | SQL clause | notes |
|---|---|---|
| `filter(col == x)` | `WHERE col = x` | single `=` for equality in SQL |
| `filter(a & b)` | `WHERE a AND b` | |
| `filter(a \| b)` | `WHERE a OR b` | |
| `filter(col %in% c(...))` | `WHERE col IN (...)` | |
| `filter(!is.na(col))` | `WHERE col IS NOT NULL` | |
| `select(a, b)` | `SELECT a, b FROM table` | |
| `rename(new = old)` | `SELECT old AS new` | |
| `mutate(new = expr)` | `SELECT ..., expr AS new` | |
| `arrange(col)` | `ORDER BY col` | ascending by default |
| `arrange(desc(col))` | `ORDER BY col DESC` | |
| `head(n)` | `LIMIT n` | `TOP(n)` in T-SQL |
| `group_by(col)` | `GROUP BY col` | |
| `summarize(x = sum(y))` | aggregate in `SELECT` | no explicit `summarize` keyword |
| `inner_join(...)` | `INNER JOIN ... ON ...` | |
| `left_join(...)` | `LEFT JOIN ... ON ...` | |

### Building the translation with `show_query()`

`show_query()` is the pedagogical key — it prints the SQL that dbplyr would send to the database for any pipeline. Seeing the translation side-by-side is the fastest way to internalize both.

```{r}
flights |>
    filter(dest == "IAH") |>
    arrange(dep_delay) |>
    show_query()
```

```{r}
flights |>
    filter(dest == "IAH") |>
    arrange(dep_delay) |>
    head(n = 10) |>
    show_query()
```

Three things to read off the SQL:

- `filter()` becomes `WHERE`, and R's `==` becomes SQL's single `=`.
- `arrange()` becomes `ORDER BY`; the direction defaults to ascending, and `desc()` maps to `DESC`.
- dbplyr conservatively wraps the filter as a subquery (`FROM (SELECT * FROM flights WHERE dest = 'IAH')`), which is functionally equivalent to a flat query but makes the pipeline's compositionality explicit.

### Logical operators

```{r}
flights |>
    filter(dest == "IAH" & origin == "JFK") |>
    arrange(dep_delay) |>
    show_query()
```

```{r}
flights |>
    filter(dest == "IAH" | origin == "JFK") |>
    arrange(year, month, day, desc(dep_delay)) |>
    show_query()
```

`&` and `|` become `AND` and `OR`. The multi-column `arrange()` produces a comma-separated `ORDER BY` list with `DESC` attached per column.

### Set membership and missing values

```{r}
flights |>
    filter(dest %in% c("IAH", "HOU")) |>
    show_query()
```

```{r}
flights |>
    filter(!is.na(dep_delay)) |>
    show_query()
```

`%in%` maps to SQL's `IN (...)`, which is the standard way to express set membership. `is.na()` becomes `IS NULL`, and `!is.na()` becomes `IS NOT NULL` — note that SQL uses a different operator for null testing (`IS`) rather than equality (`=`), because null in SQL is the absence of a value, not a value equal to itself. This is the `NULL ≠ NULL` rule, and it's the single most common source of subtle SQL bugs.

### Select, rename, and mutate

```{r}
planes |>
    select(tailnum, type, manufacturer, model, year) |>
    show_query()
```

```{r}
planes |>
    select(tailnum, type, manufacturer, model, year) |>
    rename(year_built = year) |>
    show_query()
```

```{r}
flights |>
    mutate(speed = distance / (air_time / 60)) |>
    show_query()
```

Both `rename()` and `mutate()` produce the same underlying SQL structure: `old_column AS new_name` or `expression AS new_column`. Under the hood, renaming and computing a new variable are the same operation in SQL — the difference is only whether the right-hand side is a column reference or an expression.

### Joins

```{r}
flights |>
    left_join(planes, by = "tailnum") |>
    show_query()
```

The `USING (tailnum)` syntax is shorthand for `ON flights.tailnum = planes.tailnum`. If the join key had different names in the two tables, dbplyr would emit an explicit `ON` clause.

### Aggregation — `group_by()` + `summarize()`

```{r}
flights |>
    group_by(origin) |>
    summarize(
        n_flights    = n(),
        mean_dep_del = mean(dep_delay, na.rm = TRUE),
        mean_arr_del = mean(arr_delay, na.rm = TRUE)
    ) |>
    show_query()
```

Two characteristics of SQL aggregation worth naming:

- **There is no explicit `SUMMARIZE` keyword.** Aggregation is implicit — any column in `SELECT` that isn't in `GROUP BY` *must* be inside an aggregate function (`COUNT`, `SUM`, `AVG`, `MAX`, `MIN`, etc.). Violating this produces the classic "column must appear in GROUP BY clause or be used in aggregate function" error.
- **Aggregate expressions get aliased with `AS`.** The same `expr AS name` pattern from `mutate()` applies — the aggregate column needs a name to be referred to downstream, including in `ORDER BY`.

### Computing aggregation on the remote table

```{r}
flights |>
    group_by(origin) |>
    summarize(
        n_flights    = n(),
        mean_dep_del = mean(dep_delay, na.rm = TRUE)
    ) |>
    arrange(desc(n_flights)) |>
    collect()   # pull results into R
```

`collect()` is where the query actually executes and the result comes back as an R tibble. Everything before it was lazy — composing SQL, not running it. On a large database, this distinction is what makes dbplyr workable: you can iterate on the pipeline without pulling data until you're ready.

## Travel Stack Exchange — SQL Exercises

The following SQL queries target the Travel Stack Exchange Data Explorer (<https://data.stackexchange.com/travel>), which uses T-SQL (so `TOP(n)` works but the standard `LIMIT n` does not). They are not evaluated here — the Stack Exchange data lives remotely and the queries are demonstrated at the Data Explorer — but the SQL syntax is what you would paste there.

### Exercise 1 — Posts with score ≥ 110

```sql
SELECT Title, Score
FROM   Posts
WHERE  PostTypeId = 1       -- restrict to questions
  AND  Score      >= 110
ORDER BY Score DESC;
```

No `TOP()` is needed because the result set is naturally small — the cutoff does the limiting. `PostTypeId = 1` filters to questions (as opposed to answers, which have `PostTypeId = 2`).

### Exercise 2 — Posts whose title mentions a place

```sql
SELECT Title, Score
FROM   Posts
WHERE  PostTypeId = 1
  AND  Title LIKE '%Rwanda%'
ORDER BY Score DESC;
```

`LIKE` with `%` wildcards is SQL's substring match. `%Rwanda%` matches any title containing the substring "Rwanda" regardless of position. Note that `LIKE` is case-sensitive in some SQL dialects (PostgreSQL) and case-insensitive in others (T-SQL, MySQL default collation) — a genuine portability gotcha when moving queries across databases.

### Exercise 3 — Posts with two co-occurring tags

```sql
SELECT TOP(50) Title, Score, Tags
FROM   Posts
WHERE  PostTypeId = 1
  AND  Tags LIKE '%<rwanda>%'
  AND  Tags LIKE '%<visas>%'
ORDER BY Score DESC;
```

The Stack Exchange schema stores tags as a single string field with `<tag>` delimiters (e.g., `<rwanda><visas><east-africa>`). To match a specific tag cleanly, use the angle-bracket form `%<tag>%` — otherwise a search for `%visa%` would also match posts tagged `<visa-free>` or `<visa-on-arrival>`. This is one of the places the Stack Exchange schema's denormalization (multiple tags in one field instead of a separate Tags table) forces pattern-matching solutions that a properly normalized schema would avoid.

### Exercise 4 — Count posts by type, ordered by frequency

```sql
SELECT
    PostTypeId,
    COUNT(Id) AS numPosts
FROM   Posts
GROUP BY PostTypeId
ORDER BY numPosts DESC;
```

The aggregate column alias `numPosts` is what makes it possible to `ORDER BY numPosts` — referring to the aggregate by the expression `COUNT(Id)` works in some dialects but not all. Naming it once and referring to it by name is the portable pattern.

### Exercise 5 — Most common tagsets

```sql
SELECT TOP(20)
    Tags,
    COUNT(Tags) AS numTagsets
FROM   Posts
WHERE  PostTypeId = 1
GROUP BY Tags
ORDER BY numTagsets DESC;
```

This counts occurrences of exact tagset strings (`<rwanda><visas>` is different from `<rwanda><visas><east-africa>`) rather than individual tag frequencies. Individual-tag counts would require splitting the `Tags` field apart, which in T-SQL would involve `STRING_SPLIT()` or a recursive CTE — considerably more complex.

### Exercise 6 — Join top comments with user info

```sql
SELECT TOP(100)
    c.Text,
    c.Score,
    u.DisplayName,
    u.Reputation
FROM   Comments c
JOIN   Users    u  ON c.UserId = u.Id
ORDER BY c.Score DESC;
```

Three pieces of syntax worth naming:

- `Comments c` and `Users u` are *table aliases* — within this query, `c` refers to `Comments` and `u` to `Users`. Qualifying columns with `c.` and `u.` prevents ambiguity when both tables have a column of the same name.
- `JOIN` without `INNER`, `LEFT`, `RIGHT`, or `FULL` defaults to `INNER JOIN` — only rows present in both tables are returned. Comments whose `UserId` doesn't match a user (deleted accounts, for example) are dropped.
- `ON c.UserId = u.Id` specifies the join predicate. The column names differ across tables here — `UserId` in `Comments`, `Id` in `Users` — so the `ON` form is required rather than the shorthand `USING(col)`.

## Closing the Connection

```{r}
dbDisconnect(con, shutdown = TRUE)
```

Always disconnect when finished. For in-memory DuckDB this is low-stakes, but the same pattern applies to networked databases where an unclosed connection can hold a connection-pool slot open and eventually exhaust the server.

## Reflection

**SQL and dplyr describe the same shape of operation with different syntax conventions.** `filter() + select() + arrange() + head()` is `WHERE + SELECT + ORDER BY + LIMIT`. `group_by() + summarize()` is `GROUP BY + aggregate-in-SELECT`. `*_join()` is `JOIN ... ON`. The core conceptual vocabulary of data manipulation — select rows, pick columns, compute new values, group, aggregate, combine tables — is the same across both. What differs is where the work happens: dplyr materializes every intermediate step into an R object in memory; SQL composes a single query and ships it to the database, which optimizes execution across the whole plan before returning anything. For data that fits in RAM, this difference is cosmetic. For data that doesn't, SQL is the only viable path.

**`show_query()` is the best teacher I've found for this material.** Rather than memorizing translation tables, the practical way to build SQL fluency is to write what you want in dplyr, see the SQL it produces, and adjust. After a few iterations, you internalize the mapping without consciously consulting it. This is the same pedagogical move as inspecting compiled code to learn the language underneath — in this case the language underneath is what every database in your future professional life speaks.

**The `NULL ≠ NULL` rule is the single trap worth pinning.** In SQL, `NULL` is the absence of a value, not a value that happens to equal other `NULL`s. Therefore `WHERE x = NULL` matches no rows, ever — you have to write `WHERE x IS NULL`. This is a semantic decision (null is "unknown," and two unknowns cannot be asserted equal) that trips up almost every new SQL user. The R `is.na()` / `!is.na()` → SQL `IS NULL` / `IS NOT NULL` mapping handled by dbplyr hides this, but the moment you write raw SQL by hand, you meet the rule directly.
