🔥 Data Story Critique — What Africa will look like in 100 years (The Telegraph, March 2016)

Source: http://s.telegraph.co.uk/graphics/projects/Africa-in-100-years/index.html

Author: Ashley Kirk, Telegraph data journalist.

What is the data story?

The piece takes UN population projections — Africa reaching 4.4 billion by 2100, a roughly fourfold increase — and builds a continent-scale prognostic narrative around the challenges and opportunities of that growth. Chapters move through urbanization (projecting 56.5% urban-dweller share by 2050), housing and slums (with a profile of Lydia, a young woman from Nairobi’s Kibera), public health (HIV, malaria, maternal mortality), conflict (Boko Haram, South Sudan, the Fragile States Index), education (primary-school enrollment, illiteracy projections), and life expectancy. Each chapter interleaves data charts with expert quotes — primarily from UN agencies (Joan Clos, Dr Davison Munodawafa), UK government bodies (Justine Greening, DFID figures), and Western-linked institutions (Barclays Africa, Africa Educational Trust, The Borgen Project). The piece closes on a qualified note of opportunity: African urbanization is a late-starting transition that could embrace “new urban paradigms,” provided it is accompanied by international support and better data.

What is effective?

The underlying demographic data is solid and the projection is important to communicate. UN population projections showing Africa’s quadrupling by 2100 are a consequential fact that most readers have not internalized. Getting this number into broadsheet readership with the accompanying structural implications (working-age cohort composition, urbanization pace, health-system strain) is a legitimate journalistic contribution.

Interleaving a named first-person testimony — Lydia from Kibera — gives the abstract demographic projection a texture that pure charts cannot. Whatever the problems with how her voice is positioned (see below), having an African woman speak in her own words at substantial length is above the norm for this genre and register.

Naming specific success cases alongside the challenges. Burundi’s doubling of primary-school enrollment between 2000 and 2010 and Mozambique’s 35-percentage-point gain in the same window are given explicit space. This is not a piece that argues every African country is on the same trajectory; it at least gestures at heterogeneity in outcomes.

The closing framing borrows from demographic-dividend thinking. “Given the comparatively late onset of the urban transition in Africa, there exist real opportunities for embracing new urban paradigms” is drawn from the scholarly literature that treats Africa’s youth-heavy demographic profile as an asset if paired with right policy. That framing appears only at the end and is not developed, but its presence at all is worth noting.

What could be improved?

The piece operates in an external-observation register that flattens Africa’s analytical autonomy. The entire document reads as the Telegraph looking at Africa and reporting on its future — not as Africans authoring their own trajectory. No comparable Telegraph feature would ask “What will America look like in 100 years?” in the same anxious-prognostic register, despite America’s demographic shifts being similarly substantial. The genre convention this piece sits inside — broadsheet European development journalism — has a long history going back to colonial-era reportage, and the piece does not distance itself from that inheritance. A reader learns what the world should worry about regarding Africa, not what Africans are doing about Africa.

“Africa” is treated as a single analytical object when the data does not support aggregation at that level. The continent contains 54 states with radically different state capacities, resource profiles, governance configurations, colonial-inheritance structures, and demographic trajectories. Rwanda and DRC are not one story. Botswana and Zimbabwe are not one story. Tunisia and Sudan are not one story. The piece moves between “Africa” generalizations (“Africa’s poor health is linked to its people’s poverty”) and country-specific data points as if the two scales are interchangeable — this is the same analytical error as treating “Europe” as a single object when discussing Greek debt. The charting compounds this: “Continent populations” plots Africa as a single line against Asia, Europe, LAC, and North America, baking the aggregation into the visual grammar.

Causal framing is absent where it is most needed. Poverty, conflict, low education, weak health systems are presented as conditions of the continent, not as outcomes of identifiable historical and structural processes. The piece does not name colonial boundary-drawing (every African state border was externally imposed within two lifetimes), structural adjustment programs of the 1980s–90s that systematically defunded African health and education under IMF/World Bank conditionalities, extractive trade arrangements that still favor raw-commodity exports, or capital flight — the African Development Bank and others have estimated net illicit financial outflows from Africa at magnitudes exceeding inward aid. By presenting effects without causes, the piece frames African poverty as something to be alleviated by intervention rather than as something produced by interaction with the global economic order. This is the single most serious framing problem in the piece.

The epistemic hierarchy between who gets to analyze and who gets to illustrate is stark. Analytical authority speaking about Africa in the piece: UN Under-Secretary-General Joan Clos, the UK’s International Development Minister Justine Greening, the UK’s Department for International Development, a World Bank lead economist, the CEO of Barclays Africa, the executive director of the Africa Educational Trust (UK-based), the Borgen Project (US-based). The Kenyan voices quoted are Lydia (Kibera resident, positioned as experiential) and Dr Munodawafa (WHO programme manager, speaking within the international-institutional frame). No African economist, African demographer, African political scientist, or African development scholar is quoted as an analyst of Africa’s future. This is a systematic editorial pattern. The Telegraph has the resources to reach African scholars. It didn’t.

Lydia’s voice is selected to confirm the narrative, not to challenge it. Her childhood memory — “I would make dolls from thread and plastic bags” — is the conventional slum-poverty aesthetic, poverty-as-innocence in visual grammar. When she offers analysis, it aligns with Western development-NGO orthodoxy: skills training, self-employment. What’s absent is any critique by her of the structural conditions — land tenure in Nairobi, urban-planning decisions that created Kibera’s informal status, Kenyan state response to slum dwellers — that a resident of Kibera could offer and that would contradict the piece’s frame of “opportunity combined with right government intervention.” A meaningful inclusion of a slum resident’s voice would let her analyze, not just feel.

The “data-collection flaws in Africa” disclaimer papers over a deeper epistemological problem. The piece notes that “there are flaws in data collection and publication processes in Africa” — framing African data infrastructure as deficient — and then proceeds to trust aggregate figures to drive the narrative. What it doesn’t name is that much of the data about Africa is produced by non-African institutions (UN, World Bank, IMF, Western-based indices like the Fragile States Index from the Fund for Peace) whose methodological choices and political-economic interests shape what gets measured and how. “African data” in this piece is often “data about Africa produced by people who are not African,” and the gatekeeping that entails is invisible.

Visual grammar is dominated by crisis imagery. The photographs in the piece are dominated by Ebola signage, rubbish offloading, military patrols, armed security. A piece that wanted to center the “opportunity” framing would have chosen images that aren’t exclusively drawn from the crisis aesthetic — images of African urban innovation, African scholars, African science, African economic production. The ratio of crisis imagery to opportunity imagery tells the reader what the piece actually thinks Africa is.

Proportionality. This is a serious broadsheet feature by a competent data journalist using real UN data. The problems above are not technical deficits but framing choices, and the framing is specifically the inherited British-development-journalism frame that Africa-studies scholars have critiqued for decades. Naming these problems is fair because this piece is positioned as authoritative public communication, not as student work. A 2026 version of this piece — with African analytical voices centered, causal framing acknowledged, the aggregation of “Africa” disciplined — would be a meaningfully different document.

Exercise — Human-Centered Data Science

What the Dear Abby letters reveal that no dataset would

The Pudding’s 30 Years of American Anxieties works with the text of letters to an advice column — and the letters themselves do analytical work that a tabular dataset cannot do. Five kinds of context that are present in letters and absent from aggregated survey data:

Temporal-emotional register. A letter written at 2 a.m. after a fight with a spouse has a different tone and content than the same person’s answer to a survey question about “marital satisfaction” six weeks later in a calmer moment. The letter captures the state; the survey captures the summary. Aggregated data cannot reconstruct the states that produced it.

Relational and circumstantial context. Letters embed their subject matter in explanation — “I have been married forty years, have eight children, am sixty and feel eighty-five” (from a 1985 letter in the dataset). This is the full scaffold of a life situation. A survey variable “age = 60” flattens it entirely.

Social norms that govern what people can say aloud. What letter-writers worry about, what they assume the advice columnist and her public audience will find reasonable, and what they frame as shameful or forbidden all reveal the prevailing moral framework of the decade more precisely than any explicit measure of “values.” The letters document norms by performing within them.

The selection and gatekeeping of what becomes data. Not all letters are published. Editorial choices by the columnist and newspaper determine which anxieties become visible and which stay private. The dataset The Pudding worked with is already a filtered artefact — the worries of people who wrote in, as curated by editors for public readership.

Language itself as context. Word choice, formality, structure, length — the same underlying problem articulated in 1985 uses different vocabulary, different euphemisms, different appeals to authority than the same problem articulated in 2017. Computational text analysis can surface these shifts, but only because the texts carry them; a numeric survey could not.

The questions below are scaffolded for reflection on my course project dataset; I complete them in context of the project write-up.

  • Who collected this data and why? [fill in: the producing institution, the institutional purpose that motivated the collection, the audience the data was produced for]
  • What might have been the collectors’ agenda, and how might that have shaped what is in the data? [fill in: what interests the producer had in measuring certain outcomes and not others]
  • What cases are present and absent? [fill in: eligibility criteria, sampling frame, non-response, censoring, selection-into-observation dynamics]
  • What variables are available, and in what format? [fill in: what gets binned into categories, which categories are treated as ordered/unordered, which continuous quantities are recorded at what precision]
  • Whose labor is visible in the data, and whose is invisible? [fill in: the visible producers — named authors, named agencies — and the invisible producers — enumerators, data entry workers, domain-expert respondents, households providing unpaid information]
  • What additional information would give a more full picture of the lives connected to this data? [fill in: qualitative interviews, administrative texture, historical records, first-person testimony, community-held knowledge]

The move this exercise is asking for — treating the dataset as a produced artefact rather than as a natural object — is the move that separates descriptive data work from accountable data work. A dataset is not the thing itself; it is a specific institutional choice about what counted as worth recording.

Exercise — Project in 3 Visuals

If the digital deliverable could only show three visuals, the decision rule is: what three views together answer the research question with the least redundancy and the most interpretive coverage? The framework below is what I use to choose.

Candidate roles for the three visuals

Visual 1 — Context / Scale. Establishes the problem. Answers: what is the magnitude of the phenomenon? how does it compare across time, place, or groups? This visual earns its space by making the reader care before any analysis happens.

Visual 2 — Structure / Mechanism. Shows the internal shape of the phenomenon. Answers: what is the pattern behind the headline number? what is the distribution, the relationship between key variables, the within-group variation? This is where the analytical work lives.

Visual 3 — Implication / Comparison. Drives toward the project’s argument. Answers: what does this mean? what is the comparison that carries the interpretive payoff? what should the reader take away? This visual should be the one the reader remembers.

For my project

The candidate list, ordering, and combination decisions below are scaffolded; I complete them with project specifics.

  • Visual 1 (Context): [fill in: the chart type and data behind it — e.g., a time series showing the trend, a map showing the geographic distribution, a bar chart showing comparative scale]
  • Visual 2 (Structure): [fill in]
  • Visual 3 (Implication): [fill in]

Ordering. The default sequence is Context → Structure → Implication, which moves the reader from “what are we looking at” through “what is happening inside it” to “what should we conclude.” Sometimes inverting — opening on the implication and then building back — works if the implication is counterintuitive enough to function as a hook.

Combining. Two visuals can sometimes fuse into one without losing analytical content: a small-multiples panel where each facet answers a different question, a dual-axis chart that pairs magnitude with rate, a map with an inset time series. The test for whether combining works: does the combined figure still have one clear reading order, or does it fragment the reader’s attention?

Reflection

Effective visualization is a chain from question → data → decisions → audience. Every one of the six guidelines — data density, clear labels, useful references, highlights, small multiples, meaningful order — is really a question about the audience’s cognition: what will they pay attention to, what do they already know, what comparison do they need to perform, what would make that comparison easier? When any visualization feels wrong, the diagnosis almost always traces back to a mismatch at one of these links. The chart has insufficient context because the decision about what the audience needs was unexamined; the highlight is buried because the designer hadn’t decided which comparison was load-bearing.

Human-centered data science is what stops the chain from running on autopilot. The Dear Abby letters exercise is a compact version of the broader discipline: treat the data as a produced artefact, ask who produced it and why, ask whose labor is invisible, ask what additional context a complete picture would require. A data scientist who skips these questions can still make technically competent visualizations — but those visualizations will carry forward whatever biases, omissions, and silences were already embedded in the data. The technical discipline and the human-centered discipline are not separate skills; the first is an application of the second.

The Africa-in-100-years critique earlier in this document is, at the end of the day, a failure of human-centered practice. The piece used real UN data and produced charts that are technically fine. What it did not do is ask the human-centered questions: whose analysis is this? who gets to speak about this? what causal framing have I inherited from my editorial tradition, and is it the right one? The consequence is a piece that describes Africa to an external audience rather than letting Africa’s people and scholars author their own representation. Every chart in that piece could be re-plotted with the same data and produce an entirely different document, once the human-centered questions were answered differently.

This is the full scaffold of a life situation. A survey variable "age = 60" flattens it entirely.

**Social norms that govern what people can say aloud.** What letter-writers worry about, what they assume the advice columnist and her public audience will find reasonable, and what they frame as shameful or forbidden all reveal the prevailing moral framework of the decade more precisely than any explicit measure of "values." The letters document norms by *performing within them*.

**The selection and gatekeeping of what becomes data.** Not all letters are published. Editorial choices by the columnist and newspaper determine which anxieties become visible and which stay private. The dataset The Pudding worked with is already a filtered artefact — the worries of people who wrote in, as curated by editors for public readership.

**Language itself as context.** Word choice, formality, structure, length — the same underlying problem articulated in 1985 uses different vocabulary, different euphemisms, different appeals to authority than the same problem articulated in 2017. Computational text analysis can surface these shifts, but only because the texts carry them; a numeric survey could not.

## Exercise — Project in 3 Visuals

If the digital deliverable could only show three visuals, the decision rule is: *what three views together answer the research question with the least redundancy and the most interpretive coverage?* The framework below is what I use to choose.

### Candidate roles for the three visuals

**Visual 1 — Context / Scale.** Establishes the problem. Answers: *what is the magnitude of the phenomenon? how does it compare across time, place, or groups?* This visual earns its space by making the reader care before any analysis happens.

**Visual 2 — Structure / Mechanism.** Shows the internal shape of the phenomenon. Answers: *what is the pattern behind the headline number? what is the distribution, the relationship between key variables, the within-group variation?* This is where the analytical work lives.

**Visual 3 — Implication / Comparison.** Drives toward the project's argument. Answers: *what does this mean? what is the comparison that carries the interpretive payoff? what should the reader take away?* This visual should be the one the reader remembers.

## Reflection

**Effective visualization is a chain from question → data → decisions → audience.** Every one of the six guidelines — data density, clear labels, useful references, highlights, small multiples, meaningful order — is really a question about the audience's cognition: *what will they pay attention to, what do they already know, what comparison do they need to perform, what would make that comparison easier?* When any visualization feels wrong, the diagnosis almost always traces back to a mismatch at one of these links. The chart has insufficient context because the decision about what the audience needs was unexamined; the highlight is buried because the designer hadn't decided which comparison was load-bearing.

**Human-centered data science is what stops the chain from running on autopilot.** The Dear Abby letters exercise is a compact version of the broader discipline: *treat the data as a produced artefact, ask who produced it and why, ask whose labor is invisible, ask what additional context a complete picture would require.* A data scientist who skips these questions can still make technically competent visualizations — but those visualizations will carry forward whatever biases, omissions, and silences were already embedded in the data. The technical discipline and the human-centered discipline are not separate skills; the first is an application of the second.

**The Africa-in-100-years critique earlier in this document is, at the end of the day, a failure of human-centered practice.** The piece used real UN data and produced charts that are technically fine. What it did not do is ask the human-centered questions: *whose analysis is this? who gets to speak about this? what causal framing have I inherited from my editorial tradition, and is it the right one?* The consequence is a piece that describes Africa to an external audience rather than letting Africa's people and scholars author their own representation. Every chart in that piece could be re-plotted with the same data and produce an entirely different document, once the human-centered questions were answered differently.
