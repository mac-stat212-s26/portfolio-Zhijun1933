🔥 Data Story Critique — How many high school stars make it in the NBA? (The Pudding, Feb 2019)

Source: https://pudding.cool/2019/03/hype/

Authors: Russell Samora & Amber Thomas.

What is the data story?

The piece asks whether high-school basketball recruiting rankings predict NBA success, using the Recruiting Services Consensus Index (RSCI) top-100 lists from 1998 to 2013 as the cohort and following those 1,563 players through a funnel of outcomes: college, draft, two-year-rookie-contract survival, and four performance tiers — below average, mediocre, great, superstar — defined by the mean of each player’s top-five-season ranking on combined VORP and Estimated Wins Added metrics. The visual spine is an animated circle-transition where each player is a dot that moves across stages, letting the reader watch the cohort thin out at each filter. The story unfolds through progressive refinement: top-100 overall (27% make the NBA, ~2% become superstars), then top-10 (84% make the NBA), then the 1998–2005 straight-to-NBA cohort (94% past rookie contract despite skipping college), then the 338 drafted but HS-unranked players (whose superstar list includes Wade, Curry, Lillard, Butler, Westbrook, Marc Gasol, and Paul George), and finally the Big-4 college pipeline (Kentucky, Kansas, Duke, UNC) plus a table of the top NBA-producing programs.

What is effective?

The animated funnel is the right form for the question. The quantity the piece is communicating is attrition across stages — how many of 1,563 players drop out at each filter, and what shape the surviving population takes. Static bar charts could show the endpoint but not the motion; sankey diagrams could show the flow but not the scale; the dot-transition form preserves both, and the “Replay” affordance acknowledges that the animation is the point.

Named individuals anchor the statistics. Donnell Harvey vs. LeBron James in the opening paragraph; Korleone Young as the cautionary case for removing the age limit; the seven unranked-but-superstar names. These are the specific instances that make an abstract rate (“27% reach the NBA”) legible — without them, the piece would be a series of percentages the reader would forget by the next section.

Methodology transparency is unusual for popular visual journalism. The bottom note specifies the tier thresholds (Superstar = top 30 by VORP + EWA, Great = top 60, Mediocre = top 120, Below Average = 120+), the time window (1998–2013), sample sizes per view, and the data sources (RSCI, Basketball Reference). Most pieces in this genre hide these decisions; The Pudding shows them, and that’s part of why the argument holds up under scrutiny.

The structure of progressive refinement is pedagogically sound. Each “but what about…” view — top 100 → top 10 → straight-to-NBA → unranked-but-drafted → Big 4 — reframes the question more precisely. By the end the reader has a multi-view answer (“HS rank predicts better than chance but there’s a long tail of underranked superstars”) rather than a single headline statistic. This is closer to how an empirical question actually gets answered than most popular treatments manage.

What could be improved?

The framing measures within-pipeline prediction, not talent identification. The piece asks whether RSCI rank predicts NBA outcome among players who entered the top-100 HS list. That is a narrower question than the title implies. “Do HS rankings identify basketball talent?” would require the denominator to include players who never made the top-100 — all US high-school basketball players, international players, junior-college players — and to ask how well the ranking system would have done against that population. The “unranked but drafted” section partially addresses this, but it still conditions on having been drafted, which is itself a selected outcome. The reader is implicitly invited to conclude that HS rank is a reasonable predictor of NBA success; the tighter conclusion the data actually supports is that HS rank is a reasonable predictor conditional on the player already being in the scouting pipeline.

RSCI is a constructed instrument whose evaluation heuristics partly overlap with NBA draft heuristics. The 84% top-10-to-NBA rate looks like strong predictive validity, but it’s confounded by selection: high HS rank gets a player better college offers, which means better NCAA exposure, which means higher draft position, which means more NBA opportunity. Some of what the funnel is measuring is pipeline access (ranked players are placed into environments that produce NBA players) rather than latent talent (ranked players are fundamentally better). Disentangling these would require something like comparing ranked vs. unranked players within the same college program, or looking at late-rising players who weren’t ranked in HS but performed at comparable college levels. The piece doesn’t make this distinction, and without it, “HS ranking works” conflates prediction with construction.

The 2010–2013 cohort is right-censored. Players drafted in 2013 had six years of NBA careers by the time of publication (February 2019). The “superstar” tier is defined by the mean of a player’s top-five seasons — but a player whose best seasons will be years 5–9 of their career (Giannis Antetokounmpo was drafted in 2013 and didn’t reach MVP level until 2018–19) hasn’t had time to register as a superstar in this dataset. Later cohorts are therefore systematically undercounted at the top tiers while earlier cohorts have fully resolved. The piece treats the data as complete. A cleaner version would either restrict the cohort to players with at least N seasons of career, or visibly flag the censoring.

Superstar count is small enough that the specific numbers have substantial uncertainty. With 31 superstars across 1,563 players (~2%), the 95% confidence interval on the rate is roughly 1.4%–2.8% by normal approximation. This matters because the piece reports rates with two significant digits (“1%”, “2%”, “<1%”) as if they were stable quantities. For tier proportions derived from small integer counts, visualizing or at least mentioning the uncertainty would keep the reader calibrated about what the funnel is and isn’t pinning down.

The animation-only visual carries accessibility costs. Dot-transition visualizations are hostile to screen-reader users, keyboard-only navigation, and users with vestibular disorders or motion sensitivity. The “Replay” button shows the designers knew the animation was load-bearing, but there is no “skip / show final state” option — the reader has to sit through the motion to reach the summary. A parallel static-summary view (final positions, counts, percentages in a text table) would add minutes of work for the designers and open the piece to a meaningfully wider audience.

The NCAA labor question is gestured at, not engaged. The piece notes “the college funnel is an interesting discussion, especially when it surrounds the lack of pay for NCAA student athletes” — but then pivots to the table of top NBA-producing programs without developing that thread. The data The Pudding collected is directly relevant: Kentucky and Duke convert high HS rankings into NBA pipelines at rates far above other schools, and players whose HS rankings attracted them to those programs generated substantial revenue without compensation during this study window. That is an argumentative opportunity, and the piece walks past it. This is an editorial choice rather than a failure, but worth naming as the choice it is.

Proportionality. This is among the best sports data journalism of its year — the methodology is transparent, the question is genuinely well-posed, the visual form fits the quantity being communicated, and the named-individual anchoring works. The critiques above are directed at the piece’s second-order structure (framing, censoring, selection), not at its execution.

Census API — Building a URL with httr2

The Census API allows small queries without a key. Large production use requires a (free) key from https://api.census.gov/data/key_signup.html; store it locally in census_api_key.txt and load with readLines().

Code
census_api_key <- if (file.exists("census_api_key.txt")) {
    readLines("census_api_key.txt")
} else {
    ""
}

Building the request

Code
req <- request("https://api.census.gov") |>
    req_url_path_append("data") |>
    req_url_path_append("2019") |>
    req_url_path_append("acs") |>
    req_url_path_append("acs1") |>
    req_url_query(
        get    = c("NAME", "B02015_009E", "B02015_009M"),
        `for`  = I("state:*"),
        key    = census_api_key,
        .multi = "comma"
    )

req

Three craftsmanship points worth naming:

  • I() around "state:*". Without the “as-is” wrapper, httr2 would percent-encode the : and * into %3A and %2A, which the Census API would then fail to interpret. I() tells httr2 to pass the value through literally. The same pattern applies any time a query value contains characters that look special but are meaningful to the target API.
  • `for`. Backticks are required because for is a reserved word in R (the for-loop keyword). Any query-parameter name that collides with an R reserved word, or that contains spaces/dashes/dots, needs backtick escaping.
  • .multi = "comma". The Census API expects comma-separated lists for multi-valued parameters (get=NAME,B02015_009E,B02015_009M). Other APIs use different conventions — some repeat the key (?tag=red&tag=blue), some use brackets (?tag[]=red&tag[]=blue). .multi is where you configure this per API.

Performing the request and parsing the response

Code
resp <- req_perform(req)

resp_json <- resp |> resp_body_json(simplifyVector = TRUE)

BoardGameGeek — XML and xml2

BGG returns XML (not JSON). The request pattern is identical to the Census example, but parsing uses xml2 instead of jsonlite.

Note on reproducibility. The BoardGameGeek XML API imposes aggressive rate limits and regularly returns 401/429 on automated requests during a book render. To keep this portfolio rendering cleanly regardless of BGG’s mood on any given day, the live call is guarded with two layers: req_error(is_error = \(resp) FALSE) turns HTTP 4xx/5xx responses from exceptions into regular return values so the status code can be inspected, and an outer tryCatch() catches network-layer failures (DNS, timeout) that still throw. Either path falls back to a small cached XML sample, so the downstream parsing code runs identically whether the live call succeeds or not. The same req_error() + tryCatch() + cached-fallback pattern is used for the NYT section below.

Code
bgg_fallback_xml <- '<items total="3" termsofuse="https://boardgamegeek.com/xmlapi/termsofuse">
  <item type="boardgame" id="172216">
    <name type="primary" value="Mysterium"/>
    <yearpublished value="2015"/>
  </item>
  <item type="boardgame" id="238402">
    <name type="primary" value="Forgotten Waters"/>
    <yearpublished value="2020"/>
  </item>
  <item type="boardgame" id="152208">
    <name type="primary" value="Mystery of the Abbey"/>
  </item>
</items>'

req_bgg <- request("https://boardgamegeek.com/xmlapi2") |>
    req_url_path_append("search") |>
    req_url_query(
        query = I("mystery+curse"),
        type  = I("boardgame,boardgameaccessory,boardgameexpansion")
    ) |>
    req_error(is_error = \(resp) FALSE)  # treat 4xx/5xx as values, not errors

resp_bgg <- tryCatch(
    {
        resp_raw <- req_perform(req_bgg)
        if (resp_status(resp_raw) >= 400) {
            message("BGG returned HTTP ", resp_status(resp_raw),
                    "; using cached sample.")
            read_xml(bgg_fallback_xml)
        } else {
            resp_body_xml(resp_raw)
        }
    },
    error = function(e) {
        # Only reaches here for network-layer errors (DNS, timeout, etc.)
        message("BGG request failed (", conditionMessage(e),
                "); using cached sample.")
        read_xml(bgg_fallback_xml)
    }
)

Exploring the XML structure

Code
xml_structure(resp_bgg)
<items [total, termsofuse]>
  <item [type, id]>
    <name [type, value]>
    <yearpublished [value]>
  <item [type, id]>
    <name [type, value]>
    <yearpublished [value]>
  <item [type, id]>
    <name [type, value]>

Each <item> node has attributes (type, id) and child nodes (<name>, <yearpublished>). The <name> child itself has a value attribute holding the actual name string. xml2’s navigation functions parallel CSS/XPath: xml_find_all() with an XPath expression picks nodes, xml_attr() extracts a named attribute.

Exercise — extract names and IDs

Code
game_names <- resp_bgg |>
    xml_find_all("item/name") |>
    xml_attr("value")

game_ids <- resp_bgg |>
    xml_find_all("item") |>
    xml_attr("id")

game_types <- resp_bgg |>
    xml_find_all("item") |>
    xml_attr("type")

tibble(id = game_ids, type = game_types, name = game_names) |>
    head(10)
# A tibble: 3 × 3
  id     type      name                
  <chr>  <chr>     <chr>               
1 172216 boardgame Mysterium           
2 238402 boardgame Forgotten Waters    
3 152208 boardgame Mystery of the Abbey

Why item/name returns value as an attribute rather than text content. In XML the designer chooses whether to put a piece of data inside an element (<name>Foo</name>, extracted via xml_text()) or as an attribute on an element (<name value="Foo"/>, extracted via xml_attr("value")). BGG chose attributes, so we use xml_attr(). There is no universal rule — when consuming any XML feed, xml_structure() is always the first call because it shows you which pattern the source is using.

Edge case to be aware of. Notice that xml_find_all("item/yearpublished") in the lesson notes returns length 8, while xml_find_all("item") returns length 9 — one item has no publication year. This is the XML analogue of the missing-field issue we saw in JSON: sub-elements are optional, and any extraction function that zips results by position across different node sets will break silently. Safer patterns: use xml_find_first() per item inside a loop (returns NA when missing), or use XPath predicates to filter.

New York Times API — Function-Wrapped Requests and Nested JSON

The NYT Article Search API requires a key (free, rate-limited at 1,000 calls/day): register at https://developer.nytimes.com, create an “app” with Article Search enabled, and save the key locally as nyt_api_key.txt.

Setup (guarded)

Code
times_key <- if (file.exists("nyt_api_key.txt")) {
    readLines("nyt_api_key.txt")
} else {
    ""
}

have_nyt_key <- nchar(times_key) > 0

A reusable request-builder

Code
create_nyt_url <- function(query, key = times_key) {
    request("https://api.nytimes.com") |>
        req_url_path_append("svc") |>
        req_url_path_append("search") |>
        req_url_path_append("v2") |>
        req_url_path_append("articlesearch.json") |>
        req_url_query(q = query, `api-key` = key)
}

The function earns its keep the moment we want to issue multiple queries — compare to hardcoding the URL for each. This is the same wrap-the-API-as-a-function pattern you would see in any production pipeline.

Running the query

The live call is guarded with the same two-layer pattern as the BGG request: req_error() converts HTTP 4xx/5xx into return values so a missing-key 401 doesn’t abort the chunk, and tryCatch() catches any remaining network-layer failure. Either way, a structurally faithful fallback JSON string gets parsed with fromJSON() and the downstream access pattern runs against whatever resp_pt ends up being.

Code
nyt_fallback_json <- '{
  "status": "OK",
  "copyright": "Copyright (c) 2024 The New York Times Company.",
  "response": {
    "meta": {"hits": 12, "offset": 0, "time": 15},
    "docs": [
      {
        "abstract": "A review of the magicians latest Las Vegas show.",
        "lead_paragraph": "Penn and Teller have been performing together since 1975..."
      },
      {
        "abstract": "The duo discuss their skeptical approach to the supernatural.",
        "lead_paragraph": "In a rare joint interview, the magicians describe..."
      }
    ]
  }
}'

req_pt <- create_nyt_url(query = "Penn & Teller") |>
    req_error(is_error = \(resp) FALSE)

resp_pt <- tryCatch(
    {
        resp_raw <- req_perform(req_pt)
        if (resp_status(resp_raw) >= 400) {
            message("NYT returned HTTP ", resp_status(resp_raw),
                    " (likely missing/invalid key); using cached sample.")
            fromJSON(nyt_fallback_json, simplifyVector = TRUE)
        } else {
            resp_body_json(resp_raw, simplifyVector = TRUE)
        }
    },
    error = function(e) {
        message("NYT request failed (", conditionMessage(e),
                "); using cached sample.")
        fromJSON(nyt_fallback_json, simplifyVector = TRUE)
    }
)

Reflection

URLs are structured objects, not strings. The habit of pasting together URLs with paste0() works for simple cases and fails quietly on anything with spaces, ampersands, unicode characters, or special regex-adjacent punctuation. httr2’s stepwise construction — request() + req_url_path_append() + req_url_query() — makes the structure explicit and handles encoding correctly. The I() “as-is” wrapper and the .multi parameter are the two places this structure leaks from documentation into code. Once you’ve internalized the pattern, it carries across any HTTP API — GitHub, Twitter, Spotify, Reddit, Slack — with only the endpoint and parameter names changing.

JSON and XML are both hierarchical, but their tooling is different. jsonlite::fromJSON(simplifyVector = TRUE) aggressively collapses JSON arrays of compatible objects into data frames — which is what you want 80% of the time for data-on-the-web. xml2::xml_find_all() with XPath expressions walks the tree node by node — which is what you have to do because XML makes no assumption that the tree has tabular structure. The Census/NYT JSON pipelines look very different from the BGG XML pipeline in this document, and that difference is inherent to the formats rather than accidental. When working with a new API, the first thing to check is which format it speaks, because that decides which tool family you’ll spend the next hour in.

API-wrapped-as-function is where the iteration patterns from ICA 12 pay off. create_nyt_url() takes one query string and returns a request. Once that’s in place, issuing a thousand queries is crossing() + pmap() — each row of a crossing grid becomes one query, each query becomes one request, each request gets performed in a map() step, results get parsed with resp_body_json() in another map(), and the whole thing lands in a tidy data frame via bind_rows(). The architecture for a sensitivity analysis over model specifications and the architecture for a rate-limited scrape of a JSON API are the same skeleton. Everything in ICAs 11–14 has been constructing the vocabulary for that skeleton; the APIs layer is where it starts to do work you couldn’t do by hand.

**RSCI is a constructed instrument whose evaluation heuristics partly overlap with NBA draft heuristics.** The 84% top-10-to-NBA rate looks like strong predictive validity, but it's confounded by selection: high HS rank gets a player better college offers, which means better NCAA exposure, which means higher draft position, which means more NBA opportunity. Some of what the funnel is measuring is *pipeline access* (ranked players are placed into environments that produce NBA players) rather than *latent talent* (ranked players are fundamentally better). Disentangling these would require something like comparing ranked vs. unranked players *within* the same college program, or looking at late-rising players who weren't ranked in HS but performed at comparable college levels. The piece doesn't make this distinction, and without it, "HS ranking works" conflates prediction with construction.

**The 2010–2013 cohort is right-censored.** Players drafted in 2013 had six years of NBA careers by the time of publication (February 2019). The "superstar" tier is defined by the mean of a player's top-five seasons — but a player whose best seasons will be years 5–9 of their career (Giannis Antetokounmpo was drafted in 2013 and didn't reach MVP level until 2018–19) hasn't had time to register as a superstar in this dataset. Later cohorts are therefore systematically undercounted at the top tiers while earlier cohorts have fully resolved. The piece treats the data as complete. A cleaner version would either restrict the cohort to players with at least N seasons of career, or visibly flag the censoring.

**Superstar count is small enough that the specific numbers have substantial uncertainty.** With 31 superstars across 1,563 players (~2%), the 95% confidence interval on the rate is roughly 1.4%–2.8% by normal approximation. This matters because the piece reports rates with two significant digits ("1%", "2%", "<1%") as if they were stable quantities. For tier proportions derived from small integer counts, visualizing or at least mentioning the uncertainty would keep the reader calibrated about what the funnel is and isn't pinning down.

**The animation-only visual carries accessibility costs.** Dot-transition visualizations are hostile to screen-reader users, keyboard-only navigation, and users with vestibular disorders or motion sensitivity. The "Replay" button shows the designers knew the animation was load-bearing, but there is no "skip / show final state" option — the reader has to sit through the motion to reach the summary. A parallel static-summary view (final positions, counts, percentages in a text table) would add minutes of work for the designers and open the piece to a meaningfully wider audience.

**The NCAA labor question is gestured at, not engaged.** The piece notes "the college funnel is an interesting discussion, especially when it surrounds the lack of pay for NCAA student athletes" — but then pivots to the table of top NBA-producing programs without developing that thread. The data The Pudding collected is directly relevant: Kentucky and Duke convert high HS rankings into NBA pipelines at rates far above other schools, and players whose HS rankings attracted them to those programs generated substantial revenue without compensation during this study window. That is an argumentative opportunity, and the piece walks past it. This is an editorial choice rather than a failure, but worth naming as the choice it is.

**Proportionality.** This is among the best sports data journalism of its year — the methodology is transparent, the question is genuinely well-posed, the visual form fits the quantity being communicated, and the named-individual anchoring works. The critiques above are directed at the piece's second-order structure (framing, censoring, selection), not at its execution.

## Census API — Building a URL with `httr2`

The Census API allows small queries without a key. Large production use requires a (free) key from <https://api.census.gov/data/key_signup.html>; store it locally in `census_api_key.txt` and load with `readLines()`.

```{r}
#| label: census-key
census_api_key <- if (file.exists("census_api_key.txt")) {
    readLines("census_api_key.txt")
} else {
    ""
}
```

### Building the request

```{r}
#| label: census-request
#| cache: true
req <- request("https://api.census.gov") |>
    req_url_path_append("data") |>
    req_url_path_append("2019") |>
    req_url_path_append("acs") |>
    req_url_path_append("acs1") |>
    req_url_query(
        get    = c("NAME", "B02015_009E", "B02015_009M"),
        `for`  = I("state:*"),
        key    = census_api_key,
        .multi = "comma"
    )

req
```

Three craftsmanship points worth naming:

- **`I()` around `"state:*"`**. Without the "as-is" wrapper, `httr2` would percent-encode the `:` and `*` into `%3A` and `%2A`, which the Census API would then fail to interpret. `I()` tells `httr2` to pass the value through literally. The same pattern applies any time a query value contains characters that look special but are meaningful to the target API.
- **`` `for` ``**. Backticks are required because `for` is a reserved word in R (the for-loop keyword). Any query-parameter name that collides with an R reserved word, or that contains spaces/dashes/dots, needs backtick escaping.
- **`.multi = "comma"`**. The Census API expects comma-separated lists for multi-valued parameters (`get=NAME,B02015_009E,B02015_009M`). Other APIs use different conventions — some repeat the key (`?tag=red&tag=blue`), some use brackets (`?tag[]=red&tag[]=blue`). `.multi` is where you configure this per API.

### Performing the request and parsing the response

```{r}
#| label: census-parse
#| cache: true
resp <- req_perform(req)

resp_json <- resp |> resp_body_json(simplifyVector = TRUE)

head(resp_json, 3)
```

The response comes back as a matrix with the first row as column headers — Census's convention, not JSON's. `janitor::row_to_names()` promotes the first row to column names, and then we convert the numeric variables:

```{r}
#| label: census-clean
resp_df <- resp_json |>
    row_to_names(1) |>
    as_tibble() |>
    mutate(across(starts_with("B"), as.numeric))

resp_df |>
    arrange(desc(B02015_009E)) |>
    head(10)
```

`B02015_009E` is the estimated count of persons self-identifying as Korean in the 2019 ACS 1-year sample; `_009M` is the margin of error. The `_E`/`_M` convention is pervasive in Census data and worth knowing — every estimate is paired with a margin of error, and responsible analysis respects that pairing.

## BoardGameGeek — XML and `xml2`

BGG returns XML (not JSON). The request pattern is identical to the Census example, but parsing uses `xml2` instead of `jsonlite`.

**Note on reproducibility.** The BoardGameGeek XML API imposes aggressive rate limits and regularly returns 401/429 on automated requests during a book render. To keep this portfolio rendering cleanly regardless of BGG's mood on any given day, the live call is guarded with two layers: `req_error(is_error = \(resp) FALSE)` turns HTTP 4xx/5xx responses from exceptions into regular return values so the status code can be inspected, and an outer `tryCatch()` catches network-layer failures (DNS, timeout) that still throw. Either path falls back to a small cached XML sample, so the downstream parsing code runs identically whether the live call succeeds or not. The same `req_error()` + `tryCatch()` + cached-fallback pattern is used for the NYT section below.

```{r}
#| label: bgg-request
bgg_fallback_xml <- '<items total="3" termsofuse="https://boardgamegeek.com/xmlapi/termsofuse">
  <item type="boardgame" id="172216">
    <name type="primary" value="Mysterium"/>
    <yearpublished value="2015"/>
  </item>
  <item type="boardgame" id="238402">
    <name type="primary" value="Forgotten Waters"/>
    <yearpublished value="2020"/>
  </item>
  <item type="boardgame" id="152208">
    <name type="primary" value="Mystery of the Abbey"/>
  </item>
</items>'

req_bgg <- request("https://boardgamegeek.com/xmlapi2") |>
    req_url_path_append("search") |>
    req_url_query(
        query = I("mystery+curse"),
        type  = I("boardgame,boardgameaccessory,boardgameexpansion")
    ) |>
    req_error(is_error = \(resp) FALSE)  # treat 4xx/5xx as values, not errors

resp_bgg <- tryCatch(
    {
        resp_raw <- req_perform(req_bgg)
        if (resp_status(resp_raw) >= 400) {
            message("BGG returned HTTP ", resp_status(resp_raw),
                    "; using cached sample.")
            read_xml(bgg_fallback_xml)
        } else {
            resp_body_xml(resp_raw)
        }
    },
    error = function(e) {
        # Only reaches here for network-layer errors (DNS, timeout, etc.)
        message("BGG request failed (", conditionMessage(e),
                "); using cached sample.")
        read_xml(bgg_fallback_xml)
    }
)

resp_bgg
```

The third `<item>` in the fallback deliberately omits `<yearpublished>` — so the missing-sub-element edge case discussed below is demonstrable even when the live call doesn't go through.

### Exploring the XML structure

```{r}
#| label: bgg-structure
xml_structure(resp_bgg)
```

Each `<item>` node has attributes (`type`, `id`) and child nodes (`<name>`, `<yearpublished>`). The `<name>` child itself has a `value` attribute holding the actual name string. `xml2`'s navigation functions parallel CSS/XPath: `xml_find_all()` with an XPath expression picks nodes, `xml_attr()` extracts a named attribute.

### Exercise — extract names and IDs

```{r}
#| label: bgg-extract
game_names <- resp_bgg |>
    xml_find_all("item/name") |>
    xml_attr("value")

game_ids <- resp_bgg |>
    xml_find_all("item") |>
    xml_attr("id")

game_types <- resp_bgg |>
    xml_find_all("item") |>
    xml_attr("type")

tibble(id = game_ids, type = game_types, name = game_names) |>
    head(10)
```

**Why `item/name` returns `value` as an attribute rather than text content.** In XML the designer chooses whether to put a piece of data inside an element (`<name>Foo</name>`, extracted via `xml_text()`) or as an attribute on an element (`<name value="Foo"/>`, extracted via `xml_attr("value")`). BGG chose attributes, so we use `xml_attr()`. There is no universal rule — when consuming any XML feed, `xml_structure()` is always the first call because it shows you which pattern the source is using.

**Edge case to be aware of.** Notice that `xml_find_all("item/yearpublished")` in the lesson notes returns length 8, while `xml_find_all("item")` returns length 9 — one item has no publication year. This is the XML analogue of the missing-field issue we saw in JSON: sub-elements are optional, and any extraction function that zips results by position across different node sets will break silently. Safer patterns: use `xml_find_first()` per item inside a loop (returns `NA` when missing), or use XPath predicates to filter.

## New York Times API — Function-Wrapped Requests and Nested JSON

The NYT Article Search API requires a key (free, rate-limited at 1,000 calls/day): register at <https://developer.nytimes.com>, create an "app" with Article Search enabled, and save the key locally as `nyt_api_key.txt`.

### Setup (guarded)

```{r}
#| label: nyt-key
times_key <- if (file.exists("nyt_api_key.txt")) {
    readLines("nyt_api_key.txt")
} else {
    ""
}

have_nyt_key <- nchar(times_key) > 0
```

### A reusable request-builder

```{r}
#| label: nyt-function
create_nyt_url <- function(query, key = times_key) {
    request("https://api.nytimes.com") |>
        req_url_path_append("svc") |>
        req_url_path_append("search") |>
        req_url_path_append("v2") |>
        req_url_path_append("articlesearch.json") |>
        req_url_query(q = query, `api-key` = key)
}
```

The function earns its keep the moment we want to issue multiple queries — compare to hardcoding the URL for each. This is the same wrap-the-API-as-a-function pattern you would see in any production pipeline.

### Running the query

The live call is guarded with the same two-layer pattern as the BGG request: `req_error()` converts HTTP 4xx/5xx into return values so a missing-key 401 doesn't abort the chunk, and `tryCatch()` catches any remaining network-layer failure. Either way, a structurally faithful fallback JSON string gets parsed with `fromJSON()` and the downstream access pattern runs against whatever `resp_pt` ends up being.

```{r}
#| label: nyt-request
nyt_fallback_json <- '{
  "status": "OK",
  "copyright": "Copyright (c) 2024 The New York Times Company.",
  "response": {
    "meta": {"hits": 12, "offset": 0, "time": 15},
    "docs": [
      {
        "abstract": "A review of the magicians latest Las Vegas show.",
        "lead_paragraph": "Penn and Teller have been performing together since 1975..."
      },
      {
        "abstract": "The duo discuss their skeptical approach to the supernatural.",
        "lead_paragraph": "In a rare joint interview, the magicians describe..."
      }
    ]
  }
}'

req_pt <- create_nyt_url(query = "Penn & Teller") |>
    req_error(is_error = \(resp) FALSE)

resp_pt <- tryCatch(
    {
        resp_raw <- req_perform(req_pt)
        if (resp_status(resp_raw) >= 400) {
            message("NYT returned HTTP ", resp_status(resp_raw),
                    " (likely missing/invalid key); using cached sample.")
            fromJSON(nyt_fallback_json, simplifyVector = TRUE)
        } else {
            resp_body_json(resp_raw, simplifyVector = TRUE)
        }
    },
    error = function(e) {
        message("NYT request failed (", conditionMessage(e),
                "); using cached sample.")
        fromJSON(nyt_fallback_json, simplifyVector = TRUE)
    }
)

str(resp_pt, max.level = 2)
```

### Exercise — extract copyright, hits, abstracts, and lead paragraphs

The access patterns work identically on the live response and on the fallback, because both conform to the NYT Article Search schema:

```{r}
#| label: nyt-extract
resp_pt$copyright

resp_pt$response$meta$hits

resp_pt$response$docs |>
    select(abstract, lead_paragraph) |>
    head(5)
```

## Reflection

**URLs are structured objects, not strings.** The habit of pasting together URLs with `paste0()` works for simple cases and fails quietly on anything with spaces, ampersands, unicode characters, or special regex-adjacent punctuation. `httr2`'s stepwise construction — `request()` + `req_url_path_append()` + `req_url_query()` — makes the structure explicit and handles encoding correctly. The `I()` "as-is" wrapper and the `.multi` parameter are the two places this structure leaks from documentation into code. Once you've internalized the pattern, it carries across any HTTP API — GitHub, Twitter, Spotify, Reddit, Slack — with only the endpoint and parameter names changing.

**JSON and XML are both hierarchical, but their tooling is different.** `jsonlite::fromJSON(simplifyVector = TRUE)` aggressively collapses JSON arrays of compatible objects into data frames — which is what you want 80% of the time for data-on-the-web. `xml2::xml_find_all()` with XPath expressions walks the tree node by node — which is what you have to do because XML makes no assumption that the tree has tabular structure. The Census/NYT JSON pipelines look very different from the BGG XML pipeline in this document, and that difference is inherent to the formats rather than accidental. When working with a new API, the first thing to check is which format it speaks, because that decides which tool family you'll spend the next hour in.

**API-wrapped-as-function is where the iteration patterns from ICA 12 pay off.** `create_nyt_url()` takes one query string and returns a request. Once that's in place, issuing a thousand queries is `crossing() + pmap()` — each row of a crossing grid becomes one query, each query becomes one request, each request gets performed in a `map()` step, results get parsed with `resp_body_json()` in another `map()`, and the whole thing lands in a tidy data frame via `bind_rows()`. The architecture for a sensitivity analysis over model specifications and the architecture for a rate-limited scrape of a JSON API are the same skeleton. Everything in ICAs 11–14 has been constructing the vocabulary for that skeleton; the APIs layer is where it starts to do work you couldn't do by hand.
