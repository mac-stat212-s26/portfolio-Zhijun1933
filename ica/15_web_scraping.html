🔥 Data Story Critique — Equity in Community Development (City of Saint Paul)

Source: https://storymaps.arcgis.com/collections/7ccaef0b7a4b4166804f36e64e43c4cd?item=5

What is the data story?

The piece is a municipal data dashboard produced by the City of Saint Paul as part of its open-data initiative, framed explicitly around the vision of “a city where race does not predetermine opportunities in education, employment, housing, health, and safety.” It presents a racial-demographic dot map of Saint Paul (each resident a dot randomly placed within their census block group, color-coded by race/ethnicity from ACS responses) followed by six “core racial equity metrics” with definitions and charts: unemployment, labor force participation, per capita income, homeownership, cost-burdened households, and business ownership. The dashboard sits inside the city’s broader Open Information Saint Paul portal — so it functions both as a standalone piece of civic communication and as an entry point into the city’s public data infrastructure. Unlike the NGO and news pieces we’ve critiqued, this is a government document, which imposes a distinct set of responsibilities: residents-as-audience rather than donors, public-accountability obligations, and compliance with Minnesota’s open-records and open-data expectations.

What is effective?

Upfront operational definitions for every metric. Unemployment is defined as “16 years and older, actively looking for work, not currently employed,” and explicitly notes what it excludes — discouraged workers, underemployed, skills-mismatched. This kind of operational transparency is unusual for municipal communication and is what separates data-as-civic-infrastructure from data-as-rhetoric. A reader can check what the number means and what it doesn’t.

Self-awareness about metric limitations. The cost-burdened households section names the known shortcomings of the 30%-of-income threshold: “a low-income household that earns $12,000 per year may not have enough money left over for other needed expenses after paying 30% of their income for housing. Additionally, the statistic makes no adjustment for family size.” This caveat rarely appears in government dashboards, and its presence here signals that the authors understand the residual-income critique of the HUD affordability threshold. Naming the limitation is the first step toward choosing better metrics.

The dot map is ethically designed. Random placement within census block groups, rather than at address-level precision, prevents the map from functioning as a targeting tool for anyone who might use racial demographics for discriminatory purposes. This is the design pattern pioneered by the University of Virginia’s Racial Dot Map (2013); Saint Paul correctly adopts it. The choice of block-group aggregation is also right — finer than tracts (preserves neighborhood texture), coarser than parcels (respects privacy).

Integration with the broader open-data portal. The dashboard is not a standalone marketing piece — it links out to Open Information Saint Paul, the city’s hub for public data. This positions the dashboard as part of an infrastructure of transparency rather than as a communications artifact, which is the correct institutional framing for municipal data work.

What could be improved?

“People of color” as a single category flattens heterogeneity that is uniquely important in Saint Paul. The city has one of the largest Hmong-American communities in the United States (~11% of the population, one of the highest concentrations nationally), plus substantial Somali, Ethiopian, Karen, and Latino populations. These communities have different immigration histories, different generational depths in Minnesota, and therefore different economic and housing trajectories — Hmong homeownership rates are much higher than Somali homeownership rates for structural reasons tied to arrival timing and refugee resettlement cohorts, not to any cross-group comparison of effort or values. Presenting aggregate “white vs. people of color” gaps obscures where the equity work is most acute and where it has been partially successful. The city has the ACS data to disaggregate — the decision not to is a communications choice that costs policy precision.

All six metrics are outcome metrics; none are process metrics under the city’s direct control. Unemployment, income, homeownership, cost-burden, business ownership — these are places in the system where inequity arrives. Missing are the upstream processes the city government itself has levers on: mortgage rejection rates by race (HMDA data is public), eviction filings by neighborhood demographic, police stop rates by neighborhood demographic, school-funding disparities, city contracting disaggregated by owner demographic, Section 8 voucher placement rates. A municipal equity dashboard without process metrics is a dashboard of the problem; adding process metrics turns it into a dashboard the city can be held accountable to.

The framing is point-in-time for a question that is fundamentally about trajectory. The piece notes that “the gap has widened in per capita income between white people and people of color” but the standard presentation is a snapshot. Equity work is about direction and rate of change: is the gap closing, at what pace, and relative to what target? A commitment to “a city where race does not predetermine opportunities” is meaningful only when paired with a temporal trajectory and a target trajectory. The ArcGIS host may support this through tabs or sliders, but the document as written does not foreground trend.

No commitments, no targets, no accountability mechanisms. The dashboard describes where Saint Paul is. It does not tell the reader what the city has promised to be, by when, measured how, or with what consequences if the gaps do not close. In municipal governance scholarship, the distance between describing inequity and committing to measurable correction is sometimes called “equity theater” — the performance of awareness without the commitment to change. For a city that has positioned this dashboard as part of an equity framework, the absence of linked commitments is the single most important next step.

Causal framing is absent where it is most needed. “People of color drove much of that [population] growth, while the white population has primarily remained the same over time” — presented as observation. The forces producing this pattern are not named: immigration, differential birth rates, white flight to suburban ring, differential cost-of-living displacement, the city’s own historical role (redlining of Rondo, the construction of I-94 through the heart of that Black neighborhood in the 1960s). A municipal equity dashboard that does not name the city government’s own historical production of current disparities — including the documented Rondo displacement — is accepting the disparities as demographic rather than structural. The accountability question is sharper when the production is acknowledged.

Proportionality. This is a credible municipal effort — above-median for city-produced equity dashboards nationally on definitional clarity, open-data integration, and ethical map design. The critiques above are directed at the next iteration: within-POC disaggregation, process metrics under city control, temporal trajectory, explicit targets with accountability, and causal acknowledgment. These are the moves that separate a civic-transparency document from a governance-accountability instrument.

Ethics — Reading robots.txt

Before scraping, check the site’s robots.txt file. For the National Institutes of Health, this is at https://www.nih.gov/robots.txt. The key fields to look for:

  • User-agent: * — who is being addressed. * means everyone; specific bot names (Googlebot, Bingbot) mean only those.
  • Disallow: /path/ — paths you must not scrape.
  • Crawl-delay: N — minimum wait between requests in seconds.
  • Visit-time: HH:MM-HH:MM — permitted hours of scraping (rare).

For the NIH news releases path we’re targeting, none of these are restrictive: scraping is allowed, no crawl-delay specified, no time-of-day restriction. Even so, the polite default is to Sys.sleep(2) between page requests — you’re sharing the server with the hospital systems and researchers who are the actual intended users.

Splitting date from abstract

The date-abstract separator on NIH pages is typically an em-dash (—) surrounded by spaces. separate_wider_regex() is the right tool here because it tolerates slight variations in dash type (em-dash —, en-dash –, or hyphen -):

Code
tibble(desc = article_descriptions) |>
    separate_wider_regex(
        desc,
        patterns = c(
            pub_date  = "^[^—–-]+",        # everything up to the first dash
            "\\s*[—–-]\\s*",              # the dash separator (discarded)
            abstract  = ".*$"              # everything after
        ),
        too_few = "align_start"            # graceful on rows without a dash
    ) |>
    mutate(
        pub_date = str_trim(pub_date),
        abstract = str_trim(abstract)
    ) |>
    head(3)

Function-izing the Scraper

The ICA template wants one function that, given a URL, returns a tibble of title / pub_date / abstract for that page. Writing it this way matches the ICA 12 pattern: a reusable dispatcher that a map/for-loop can iterate over.

Code
get_text_from_page <- function(page, css_selector) {
    page |>
        html_elements(css_selector) |>
        html_text() |>
        str_squish()
}

# Structured placeholder for when a live scrape fails. Same columns as the
# success path so downstream bind_rows / mutate operate on a consistent shape.
nih_fallback_row <- function(url) {
    tibble(
        title    = "[live scrape unavailable — cached sample]",
        pub_date = "January 1, 2026",
        abstract = "Placeholder row inserted because the live NIH scrape failed at render time (network hiccup, rate limit, or upstream change). Re-render on a stable connection to repopulate.",
        src_url  = url
    )
}

scrape_nih_page <- function(url, delay_sec = 2) {
    Sys.sleep(delay_sec)                    # respectful pacing

    # Guard the network call. read_html() throws on DNS / timeout / empty reply;
    # tryCatch returns the fallback row so render proceeds instead of halting.
    tryCatch({
        page <- read_html(url)

        titles       <- get_text_from_page(page, ".thumbnail-teaser__heading")
        descriptions <- get_text_from_page(page, ".thumbnail-teaser__description")

        # If the page loaded but selectors returned nothing (site redesign),
        # treat it as a failure and use the fallback.
        if (length(titles) == 0 || length(descriptions) == 0) {
            message("NIH page returned no matching elements (", url,
                    "); using fallback.")
            return(nih_fallback_row(url))
        }

        tibble(title = titles, desc = descriptions) |>
            separate_wider_regex(
                desc,
                patterns = c(
                    pub_date = "^[^—–-]+",
                    "\\s*[—–-]\\s*",
                    abstract = ".*$"
                ),
                too_few = "align_start"
            ) |>
            mutate(
                pub_date = str_trim(pub_date),
                abstract = str_trim(abstract),
                src_url  = url
            )
    },
    error = function(e) {
        message("NIH scrape failed for ", url,
                " (", conditionMessage(e), "); using fallback.")
        nih_fallback_row(url)
    })
}

Five deliberate choices here:

  • Helper function get_text_from_page(). Two nearly-identical html_elements() |> html_text() |> str_squish() sequences would otherwise be copy-pasted; extracting the pattern into a small function makes the main scraper one step cleaner.
  • delay_sec as a parameter with a default of 2. If robots.txt specified Crawl-delay: 5, a caller can bump the default to 5 without modifying the function body. This is the parameterization lesson from ICA 9: don’t hardcode what could vary.
  • src_url column. Adding the source URL to every row makes downstream debugging trivial — if one row looks wrong, you can go directly to the page it came from.
  • str_squish() on every text extraction. HTML pages include a lot of incidental whitespace (newlines from indentation, leading/trailing spaces inside tags). Squishing once at extraction time prevents those artifacts from leaking into every downstream string operation.
  • tryCatch fallback to a structured placeholder row. Live scrapes fail for many reasons — transient network errors, site redesigns that invalidate the CSS selector, rate-limit responses, DNS hiccups during a long render. Rather than let one failed URL crash the whole map() pipeline, the function returns a clearly-labelled placeholder row for the failed URL and lets the rest of the iteration proceed. This mirrors the req_error() + tryCatch() pattern used in the API chapter.

The map() version is shorter, reads more like a sentence (“map scrape_nih_page over the URL vector”), and avoids explicit storage allocation. The for loop earns its place when there’s iteration state that map() can’t carry — adaptive backoff after a rate-limit error, for instance, where iteration i+1’s behaviour depends on iteration i’s response. For straight parallel application, map() wins on readability every time.

Reflection

CSS selectors are the interface between page design and scraping code. A selector like .thumbnail-teaser__heading is not a universal semantic ID — it is a class name chosen by the NIH web team for styling purposes. If they redesign the site tomorrow and rename that class to .news-item__title, every scraper keyed on the old name silently breaks. This is a genuine structural fragility of web scraping that APIs do not have: an API’s field names are part of a documented contract, but a website’s class names are an internal styling convention that can change without notice. The ICA’s warning — “save and date a copy of the website” — is the practical response. For any scraping pipeline intended to run beyond a single session, snapshot the HTML, re-verify selectors periodically, and write the code defensively (check that the number of elements returned matches expectations, and fail loudly if it doesn’t).

Scraping etiquette is not optional, even when robots.txt permits it. A scraper that hammers a server with unbounded parallel requests is a denial-of-service event, regardless of what the text file says. Sys.sleep(2) between requests, User-Agent identification when the library permits, caching of results to avoid re-scraping on every iteration, and running scrapes during off-peak hours are the minimum discipline. The NIH example is a public health agency whose website serves researchers, clinicians, and patients looking for trial information; a well-behaved scraper is a precondition for the long-term viability of scraping as a research technique.

The crossing() + pmap() skeleton still holds; scraping is just another pmap() target. The pattern of this ICA — write a function that handles one input, iterate over many inputs, bind results — is identical to the pattern in the API ICA, the case-study ICA in Loops I, and the simulation ICA in Missing Data. The only difference is what the function does inside: here it hits a URL, parses HTML, extracts fields; in the API case it hit a URL, parsed JSON, extracted fields. Almost every “do this analysis many times” pattern you will encounter in empirical work reduces to this skeleton. The skill being built across these weeks is the instinct for recognizing when a problem has that shape, and the discipline for wrapping the inner step cleanly enough that the outer iteration is trivial.

Equity work is about *direction and rate of change*: is the gap closing, at what pace, and relative to what target? A commitment to "a city where race does not predetermine opportunities" is meaningful only when paired with a temporal trajectory and a target trajectory. The ArcGIS host may support this through tabs or sliders, but the document as written does not foreground trend.

**No commitments, no targets, no accountability mechanisms.** The dashboard describes where Saint Paul is. It does not tell the reader what the city has promised to be, by when, measured how, or with what consequences if the gaps do not close. In municipal governance scholarship, the distance between describing inequity and committing to measurable correction is sometimes called "equity theater" — the performance of awareness without the commitment to change. For a city that has positioned this dashboard as part of an equity framework, the absence of linked commitments is the single most important next step.

**Causal framing is absent where it is most needed.** "People of color drove much of that [population] growth, while the white population has primarily remained the same over time" — presented as observation. The forces producing this pattern are not named: immigration, differential birth rates, white flight to suburban ring, differential cost-of-living displacement, the city's own historical role (redlining of Rondo, the construction of I-94 through the heart of that Black neighborhood in the 1960s). A municipal equity dashboard that does not name the city government's own historical production of current disparities — including the documented Rondo displacement — is accepting the disparities as demographic rather than structural. The accountability question is sharper when the production is acknowledged.

**Proportionality.** This is a credible municipal effort — above-median for city-produced equity dashboards nationally on definitional clarity, open-data integration, and ethical map design. The critiques above are directed at the *next iteration*: within-POC disaggregation, process metrics under city control, temporal trajectory, explicit targets with accountability, and causal acknowledgment. These are the moves that separate a civic-transparency document from a governance-accountability instrument.

## Ethics — Reading `robots.txt`

Before scraping, check the site's `robots.txt` file. For the National Institutes of Health, this is at <https://www.nih.gov/robots.txt>. The key fields to look for:

- **`User-agent: *`** — who is being addressed. `*` means everyone; specific bot names (`Googlebot`, `Bingbot`) mean only those.
- **`Disallow: /path/`** — paths you must not scrape.
- **`Crawl-delay: N`** — minimum wait between requests in seconds.
- **`Visit-time: HH:MM-HH:MM`** — permitted hours of scraping (rare).

For the NIH news releases path we're targeting, none of these are restrictive: scraping is allowed, no crawl-delay specified, no time-of-day restriction. Even so, the polite default is to `Sys.sleep(2)` between page requests — you're sharing the server with the hospital systems and researchers who are the actual intended users.

### Splitting date from abstract

The date-abstract separator on NIH pages is typically an em-dash (`—`) surrounded by spaces. `separate_wider_regex()` is the right tool here because it tolerates slight variations in dash type (em-dash `—`, en-dash `–`, or hyphen `-`):

```{r}
tibble(desc = article_descriptions) |>
    separate_wider_regex(
        desc,
        patterns = c(
            pub_date  = "^[^—–-]+",        # everything up to the first dash
            "\\s*[—–-]\\s*",              # the dash separator (discarded)
            abstract  = ".*$"              # everything after
        ),
        too_few = "align_start"            # graceful on rows without a dash
    ) |>
    mutate(
        pub_date = str_trim(pub_date),
        abstract = str_trim(abstract)
    ) |>
    head(3)
```

Using `separate_wider_regex()` rather than `separate_wider_delim(delim = " — ")` is the safer choice: the regex matches multiple dash types, and `too_few = "align_start"` prevents a single malformed row from crashing the whole pipeline.

## Function-izing the Scraper

The ICA template wants one function that, given a URL, returns a tibble of title / pub_date / abstract for that page. Writing it this way matches the ICA 12 pattern: a reusable dispatcher that a map/for-loop can iterate over.

```{r}
#| label: scrape-fn
get_text_from_page <- function(page, css_selector) {
    page |>
        html_elements(css_selector) |>
        html_text() |>
        str_squish()
}

# Structured placeholder for when a live scrape fails. Same columns as the
# success path so downstream bind_rows / mutate operate on a consistent shape.
nih_fallback_row <- function(url) {
    tibble(
        title    = "[live scrape unavailable — cached sample]",
        pub_date = "January 1, 2026",
        abstract = "Placeholder row inserted because the live NIH scrape failed at render time (network hiccup, rate limit, or upstream change). Re-render on a stable connection to repopulate.",
        src_url  = url
    )
}

scrape_nih_page <- function(url, delay_sec = 2) {
    Sys.sleep(delay_sec)                    # respectful pacing

    # Guard the network call. read_html() throws on DNS / timeout / empty reply;
    # tryCatch returns the fallback row so render proceeds instead of halting.
    tryCatch({
        page <- read_html(url)

        titles       <- get_text_from_page(page, ".thumbnail-teaser__heading")
        descriptions <- get_text_from_page(page, ".thumbnail-teaser__description")

        # If the page loaded but selectors returned nothing (site redesign),
        # treat it as a failure and use the fallback.
        if (length(titles) == 0 || length(descriptions) == 0) {
            message("NIH page returned no matching elements (", url,
                    "); using fallback.")
            return(nih_fallback_row(url))
        }

        tibble(title = titles, desc = descriptions) |>
            separate_wider_regex(
                desc,
                patterns = c(
                    pub_date = "^[^—–-]+",
                    "\\s*[—–-]\\s*",
                    abstract = ".*$"
                ),
                too_few = "align_start"
            ) |>
            mutate(
                pub_date = str_trim(pub_date),
                abstract = str_trim(abstract),
                src_url  = url
            )
    },
    error = function(e) {
        message("NIH scrape failed for ", url,
                " (", conditionMessage(e), "); using fallback.")
        nih_fallback_row(url)
    })
}
```

Five deliberate choices here:

- **Helper function `get_text_from_page()`.** Two nearly-identical `html_elements() |> html_text() |> str_squish()` sequences would otherwise be copy-pasted; extracting the pattern into a small function makes the main scraper one step cleaner.
- **`delay_sec` as a parameter with a default of 2.** If `robots.txt` specified `Crawl-delay: 5`, a caller can bump the default to 5 without modifying the function body. This is the parameterization lesson from ICA 9: don't hardcode what could vary.
- **`src_url` column.** Adding the source URL to every row makes downstream debugging trivial — if one row looks wrong, you can go directly to the page it came from.
- **`str_squish()` on every text extraction.** HTML pages include a lot of incidental whitespace (newlines from indentation, leading/trailing spaces inside tags). Squishing once at extraction time prevents those artifacts from leaking into every downstream string operation.
- **`tryCatch` fallback to a structured placeholder row.** Live scrapes fail for many reasons — transient network errors, site redesigns that invalidate the CSS selector, rate-limit responses, DNS hiccups during a long render. Rather than let one failed URL crash the whole `map()` pipeline, the function returns a clearly-labelled placeholder row for the failed URL and lets the rest of the iteration proceed. This mirrors the `req_error()` + `tryCatch()` pattern used in the API chapter.

## Iterating Across Pages — `map()` and `for` Loop

### URL pattern

```{r}
# Mousing over the pagination buttons at the bottom of the NIH page reveals
# that page 1 is the base URL, and subsequent pages append `?page=1`, `?page=2`, etc.
base_url <- "https://www.nih.gov/news-events/news-releases"
urls_5   <- c(base_url, str_c(base_url, "?page=", 1:4))
urls_5
```

### With `purrr::map()`

```{r}
#| label: scrape-map
#| cache: true
pages_map <- map(urls_5, scrape_nih_page)
df_map    <- bind_rows(pages_map)

nrow(df_map)
head(df_map, 5)
```

### With a `for` loop

```{r}
#| label: scrape-forloop
#| cache: true
pages_for <- vector("list", length = length(urls_5))

for (i in seq_along(urls_5)) {
    pages_for[[i]] <- scrape_nih_page(urls_5[i])
}

df_for <- bind_rows(pages_for)

nrow(df_for)
```

### Equivalence check

```{r}
identical(df_map, df_for)
```

The `map()` version is shorter, reads more like a sentence ("map `scrape_nih_page` over the URL vector"), and avoids explicit storage allocation. The `for` loop earns its place when there's iteration state that `map()` can't carry — adaptive backoff after a rate-limit error, for instance, where iteration `i+1`'s behaviour depends on iteration `i`'s response. For straight parallel application, `map()` wins on readability every time.

## Reflection

**CSS selectors are the interface between page design and scraping code.** A selector like `.thumbnail-teaser__heading` is not a universal semantic ID — it is a class name chosen by the NIH web team for styling purposes. If they redesign the site tomorrow and rename that class to `.news-item__title`, every scraper keyed on the old name silently breaks. This is a genuine structural fragility of web scraping that APIs do not have: an API's field names are part of a documented contract, but a website's class names are an internal styling convention that can change without notice. The ICA's warning — "save and date a copy of the website" — is the practical response. For any scraping pipeline intended to run beyond a single session, snapshot the HTML, re-verify selectors periodically, and write the code defensively (check that the number of elements returned matches expectations, and fail loudly if it doesn't).

**Scraping etiquette is not optional, even when `robots.txt` permits it.** A scraper that hammers a server with unbounded parallel requests is a denial-of-service event, regardless of what the text file says. `Sys.sleep(2)` between requests, `User-Agent` identification when the library permits, caching of results to avoid re-scraping on every iteration, and running scrapes during off-peak hours are the minimum discipline. The NIH example is a public health agency whose website serves researchers, clinicians, and patients looking for trial information; a well-behaved scraper is a precondition for the long-term viability of scraping as a research technique.

**The `crossing() + pmap()` skeleton still holds; scraping is just another `pmap()` target.** The pattern of this ICA — write a function that handles one input, iterate over many inputs, bind results — is identical to the pattern in the API ICA, the case-study ICA in Loops I, and the simulation ICA in Missing Data. The only difference is what the function does inside: here it hits a URL, parses HTML, extracts fields; in the API case it hit a URL, parsed JSON, extracted fields. Almost every "do this analysis many times" pattern you will encounter in empirical work reduces to this skeleton. The skill being built across these weeks is the instinct for recognizing when a problem has that shape, and the discipline for wrapping the inner step cleanly enough that the outer iteration is trivial.
