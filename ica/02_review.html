🔥 Data Story Critique — The Rhythm of Food

Source: https://rhythm-of-food.net/#explore-foods

Creators: Moritz Stefaner / Truth & Beauty (creative direction, design, viz); Yuri Vishnevsky (development); Google News Lab (Simon Rogers, Alberto Cairo); Stefanie Weigele (illustration); Destry Sibley (text). 12–15 years of weekly Google Trends data, US-focused, disambiguated through Google Knowledge Graph topics. 195 topics, 129,660 data points.

What is the data story?

The piece argues that food has measurable signatures of seasonality visible in how people search for it. Each food gets a radial “year clock”: the 52 angular segments are weeks of the year, distance from center encodes relative search interest, and color encodes which year the reading came from. Stacked over 12+ years, the charts surface two layers of pattern simultaneously — the shape of a food’s seasonality (does interest peak in November? in July? in no month at all?) and its trend (is the year-color drifting outward or inward over time?). The site builds its argument upward: an overview grid of many foods makes the existence of distinct seasonality signatures visible at a glance; the single-food detail views let the reader interrogate a specific rhythm; the annotated stories tie a handful of those rhythms to cultural events (Thanksgiving pumpkin, Easter lamb) or agricultural calendars (asparagus in April, corn in August).

What is effective?

The form genuinely matches the data. Seasonality is cyclical — December rolls into January. A linear line chart of weeks 1–52 forces the reader to mentally wrap the endpoints; a radial plot removes that translation step entirely. Decoding the chart as a clock is pre-cognitive, which is the right property for a chart that asks the reader to compare hundreds of foods.

Two variables in one mark, both used. Distance encodes interest; color encodes year. Most dual-encodings fail because one variable drowns out the other. Here it works because year is ordered and the color ramp is ordered — so a reader can see trend over years as a color gradient walking outward or inward, on top of the same radial shape that encodes within-year seasonality. Reading both axes at once is what makes “the rise of avocado” or “the collapse of kale” visible; a chart that showed only one axis would lose the argument.

Knowledge Graph disambiguation. “Apple” search interest is dominated by Apple Inc., not the fruit. The site uses Google Knowledge Graph topics rather than raw keyword strings, which resolves this and a long tail of similar ambiguities (rosemary the plant vs. the name, turkey the bird vs. the country). This is invisible engineering — it doesn’t appear in the UI — but it’s the difference between a dataset that supports the argument and a dataset that doesn’t. Naming it is worth a line because the tutorial-visualization genre often skips this step and then quietly serves the reader contaminated data.

The aggregate view earns the detail view. The overview grid is where the piece’s argument lives: looking at a wall of radial charts at once, you see that different foods have fundamentally different seasonality signatures — some spike sharply once, some have twin peaks, some are flat year-round. The detail view for any single food only lands because the aggregate has already established that “signatures exist” is a meaningful category.

What could be improved?

Search interest is not consumption. This is the central methodological issue and the site only glances at it. People search Google for pumpkin pie in November because Thanksgiving is coming and they don’t make pumpkin pie in the other eleven months — not because pumpkins are biologically in season. The rhythm being visualized is the rhythm of when people ask about food, which is correlated with but distinct from the rhythm of eating food. For stable-use commodity ingredients (salt, flour, oil), the signal is close to noise; for holiday-tied dishes, the signal is enormously amplified by a single cultural event. The title, The Rhythm of Food, collapses these two rhythms — cultural-attention rhythm and eating rhythm — into one word. A more careful framing would have been The Rhythm of Food Attention, which is a weaker title and a more honest one.

The radial form makes magnitude comparison harder, not easier. Polar coordinates turn linear differences in a value into nonlinear differences in visible area — area grows with the square of the radius. A week with search interest of 40 does not look twice as prominent as a week with interest of 20; it looks closer to four times as prominent. This is a well-documented failure mode of radial charts. The form’s elegance is not neutral: it visually exaggerates peak weeks and compresses trough weeks, which systematically inflates the apparent strength of any seasonality signature. The charts are more rhythmic-looking than the underlying data actually is.

Google Trends’ “search interest” is peak-normalized and opaque. Trends values are rescaled to 0–100 relative to the maximum within the query window, not absolute search counts. This means two foods’ radial charts cannot be compared by amplitude — each is normalized to its own peak. A reader who leaves the site thinking “pumpkin pie is more popular than oatmeal” has been misled, because the comparison is formally undefined by the data pipeline. This is disclosed on the methodology page but invisible in the main viewing experience.

“US data” is underdisclosed given the title. The site restricts to US searches. The rhythm detected is therefore Anglo-American search rhythm — which is not “the rhythm of food” in the universal sense the title implies. The seasonality of Lunar New Year foods, Diwali sweets, Ramadan breakfasts, Midsummer herring in Sweden, Jixi cold noodles in July — none of these appear, because the underlying searches don’t appear. A project that names itself after a universal would do well to either include the universal in the data or name its particularity in the title. “The Rhythm of American Food Searches” is the honest title; “The Rhythm of Food” is the marketable one.

The artifact is frozen; food culture is not. The data cuts off around 2019. It is now 2026. Foods that looked ascendant at launch (kale, turmeric, avocado) have continued their curves in either direction without being shown. A rhythm, by definition, continues. Presenting it as a finished object rather than a live feed converts an argument about ongoing cultural phenomenon into a historical exhibit.

Search-as-culture over-indexes on novelty. Google Trends captures a very specific slice of food attention — the “I want to know about X right now” kind, triggered by external stimulus (a TV mention, a magazine article, a holiday, a TikTok). It misses food culture that is so normalized it generates no search (daily rice, bread, eggs) and over-represents food culture driven by novelty (fads, diets, viral recipes). The rhythm you see is therefore the rhythm of food novelty cycling, not the rhythm of food as lived practice. This is the same bias that makes Google Trends a better tool for studying marketing than for studying consumption.

Absolute vs. Relative Paths — One Sentence of Theory

An absolute path starts at the filesystem root and is specific to one machine: /Users/zhijun/Documents/Macalester/COMP-STAT-212/portfolio/ica/data/raw/weather.csv. A relative path starts from wherever the code is currently being executed and is portable: data/raw/weather.csv (from the ica/ folder).

The cost of an absolute path is not that it’s ugly. It’s that the code stops working the moment anyone else runs it, including future-me on a different machine, a collaborator, an autograder, or a CI pipeline. Relative paths are not a style preference; they are the minimum condition for reproducible code.

In a Quarto document, the working directory is the folder containing the .qmd file. So when this file lives in ica/ and the data lives in ica/data/raw/, the relative path is data/raw/weather.csv — no leading slash, no .., no ~.

Exercise — Cleaning the Weather Data

1. Load packages and read in data

Code
weather <- read_csv("data/raw/weather.csv")

The path data/raw/weather.csv is relative to the location of this .qmd file (ica/). It will work on any machine where someone has cloned the portfolio repo and preserved the folder structure — which is exactly the property we want.

2. Clean PrecipYr — replace 99999 with NA

Code
weather_clean <- weather |>
    mutate(PrecipYr = na_if(PrecipYr, 99999))

99999 is a sentinel value for missing — a punch-card-era convention where a value impossible for the measured quantity is used to signal absence. The convention is dangerous because a sentinel looks like valid data to every numeric operation downstream: mean(PrecipYr) will silently include the 99999s and return a number that is off by orders of magnitude, with no warning.

na_if(x, y) is the tidyverse idiom for this conversion: it replaces y with NA in x. The naïve alternative ifelse(PrecipYr == 99999, NA, PrecipYr) works but is wordier and has a subtle trap — ifelse() strips attributes and may coerce types, which matters if PrecipYr is a double you want to keep as a double. na_if() preserves type and is harder to misuse.

3. Add a dateInYear variable

Code
# Option 1: sort-and-assign
weather_clean_v1 <- weather_clean |>
    arrange(Month, Day) |>
    mutate(dateInYear = 1:365)
Code
# Option 2: compute from the date
weather_clean <- weather_clean |>
    mutate(dateInYear = yday(mdy(date)))

Both run. Only one is safe.

Option 1 silently assumes the dataset is exactly 365 rows, sorted cleanly, with no leap-year row, no duplicates, and no missing days. If any of those assumptions fail — a leap year adds a 366th row, a missing February day reduces to 364, a duplicate inflates to 366 — 1:365 recycles or errors depending on which. The bug is silent: the column is still produced, but day 60 is now labeled as day 61, and every downstream analysis is off by a day without warning.

Option 2 is the robust choice. mdy() parses the date string into a Date object; yday() extracts the day-of-year from that date. The computation uses the content of each row, not its position in a sorted sequence. It handles 365-day years, 366-day leap years, missing rows, duplicates, and out-of-order data identically — because it never depends on row order at all.

The general principle: prefer computations that derive the target value from row content over computations that derive it from row position. Position-based tricks break silently; content-based computations fail loudly or not at all.

months <- tibble(

weather_clean <- weather_clean |>

Both produce identical results on this dataset. They differ in what they generalize to.

month.abb (and its sibling month.name) are built-in character vectors — the kind of base-R constant that is trivially available but easy to forget. Direct subsetting month.abb[Month] is the tightest expression when (a) the lookup table is a length-12 character vector and (b) the key is the integer 1–12. When both conditions hold, it’s three tokens and zero intermediate objects.

The join approach is what generalizes. If tomorrow the lookup has to become a full-name-and-short-name-and-fiscal-quarter table, the join pattern is unchanged — only the lookup tibble grows. If the lookup has to come from a CSV, the join absorbs that too. Vector subsetting handles today’s problem more elegantly; joins handle tomorrow’s problem at all.

The right instinct: start with the join pattern as the default, and drop down to vector subsetting only when the lookup is both tiny and index-keyed.

5. Write out the cleaned data

Code
# Ensure the output directory exists before writing
dir.create("data/processed", showWarnings = FALSE, recursive = TRUE)
write_csv(weather_clean, file = "data/processed/weather_clean.csv")

Three observations worth naming:

  • data/raw/ vs. data/processed/. The output goes to processed/, not back to raw/. This enforces the principle that raw data is immutable — never overwritten, always the starting point. Processed derivatives live in a separate folder and can be regenerated at any time by re-running the pipeline. The convention is not cosmetic; it’s what makes an analysis reproducible, because it guarantees that raw/ is exactly what came in from the source.
  • Relative path, same logic as the read. data/processed/weather_clean.csv works from any machine where the repo is cloned. The symmetry between read and write is intentional — both ends of the pipeline are tied to the repo structure, not to any particular filesystem.
  • CSV because it’s durable. Not .rds, not .parquet. A plain CSV is readable by anything — R, Python, Excel, cat in a terminal in 2050. For a classroom pipeline where the next step might be done in a different language or tool, CSV is the format that doesn’t lock the pipeline into one stack.

Git Verbs — A Working Mental Model

The five verbs map to three mental locations:

  • clone — bring a remote repo to my local machine for the first time. Done once per repo.
  • add + commit — stage and record changes locally. The commit is a snapshot with a message; it lives in my local .git/ until pushed.
  • push — upload my local commits to the remote (GitHub).
  • pull — download commits from the remote that I don’t yet have locally.

The shape: remote ⇄ local working copy. clone is the initial download; pull is incremental download; push is upload; add + commit is how I decide what becomes uploadable. The loop for any work session is pull → work → add → commit → push, with the pull at the start catching anything a collaborator (or past-me on another machine) pushed since I last worked.

Two failure modes to avoid: (1) forgetting to pull before starting work, which produces merge conflicts that are avoidable; (2) committing frequently locally but never pushing, which is equivalent to not backing up the work at all until something breaks.

Keyboard Shortcuts — A Short List That Pays Back

The handful that compound over a semester:

  • Cmd/Ctrl + Enter — run the current line or selection. The most-used shortcut in the editor.
  • Cmd/Ctrl + Shift + M — insert the pipe |>. Saves three keystrokes every time I chain verbs, which is constantly.
  • Cmd/Ctrl + Shift + Enter — run the entire chunk.
  • Cmd/Ctrl + Option/Alt + I — insert a new code chunk in a Quarto/Rmd document.
  • Cmd/Ctrl + Shift + C — toggle comment on selected lines.
  • Cmd/Ctrl + S — save. Tied to Quarto render (Cmd/Ctrl + Shift + K).
  • Tab — autocomplete object and function names. Reading documentation without typing full names is how you learn what’s available in a package.

Every shortcut replaces a mouse trip. One mouse trip is not worth changing behavior for; a thousand mouse trips over a semester is the difference between the editor feeling like a barrier and feeling like an extension of thought.

Reflection

Three things to carry forward.

Relative paths are a reproducibility discipline, not an aesthetic preference. An absolute path is a commitment that the code will only ever run on one machine. Every "/Users/zhijun/..." in a codebase is a future failure, whether it surfaces in a collaborator’s IDE next week or in CI in six months. The discipline is to never let one leak in, because they are invisible in normal operation — the code works until the machine changes, and then it doesn’t.

Sort-and-assign is a silent failure waiting to happen. The Option 1 / Option 2 split in the dateInYear question is the template for a whole class of bugs: any transformation that derives a value from row position in a sorted order rather than from row content is fragile to the ordering assumptions holding. When Option 2 exists (compute the target directly from the date), take it — even when Option 1 looks simpler on the happy path. The cost of the bug is not the bug itself; it’s that you’ll never notice it ran.

Git’s four-verb loop is almost all of day-to-day git. pull → work → add → commit → push. Conflict resolution, branching, rebasing, and the rest are real but rarely needed for coursework flows. The risk isn’t learning too little git; it’s treating git as too complicated to engage with and therefore not committing frequently. The remedy is to commit often — many small commits are recoverable, one large commit after a week of work is a single point of failure.

```{r}
#| eval: !expr has_data
# Option 1: sort-and-assign
weather_clean_v1 <- weather_clean |>
    arrange(Month, Day) |>
    mutate(dateInYear = 1:365)
```

```{r}
#| eval: !expr has_data
# Option 2: compute from the date
weather_clean <- weather_clean |>
    mutate(dateInYear = yday(mdy(date)))
```

**Both run. Only one is safe.**

Option 1 silently assumes the dataset is exactly 365 rows, sorted cleanly, with no leap-year row, no duplicates, and no missing days. If any of those assumptions fail — a leap year adds a 366th row, a missing February day reduces to 364, a duplicate inflates to 366 — `1:365` recycles or errors depending on which. The bug is silent: the column is still produced, but day 60 is now labeled as day 61, and every downstream analysis is off by a day without warning.

Option 2 is the robust choice. `mdy()` parses the date string into a `Date` object; `yday()` extracts the day-of-year from that date. The computation uses the *content of each row*, not its *position in a sorted sequence*. It handles 365-day years, 366-day leap years, missing rows, duplicates, and out-of-order data identically — because it never depends on row order at all.

The general principle: prefer computations that derive the target value from row content over computations that derive it from row position. Position-based tricks break silently; content-based computations fail loudly or not at all.

### 4. Add 3-letter month abbreviations

```{r}
#| eval: !expr has_data
# Option A: lookup table + join
months <- tibble(
    Month      = 1:12,
    month_name = month.abb
)

weather_clean <- weather_clean |>
    left_join(months, by = "Month")
```

```{r}
#| eval: !expr has_data
# Option B: direct vector subsetting using the built-in constant
weather_clean |>
    mutate(month_name = month.abb[Month]) |>
    head()
```

Both produce identical results on this dataset. They differ in what they *generalize to*.

`month.abb` (and its sibling `month.name`) are built-in character vectors — the kind of base-R constant that is trivially available but easy to forget. Direct subsetting `month.abb[Month]` is the tightest expression when (a) the lookup table is a length-12 character vector and (b) the key is the integer 1–12. When both conditions hold, it's three tokens and zero intermediate objects.

The join approach is what generalizes. If tomorrow the lookup has to become a full-name-and-short-name-and-fiscal-quarter table, the join pattern is unchanged — only the lookup tibble grows. If the lookup has to come from a CSV, the join absorbs that too. Vector subsetting handles today's problem more elegantly; joins handle tomorrow's problem at all.

The right instinct: **start with the join pattern as the default, and drop down to vector subsetting only when the lookup is both tiny and index-keyed.**

### 5. Write out the cleaned data

```{r}
#| label: write-out
#| eval: !expr has_data
# Ensure the output directory exists before writing
dir.create("data/processed", showWarnings = FALSE, recursive = TRUE)
write_csv(weather_clean, file = "data/processed/weather_clean.csv")
```

Three observations worth naming:

- **`data/raw/` vs. `data/processed/`.** The output goes to `processed/`, not back to `raw/`. This enforces the principle that raw data is immutable — never overwritten, always the starting point. Processed derivatives live in a separate folder and can be regenerated at any time by re-running the pipeline. The convention is not cosmetic; it's what makes an analysis reproducible, because it guarantees that `raw/` is exactly what came in from the source.
- **Relative path, same logic as the read.** `data/processed/weather_clean.csv` works from any machine where the repo is cloned. The symmetry between read and write is intentional — both ends of the pipeline are tied to the repo structure, not to any particular filesystem.
- **CSV because it's durable.** Not `.rds`, not `.parquet`. A plain CSV is readable by anything — R, Python, Excel, `cat` in a terminal in 2050. For a classroom pipeline where the next step might be done in a different language or tool, CSV is the format that doesn't lock the pipeline into one stack.

## Git Verbs — A Working Mental Model

The five verbs map to three mental locations:

- **`clone`** — bring a remote repo to my local machine for the first time. Done once per repo.
- **`add`** + **`commit`** — stage and record changes *locally*. The commit is a snapshot with a message; it lives in my local `.git/` until pushed.
- **`push`** — upload my local commits to the remote (GitHub).
- **`pull`** — download commits from the remote that I don't yet have locally.

The shape: *remote ⇄ local working copy*. `clone` is the initial download; `pull` is incremental download; `push` is upload; `add` + `commit` is how I decide what becomes uploadable. The loop for any work session is `pull → work → add → commit → push`, with the `pull` at the start catching anything a collaborator (or past-me on another machine) pushed since I last worked.

Two failure modes to avoid: (1) forgetting to `pull` before starting work, which produces merge conflicts that are avoidable; (2) committing frequently locally but never pushing, which is equivalent to not backing up the work at all until something breaks.

## Keyboard Shortcuts — A Short List That Pays Back

The handful that compound over a semester:

- **`Cmd/Ctrl + Enter`** — run the current line or selection. The most-used shortcut in the editor.
- **`Cmd/Ctrl + Shift + M`** — insert the pipe `|>`. Saves three keystrokes every time I chain verbs, which is constantly.
- **`Cmd/Ctrl + Shift + Enter`** — run the entire chunk.
- **`Cmd/Ctrl + Option/Alt + I`** — insert a new code chunk in a Quarto/Rmd document.
- **`Cmd/Ctrl + Shift + C`** — toggle comment on selected lines.
- **`Cmd/Ctrl + S`** — save. Tied to Quarto render (`Cmd/Ctrl + Shift + K`).
- **`Tab`** — autocomplete object and function names. Reading documentation without typing full names is how you learn what's available in a package.

Every shortcut replaces a mouse trip. One mouse trip is not worth changing behavior for; a thousand mouse trips over a semester is the difference between the editor feeling like a barrier and feeling like an extension of thought.

## Reflection

Three things to carry forward.

**Relative paths are a reproducibility discipline, not an aesthetic preference.** An absolute path is a commitment that the code will only ever run on one machine. Every `"/Users/zhijun/..."` in a codebase is a future failure, whether it surfaces in a collaborator's IDE next week or in CI in six months. The discipline is to never let one leak in, because they are invisible in normal operation — the code works until the machine changes, and then it doesn't.

**Sort-and-assign is a silent failure waiting to happen.** The Option 1 / Option 2 split in the `dateInYear` question is the template for a whole class of bugs: any transformation that derives a value from *row position in a sorted order* rather than from *row content* is fragile to the ordering assumptions holding. When Option 2 exists (compute the target directly from the date), take it — even when Option 1 looks simpler on the happy path. The cost of the bug is not the bug itself; it's that you'll never notice it ran.

**Git's four-verb loop is almost all of day-to-day git.** `pull → work → add → commit → push`. Conflict resolution, branching, rebasing, and the rest are real but rarely needed for coursework flows. The risk isn't learning too little git; it's treating git as too complicated to engage with and therefore not committing frequently. The remedy is to commit often — many small commits are recoverable, one large commit after a week of work is a single point of failure.
