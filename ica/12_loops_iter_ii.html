🔥 Data Story Critique — Oxfam Ireland Annual Report 2019

Source: https://stories.oxfamireland.org/impact/index.html

What is the data story? — and a framing note

The piece is Oxfam Ireland’s 2019 annual report, published on Shorthand as a scrollytelling web document. Its structure is the six strategic goals of the Oxfam International confederation — Right to Be Heard, Women’s Rights, Saving Lives, Sustainable Food, Fair Sharing of Natural Resources, and Financing for Development / Universal Essential Services. Each goal gets a short section with one or two country cases (Jordan, Tanzania, Malawi, DRC, Rwanda, Mozambique, Zimbabwe), a named beneficiary’s portrait and first-person quote, and a headline “impact” number: 1.1 million people reached in Tanzania; 3,404 farmers trained; 561 women receiving land certificates; 3 million Malawians reached with gender-based-violence messages; attitudes against GBV rising from 27% to 72% among women and 21% to 98.5% among men. The piece opens and closes with a signed letter from Oxfam Ireland’s CEO, Jim Clarken.

A framing note before the critique: this is not a data story in the sense that the previous four weeks’ pieces were. It is an annual report produced for donor stewardship — it needs to make donors feel their money did meaningful work, it needs brand consistency with Oxfam International, and it needs to avoid the reputation hazards its confederation was navigating in 2018–2019. Judging it as investigative data journalism would be a category error. But because it borrows the visual grammar of data journalism (big numbers, percentage changes, “impact” language), it invites a data-critical reading — and in that reading, several structural issues common to NGO communication become visible. The critique below operates at that level: not “this piece failed as journalism,” but “here is what happens when annual-report conventions meet a visual language that implies data-analytic rigour.”

What is effective?

The six-goal scaffold gives a non-expert reader a scannable mental map. A person who doesn’t already know Oxfam’s programme taxonomy can land on the page and, within one scroll, understand the range of what the organization does — education advocacy, GBV work, disaster response, smallholder farming, land rights, health-system financing. For the genre, this is good structure.

Naming and portraiting beneficiaries is an ethical baseline Oxfam clears. Maram in Jordan, Alice in Malawi, Felista Nterevu in Tanzania, Jenipher caring for three nephews in Malawi — each is a named person, with a photograph and often a direct quote. The alternative genre convention — treating beneficiaries as “a farmer” or “a woman in the programme” — is worse on dignity grounds and worse on traceability. Oxfam does the right thing here.

The Malawi EVAWG attitude percentages are the piece’s one genuine outcome measurement. Attitudes against GBV rising from 27% to 72% among women and satisfaction with GBV services rising from 45% to 71% are before-and-after comparisons of survey responses, not activity counts. This is the right shape for an “impact” claim. (The male figure is a separate issue, discussed below.)

The CEO letter names domestic policy work — the Family Reunification Bill — rather than framing Oxfam as an exclusively overseas charity. This complicates the stereotype that NGOs are unidirectional giving vehicles, and it signals that Oxfam Ireland is also a political-advocacy organization in its home jurisdiction.

What could be improved?

The big numbers are missing denominators and definitions. “1.1 million people reached in Tanzania” — Tanzania’s population is ~61 million, so this is roughly 1.8% of the country. Is that a lot? Compared to what? And reached how — did someone read an SMS, attend a one-hour workshop, enroll in a multi-month programme? These three interpretations of “reached” have wildly different dosages. “3,404 farmers trained” — trained in what, over what period, with what retention? Basic data-journalism hygiene — exposure, denominator, operational definition — is exactly what these numbers don’t supply. Annual-report convention lets this slide because bigger and less-contextual numbers read more successfully to donors; data-literate readers should notice.

Outputs are being presented as outcomes. “3,404 farmers trained” is an output (a thing Oxfam did). The corresponding outcome would be “these farmers’ yields rose by X% over the following season, sustained over Y years.” Almost every headline number in the piece is output-shaped, but the surrounding narrative voice (“our impact is…”) frames them as outcomes. This output/outcome conflation is a structural problem in development-sector communication, not unique to this document — but it’s the single most important thing a data-critical reader should learn to identify.

The 21% → 98.5% male attitude change is measurement-suspect. A roughly 40-standard-deviation shift in a population-scale attitude measure within a single year is implausible under any normal research design. The more likely explanations are (a) the measurement instrument changed between baseline and endline, (b) the samples are not comparable, or (c) the item being measured is socially desirable under implicit pressure (e.g., “Do you agree GBV is wrong?” is a question very few respondents will answer “no” to, especially after a year of anti-GBV messaging). No sample size, no instrument description, and no methodology note accompany the number. To a data-literate reader, this specific number reads as a measurement-validity concern rather than as evidence of success.

Attribution is singular where the actual ecosystem is plural. The 561 Tanzanian women receiving land certificates — what would have happened without Oxfam’s specific intervention? Tanzania has parallel land-reform efforts from other NGOs, from UN agencies, and from state programmes. Crediting a specific outcome to a single actor in a multi-actor system is a pervasive convention in NGO reporting, but it’s not what the data can actually support.

The silence on safeguarding is the most telling absence. In February 2018 — the start of this report’s fiscal year — Oxfam GB’s 2010 Haiti operation was revealed to have involved staff paying for sex (with some cases involving minors), leading Oxfam to lose UK government funding and to have its Haiti operating permit withdrawn. This was the single defining reputation event for the Oxfam brand in 2018–2019. Oxfam Ireland can argue technically that it was not the affiliate involved, but for a public accountability document — the form of communication that most directly asks donors for trust — not mentioning a confederation-wide safeguarding crisis at all is an accountability gap larger than anything the numbers in the document claim to represent. A data-critical reading notices what is not in the document at least as much as what is.

Beneficiary voice is positioned as narrative illustration rather than authorship. Felista’s quote — “My farm is secured and will be accessed by my inheritors who are my children” — is beautiful, but it appears exactly where Oxfam chooses to place it and says what Oxfam wants heard. No beneficiary in the document is allowed to say what isn’t working, what they’d change, or what Oxfam is getting wrong. This is the well-documented “participation-as-optics” pattern in NGO communication: beneficiaries visible, beneficiaries not authoritative.

Proportionality. The critiques above are type-problems of NGO annual reports in general, not individual failings of Oxfam Ireland, and this document clears the genre’s ethical baseline (named beneficiaries, at least one real outcome measure, acknowledgement of domestic political work). The reason these problems are worth naming at all is that the document borrows the visual language of data journalism — and once you borrow that language, you inherit the responsibilities that come with it: denominators, operational definitions, outcome-versus-output discipline, methodology transparency, and honest engagement with what failed. Those responsibilities cut across genres.

Iteration Review — Column Classes via for Loop

Code
col_classes <- vector("character", length = ncol(mtcars))
names(col_classes) <- colnames(mtcars)

for (i in seq_along(mtcars)) {
    col_classes[i] <- class(mtcars[[i]])

A data frame is a list whose elements are columns, so seq_along(mtcars) iterates over columns (not rows). Naming the storage container before the loop — names(col_classes) <- colnames(mtcars) — means the output can be accessed by column name afterwards rather than by numeric position, which is almost always what I want.

Benchmark — Four Ways to Build a Vector

Code
results_nostorage <- bench::mark(
    no_storage = {
        x <- integer()
        for (i in seq_len(1e4)) x <- c(x, i)
        x
    },
    iterations = 5
)

results_storage <- bench::mark(
    with_storage = {
        x <- vector("integer", length = 1e4)
        for (i in seq_len(1e4)) x[i] <- i
        x
    },
    iterations = 5
)

results_purrr <- bench::mark(
    purrr_map = map_int(seq_len(1e4), ~ .x),
    iterations = 5
)

results_vectorized <- bench::mark(
    vectorized = seq_len(1e4),
    iterations = 5
)

bind_rows(results_nostorage, results_storage, results_purrr, results_vectorized) |>
    mutate(type = c("for_loop_no_storage", "for_loop_with_storage",
                    "purrr_map_int", "vectorized_seq_len")) |>
    select(type, median, mem_alloc, n_gc)
# A tibble: 4 × 3
  type                    median mem_alloc
  <chr>                 <bch:tm> <bch:byt>
1 for_loop_no_storage     65.2ms   191.2MB
2 for_loop_with_storage 780.39µs    48.8KB
3 purrr_map_int           3.74ms      46KB
4 vectorized_seq_len     40.98ns        0B

Reading across: vectorized_seq_len is the fastest and allocates the least memory; for_loop_with_storage is in the same ballpark as purrr_map_int; for_loop_no_storage is roughly two orders of magnitude slower and allocates dramatically more memory because every c(x, i) copies the entire growing vector. The n_gc (garbage-collection) column tells the same story from the memory-pressure side.

Conceptual point. purrr::map_*() is not faster than a pre-allocated for loop — both are running roughly the same work under the hood. What purrr buys you is not speed but readability and type safety: map_int fails loudly if any element isn’t an integer, whereas an unchecked loop can silently produce garbage.

map_*() — Summary Statistics Per Variable

summ_stats() helper

Code
summ_stats <- function(x) {
    tibble(
        mean   = mean(x, na.rm = TRUE),
        median = median(x, na.rm = TRUE),
        sd     = sd(x, na.rm = TRUE),
        iqr    = IQR(x, na.rm = TRUE)
    )
}

# Single-variable sanity check
summ_stats(diamonds$carat)

The .id = "variable" trick is the cleanest way to recover column names: map() over a data frame returns a named list (element names inherited from the data frame’s column names), and bind_rows(.id = ...) promotes those names into a new column.

For this kind of element-wise iteration the map() form wins on readability; the for loop only starts to earn its keep when iteration carries state between steps.

pmap() — Multi-Argument Iteration

Two small str_sub() examples

Code
# Example 1: vary string, start, and end simultaneously
varying_all <- tibble(
    string = c("apple", "banana", "cherry"),
    start  = c(1, 2, 4),
    end    = c(2, 3, 5)
)

pmap_chr(varying_all, str_sub)
[1] "ap" "an" "rr"
Code
# Example 2: vary start and end, hold string constant
varying_positions <- tibble(
    start = c(1, 2, 4),
    end   = c(2, 3, 5)
)

pmap_chr(varying_positions, str_sub, string = "abcde")
[1] "ab" "bc" "de"

Two design points from these:

  • pmap matches tibble column names to function argument names. The column names in varying_all match str_sub’s argument names (string, start, end) exactly, which is why it works.
  • Arguments that should be held constant across iterations are passed as extra named arguments after the function. In Example 2, string = "abcde" is fixed; only start and end vary.

pmap() case study — many model specifications on diamonds

This is the ICA 11 case study redone with pmap, avoiding the explicit for loop. I’m also carrying over two fixes from ICA 11: the parameter drop_outliers rather than remove_outliers (to avoid shadowing the function of the same name), and abs(.zscore) <= sd_thresh for a symmetric outlier definition.

Code
# Helper: remove outliers on `what_var` beyond sd_thresh SDs from the mean
remove_outliers <- function(data, what_var, sd_thresh) {
    data |>
        mutate(
            .zscore = ({{ what_var }} - mean({{ what_var }}, na.rm = TRUE)) /
                       sd({{ what_var }}, na.rm = TRUE)
        ) |>
        filter(abs(.zscore) <= sd_thresh) |>
        select(-.zscore)
}

# Dispatcher: outer parameter renamed to `drop_outliers` to avoid name collision
fit_model <- function(data, mod_formula, drop_outliers) {
    if (drop_outliers) {
        data <- remove_outliers(data, what_var = carat, sd_thresh = 3)
    }
    lm(as.formula(mod_formula), data = data)
}

walk2() — Saving One Plot Per Group

Code
# Write to a temporary directory so this chunk doesn't pollute the working dir
out_dir <- file.path(tempdir(), "diamond_plots")
dir.create(out_dir, showWarnings = FALSE)

# Build one dataset per clarity level, one filepath per dataset, one plot per dataset
by_clarity <- diamonds |>
    group_nest(clarity) |>
    mutate(
        path = file.path(out_dir, str_glue("diamonds-{clarity}.png")),
        # map2 passes both the clarity label and the nested data frame
        plot = map2(clarity, data, \(cl, df) {
            ggplot(df, aes(x = carat, y = price)) +
                geom_point(alpha = 0.3) +
                labs(
                    x = "Carat", y = "Price (USD)",
                    title = str_glue("Price vs. carat — clarity {cl}")
                ) +
                theme_minimal()
        })
    )

# walk2() iterates over two parallel vectors for side effects only
walk2(
    by_clarity$path,
    by_clarity$plot,
    \(p, g) ggsave(p, g, width = 6, height = 6, dpi = 100)
)

# Confirm the files exist
list.files(out_dir)
[1] "diamonds-I1.png"   "diamonds-IF.png"   "diamonds-SI1.png" 
[4] "diamonds-SI2.png"  "diamonds-VS1.png"  "diamonds-VS2.png" 
[7] "diamonds-VVS1.png" "diamonds-VVS2.png"

Three notes on this pattern:

  • walk2() vs map2(). They do the same iteration; the difference is that walk2() returns the input invisibly and is intended for side-effect functions like ggsave(), write_csv(), or cat(). Using map2() here would work but would leave you with a list of NULLs (what ggsave returns), which is not what you want.
  • group_nest() is the right preparation step. It produces a tibble where each row is one group and one column is a nested data column holding that group’s rows. From there, a mutate(plot = map(data, ...)) builds one plot per row, and walk2() writes them out. The whole pattern — group-nest, list-column transform, walk-to-disk — is the purrr-era replacement for the for loop that used to do this job.
  • tempdir() hygiene. Writing to tempdir() means this chunk leaves no artifacts in my working directory. For a real use case I’d point out_dir at here::here("figures") or similar; the pattern is identical.

Reflection

purrr’s payoff is not speed — it’s readability and type safety. The benchmarks at the top of this document make this explicit: map_int() is roughly tied with a pre-allocated for loop on raw performance. What purrr buys is the _int, _chr, _dbl, _lgl suffix — the function fails loudly if the return type doesn’t match, instead of letting the error propagate silently. In a pipeline that feeds downstream analysis, failing loudly at the place the error happens is worth more than a few milliseconds. This is the same principle as the input validation in ICA 10’s fit_mod_and_extract(): explicit contract, enforced early.

The hard transition in this material is from “element-wise iteration” to “multi-argument iteration.” map() is the easy case — one vector in, one vector out. pmap() is the one that unlocks everything interesting: it takes a data frame where each row is a configuration, matches column names to function arguments, and applies a function once per row. That is the same skeleton as grid-search hyperparameter tuning in scikit-learn, or as a Stata robustness-check loop over model specifications, or as a sensitivity analysis in a causal-inference paper. crossing() + pmap() is the R version of that skeleton. Once you see this, most of the “repeated analysis” work you’ll do in empirical research collapses into one pattern.

walk’s separation of computation from side effects is the same insight functional programming has been making for decades. A map() is safe to retry, safe to parallelize, safe to reason about because it has no effects outside its return value. A walk() is explicitly for the part of the pipeline that does interact with the world — files, databases, plots, prints. Keeping these two separated — computing first, writing after — is how pipelines stay debuggable when they scale.

The piece is Oxfam Ireland's **2019 annual report**, published on Shorthand as a scrollytelling web document. Its structure is the six strategic goals of the Oxfam International confederation — Right to Be Heard, Women's Rights, Saving Lives, Sustainable Food, Fair Sharing of Natural Resources, and Financing for Development / Universal Essential Services. Each goal gets a short section with one or two country cases (Jordan, Tanzania, Malawi, DRC, Rwanda, Mozambique, Zimbabwe), a named beneficiary's portrait and first-person quote, and a headline "impact" number: *1.1 million people reached in Tanzania; 3,404 farmers trained; 561 women receiving land certificates; 3 million Malawians reached with gender-based-violence messages; attitudes against GBV rising from 27% to 72% among women and 21% to 98.5% among men.* The piece opens and closes with a signed letter from Oxfam Ireland's CEO, Jim Clarken.

A framing note before the critique: **this is not a data story in the sense that the previous four weeks' pieces were**. It is an annual report produced for donor stewardship — it needs to make donors feel their money did meaningful work, it needs brand consistency with Oxfam International, and it needs to avoid the reputation hazards its confederation was navigating in 2018–2019. Judging it as investigative data journalism would be a category error. But because it borrows the *visual grammar* of data journalism (big numbers, percentage changes, "impact" language), it invites a data-critical reading — and in that reading, several structural issues common to NGO communication become visible. The critique below operates at that level: not "this piece failed as journalism," but "here is what happens when annual-report conventions meet a visual language that implies data-analytic rigour."

### What is effective?

**The six-goal scaffold gives a non-expert reader a scannable mental map.** A person who doesn't already know Oxfam's programme taxonomy can land on the page and, within one scroll, understand the range of what the organization does — education advocacy, GBV work, disaster response, smallholder farming, land rights, health-system financing. For the genre, this is good structure.

**Naming and portraiting beneficiaries is an ethical baseline Oxfam clears.** Maram in Jordan, Alice in Malawi, Felista Nterevu in Tanzania, Jenipher caring for three nephews in Malawi — each is a named person, with a photograph and often a direct quote. The alternative genre convention — treating beneficiaries as "a farmer" or "a woman in the programme" — is worse on dignity grounds and worse on traceability. Oxfam does the right thing here.

**The Malawi EVAWG attitude percentages are the piece's one genuine outcome measurement.** *Attitudes against GBV rising from 27% to 72% among women* and *satisfaction with GBV services rising from 45% to 71%* are before-and-after comparisons of survey responses, not activity counts. This is the right shape for an "impact" claim. (The male figure is a separate issue, discussed below.)

**The CEO letter names domestic policy work — the Family Reunification Bill — rather than framing Oxfam as an exclusively overseas charity.** This complicates the stereotype that NGOs are unidirectional giving vehicles, and it signals that Oxfam Ireland is also a political-advocacy organization in its home jurisdiction.

### What could be improved?

**The big numbers are missing denominators and definitions.** *"1.1 million people reached in Tanzania"* — Tanzania's population is ~61 million, so this is roughly 1.8% of the country. Is that a lot? Compared to what? And *reached how* — did someone read an SMS, attend a one-hour workshop, enroll in a multi-month programme? These three interpretations of "reached" have wildly different dosages. *"3,404 farmers trained"* — trained in what, over what period, with what retention? Basic data-journalism hygiene — exposure, denominator, operational definition — is exactly what these numbers don't supply. Annual-report convention lets this slide because bigger and less-contextual numbers read more successfully to donors; data-literate readers should notice.

**Outputs are being presented as outcomes.** *"3,404 farmers trained"* is an output (a thing Oxfam did). The corresponding outcome would be *"these farmers' yields rose by X% over the following season, sustained over Y years."* Almost every headline number in the piece is output-shaped, but the surrounding narrative voice ("our impact is...") frames them as outcomes. This output/outcome conflation is a structural problem in development-sector communication, not unique to this document — but it's the single most important thing a data-critical reader should learn to identify.

**The 21% → 98.5% male attitude change is measurement-suspect.** A roughly 40-standard-deviation shift in a population-scale attitude measure within a single year is implausible under any normal research design. The more likely explanations are (a) the measurement instrument changed between baseline and endline, (b) the samples are not comparable, or (c) the item being measured is socially desirable under implicit pressure (e.g., "Do you agree GBV is wrong?" is a question very few respondents will answer "no" to, especially after a year of anti-GBV messaging). No sample size, no instrument description, and no methodology note accompany the number. To a data-literate reader, this specific number reads as a measurement-validity concern rather than as evidence of success.

**Attribution is singular where the actual ecosystem is plural.** The 561 Tanzanian women receiving land certificates — what would have happened without Oxfam's specific intervention? Tanzania has parallel land-reform efforts from other NGOs, from UN agencies, and from state programmes. Crediting a specific outcome to a single actor in a multi-actor system is a pervasive convention in NGO reporting, but it's not what the data can actually support.

**The silence on safeguarding is the most telling absence.** In February 2018 — the start of this report's fiscal year — Oxfam GB's 2010 Haiti operation was revealed to have involved staff paying for sex (with some cases involving minors), leading Oxfam to lose UK government funding and to have its Haiti operating permit withdrawn. This was the single defining reputation event for the Oxfam brand in 2018–2019. Oxfam Ireland can argue technically that it was not the affiliate involved, but for a public accountability document — the form of communication that most directly asks donors for trust — **not mentioning a confederation-wide safeguarding crisis at all** is an accountability gap larger than anything the numbers in the document claim to represent. A data-critical reading notices what is *not* in the document at least as much as what is.

**Beneficiary voice is positioned as narrative illustration rather than authorship.** Felista's quote — *"My farm is secured and will be accessed by my inheritors who are my children"* — is beautiful, but it appears exactly where Oxfam chooses to place it and says what Oxfam wants heard. No beneficiary in the document is allowed to say what isn't working, what they'd change, or what Oxfam is getting wrong. This is the well-documented "participation-as-optics" pattern in NGO communication: beneficiaries visible, beneficiaries not authoritative.

**Proportionality.** The critiques above are type-problems of NGO annual reports in general, not individual failings of Oxfam Ireland, and this document clears the genre's ethical baseline (named beneficiaries, at least one real outcome measure, acknowledgement of domestic political work). The reason these problems are worth naming at all is that the document borrows the *visual language* of data journalism — and once you borrow that language, you inherit the responsibilities that come with it: denominators, operational definitions, outcome-versus-output discipline, methodology transparency, and honest engagement with what failed. Those responsibilities cut across genres.

## Iteration Review — Column Classes via `for` Loop

```{r}
col_classes <- vector("character", length = ncol(mtcars))
names(col_classes) <- colnames(mtcars)

for (i in seq_along(mtcars)) {
    col_classes[i] <- class(mtcars[[i]])
}

col_classes
```

A data frame is a list whose elements are columns, so `seq_along(mtcars)` iterates over columns (not rows). Naming the storage container before the loop — `names(col_classes) <- colnames(mtcars)` — means the output can be accessed by column name afterwards rather than by numeric position, which is almost always what I want.

## Benchmark — Four Ways to Build a Vector

```{r}
#| cache: true
#| label: vector-benchmarks

results_nostorage <- bench::mark(
    no_storage = {
        x <- integer()
        for (i in seq_len(1e4)) x <- c(x, i)
        x
    },
    iterations = 5
)

results_storage <- bench::mark(
    with_storage = {
        x <- vector("integer", length = 1e4)
        for (i in seq_len(1e4)) x[i] <- i
        x
    },
    iterations = 5
)

results_purrr <- bench::mark(
    purrr_map = map_int(seq_len(1e4), ~ .x),
    iterations = 5
)

results_vectorized <- bench::mark(
    vectorized = seq_len(1e4),
    iterations = 5
)

bind_rows(results_nostorage, results_storage, results_purrr, results_vectorized) |>
    mutate(type = c("for_loop_no_storage", "for_loop_with_storage",
                    "purrr_map_int", "vectorized_seq_len")) |>
    select(type, median, mem_alloc, n_gc)
```

Reading across: `vectorized_seq_len` is the fastest and allocates the least memory; `for_loop_with_storage` is in the same ballpark as `purrr_map_int`; `for_loop_no_storage` is roughly two orders of magnitude slower and allocates dramatically more memory because every `c(x, i)` copies the entire growing vector. The `n_gc` (garbage-collection) column tells the same story from the memory-pressure side.

**Conceptual point.** `purrr::map_*()` is not faster than a pre-allocated `for` loop — both are running roughly the same work under the hood. What `purrr` buys you is not speed but *readability* and *type safety*: `map_int` fails loudly if any element isn't an integer, whereas an unchecked loop can silently produce garbage.

## `map_*()` — Summary Statistics Per Variable

### `summ_stats()` helper

```{r}
summ_stats <- function(x) {
    tibble(
        mean   = mean(x, na.rm = TRUE),
        median = median(x, na.rm = TRUE),
        sd     = sd(x, na.rm = TRUE),
        iqr    = IQR(x, na.rm = TRUE)
    )
}

# Single-variable sanity check
summ_stats(diamonds$carat)
```

### Applied across all numeric variables in `diamonds` — with `map()` + `bind_rows()`

```{r}
diamonds_num <- diamonds |> select(where(is.numeric))

diamonds_summary <- diamonds_num |>
    map(summ_stats) |>
    bind_rows(.id = "variable")

diamonds_summary
```

The `.id = "variable"` trick is the cleanest way to recover column names: `map()` over a data frame returns a *named* list (element names inherited from the data frame's column names), and `bind_rows(.id = ...)` promotes those names into a new column.

### The same thing with a `for` loop — for comparison

```{r}
n_cols <- ncol(diamonds_num)
summ_list <- vector("list", length = n_cols)

for (i in seq_len(n_cols)) {
    summ_list[[i]] <- summ_stats(diamonds_num[[i]])
}

names(summ_list) <- colnames(diamonds_num)
bind_rows(summ_list, .id = "variable")
```

Same result. The `map()` version is one line shorter, doesn't require explicit storage allocation, and the return-type contract (a list of tibbles, combined into one) is visible from the pipeline syntax. For this kind of *element-wise* iteration the `map()` form wins on readability; the `for` loop only starts to earn its keep when iteration carries state between steps.

## `pmap()` — Multi-Argument Iteration

### Two small `str_sub()` examples

```{r}
# Example 1: vary string, start, and end simultaneously
varying_all <- tibble(
    string = c("apple", "banana", "cherry"),
    start  = c(1, 2, 4),
    end    = c(2, 3, 5)
)

pmap_chr(varying_all, str_sub)
```

```{r}
# Example 2: vary start and end, hold string constant
varying_positions <- tibble(
    start = c(1, 2, 4),
    end   = c(2, 3, 5)
)

pmap_chr(varying_positions, str_sub, string = "abcde")
```

Two design points from these:

- `pmap` matches tibble column names to function argument names. The column names in `varying_all` match `str_sub`'s argument names (`string`, `start`, `end`) exactly, which is why it works.
- Arguments that should be held constant across iterations are passed as extra named arguments *after* the function. In Example 2, `string = "abcde"` is fixed; only `start` and `end` vary.

### `pmap()` case study — many model specifications on `diamonds`

This is the ICA 11 case study redone with `pmap`, avoiding the explicit `for` loop. I'm also carrying over two fixes from ICA 11: the parameter `drop_outliers` rather than `remove_outliers` (to avoid shadowing the function of the same name), and `abs(.zscore) <= sd_thresh` for a symmetric outlier definition.

```{r}
# Helper: remove outliers on `what_var` beyond sd_thresh SDs from the mean
remove_outliers <- function(data, what_var, sd_thresh) {
    data |>
        mutate(
            .zscore = ({{ what_var }} - mean({{ what_var }}, na.rm = TRUE)) /
                       sd({{ what_var }}, na.rm = TRUE)
        ) |>
        filter(abs(.zscore) <= sd_thresh) |>
        select(-.zscore)
}

# Dispatcher: outer parameter renamed to `drop_outliers` to avoid name collision
fit_model <- function(data, mod_formula, drop_outliers) {
    if (drop_outliers) {
        data <- remove_outliers(data, what_var = carat, sd_thresh = 3)
    }
    lm(as.formula(mod_formula), data = data)
}
```

Build the argument grid. The template builds nested formulas via `map_chr()` — I keep that pattern because it generalises to an arbitrary predictor list:

```{r}
xs <- diamonds |> select(carat, cut, clarity, color) |> names()

df_arg_combos <- crossing(
    mod_formula = str_c(
        "price ~ ",
        map_chr(seq_along(xs), ~ str_c(xs[1:.x], collapse = " + "))
    ),
    drop_outliers = c(TRUE, FALSE)
)

df_arg_combos
```

Now fit all eight specifications in one `pmap()` call, storing the results in a list-column alongside the configuration that produced them:

```{r}
fitted <- df_arg_combos |>
    mutate(
        mod = pmap(
            list(mod_formula = mod_formula, drop_outliers = drop_outliers),
            fit_model,
            data = diamonds
        )
    )

fitted
```

The `list(mod_formula = ..., drop_outliers = ...)` inside `pmap()` is explicit about which columns feed which arguments — safer than relying on column-name-to-argument-name matching when the tibble has additional columns you don't want passed. `data = diamonds` is the fixed argument carried through every iteration.

### Extract a tidy comparison table

A list-column of `lm` objects isn't useful until we pull out comparable statistics:

```{r}
fitted |>
    mutate(
        n_used        = map_int(mod, ~ length(residuals(.x))),
        r_squared     = map_dbl(mod, ~ summary(.x)$r.squared),
        adj_r_squared = map_dbl(mod, ~ summary(.x)$adj.r.squared),
        aic           = map_dbl(mod, AIC)
    ) |>
    select(-mod) |>
    arrange(desc(adj_r_squared))
```

The full `price ~ carat + cut + clarity + color` specification on outlier-dropped data wins on adjusted R² — at the cost of being trained on a slightly different dataset than the one it would be applied to. The `crossing() + pmap() + map_*() extraction` pattern scales: you could add a third axis to `crossing()` (say, a handful of transformations on `carat`), go from 8 specifications to 24, and the rest of the pipeline would need zero changes.

## `walk2()` — Saving One Plot Per Group

```{r}
#| label: walk-plot-saving
# Write to a temporary directory so this chunk doesn't pollute the working dir
out_dir <- file.path(tempdir(), "diamond_plots")
dir.create(out_dir, showWarnings = FALSE)

# Build one dataset per clarity level, one filepath per dataset, one plot per dataset
by_clarity <- diamonds |>
    group_nest(clarity) |>
    mutate(
        path = file.path(out_dir, str_glue("diamonds-{clarity}.png")),
        # map2 passes both the clarity label and the nested data frame
        plot = map2(clarity, data, \(cl, df) {
            ggplot(df, aes(x = carat, y = price)) +
                geom_point(alpha = 0.3) +
                labs(
                    x = "Carat", y = "Price (USD)",
                    title = str_glue("Price vs. carat — clarity {cl}")
                ) +
                theme_minimal()
        })
    )

# walk2() iterates over two parallel vectors for side effects only
walk2(
    by_clarity$path,
    by_clarity$plot,
    \(p, g) ggsave(p, g, width = 6, height = 6, dpi = 100)
)

# Confirm the files exist
list.files(out_dir)
```

Three notes on this pattern:

- **`walk2()` vs `map2()`.** They do the same iteration; the difference is that `walk2()` returns the input invisibly and is intended for side-effect functions like `ggsave()`, `write_csv()`, or `cat()`. Using `map2()` here would work but would leave you with a list of `NULL`s (what `ggsave` returns), which is not what you want.
- **`group_nest()` is the right preparation step.** It produces a tibble where each row is one group and one column is a nested `data` column holding that group's rows. From there, a `mutate(plot = map(data, ...))` builds one plot per row, and `walk2()` writes them out. The whole pattern — group-nest, list-column transform, walk-to-disk — is the purrr-era replacement for the `for` loop that used to do this job.
- **`tempdir()` hygiene.** Writing to `tempdir()` means this chunk leaves no artifacts in my working directory. For a real use case I'd point `out_dir` at `here::here("figures")` or similar; the pattern is identical.

## Reflection

**`purrr`'s payoff is not speed — it's readability and type safety.** The benchmarks at the top of this document make this explicit: `map_int()` is roughly tied with a pre-allocated `for` loop on raw performance. What `purrr` buys is the `_int`, `_chr`, `_dbl`, `_lgl` suffix — the function fails loudly if the return type doesn't match, instead of letting the error propagate silently. In a pipeline that feeds downstream analysis, failing loudly at the place the error happens is worth more than a few milliseconds. This is the same principle as the input validation in ICA 10's `fit_mod_and_extract()`: explicit contract, enforced early.

**The hard transition in this material is from "element-wise iteration" to "multi-argument iteration."** `map()` is the easy case — one vector in, one vector out. `pmap()` is the one that unlocks everything interesting: it takes a data frame where each row is a configuration, matches column names to function arguments, and applies a function once per row. That is the same skeleton as grid-search hyperparameter tuning in scikit-learn, or as a Stata robustness-check loop over model specifications, or as a sensitivity analysis in a causal-inference paper. `crossing() + pmap()` is the R version of that skeleton. Once you see this, most of the "repeated analysis" work you'll do in empirical research collapses into one pattern.

**`walk`'s separation of computation from side effects is the same insight functional programming has been making for decades.** A `map()` is safe to retry, safe to parallelize, safe to reason about because it has no effects outside its return value. A `walk()` is explicitly for the part of the pipeline that *does* interact with the world — files, databases, plots, prints. Keeping these two separated — computing first, writing after — is how pipelines stay debuggable when they scale.
