🔥 Data Story Critique — SDG Index Dashboards (SDSN / Bertelsmann Stiftung)

Source: https://dashboards.sdgindex.org/map/goals/SDG4/

Producers: Sustainable Development Solutions Network (SDSN) and Bertelsmann Stiftung, with SDSN under Jeffrey Sachs at Columbia. The flagship annual product is the Sustainable Development Report (previously the SDG Index and Dashboards Report); the dashboard is its interactive companion.

What is the data story?

The dashboard is the best-known non-UN monitoring instrument for the Sustainable Development Goals — a global choropleth framework that scores all 193 UN member states across the 17 SDGs plus an overall composite index. The linked URL drops the reader directly into the SDG 4 (Quality Education) map view: a world choropleth in four colours (green / yellow / orange / red, plus grey for “data not available”), a trend arrow per country indicating whether progress is on-track / improving / stagnating / decreasing, and a sidebar panel exposing the indicator composition (primary completion, secondary completion, literacy rate, tertiary attainment, etc.). Each country page drills into the contributing indicators and their most recent values. The same pattern holds across all 17 goals. The story it tells, across the SDG system as a whole, is where the world stands — which countries are achieving, which are struggling, where the trend is bending in the right direction, and where it is stagnating with four years left on the 2030 deadline.

What is effective?

Choropleth is the correct first view for a problem of this dimensionality. The underlying data cube is 17 goals × 193 countries × 230+ indicators × 2015–current timeline. Any single view collapses several of those dimensions; a world map with colour-coded countries collapses “which country, at what level, on which goal” into a two-second scan. The reader can locate themselves, locate countries they care about, and see the overall spatial pattern without reading a table. A table of 193 rows or a 17×193 heatmap would fail this test.

The dual-encoding of level (colour) and trend (arrow) is a genuinely good design decision. These are two orthogonal pieces of information — where a country currently stands, and which direction it is moving — and using a single channel would lose one of them. The encoding surfaces the Green-but-stalling and Red-but-improving cases that single-dimension visualizations tend to blur. Ethiopia on SDG 4 is the classic example of the latter profile (low score, strong improvement trend); showing both avoids a misread in either direction.

Methodology transparency is higher than most comparable products. SDSN publishes a detailed methodology chapter with the Annual Sustainable Development Report that documents every indicator’s normalization formula, the handling of missing values, confidence bounds, and the inclusion criteria. The SDG Transformation Center hosts downloadable data and a full technical note. Most public users will not audit this, but users who want to can, which is the appropriate institutional posture.

Indicator composition is exposed per goal. Clicking into SDG 4 shows which indicators combine to produce the goal score, with weights, normalization bounds, and the year of the most recent data point. This is more than many composite indices offer — the World Bank’s Human Capital Index and similar composites frequently do not expose weights publicly — and it is the right default for an instrument that will be cited in policy debates.

What could be improved?

The composite structure masks failures through arithmetic. The overall SDG Index is the arithmetic mean of 17 goal scores; each goal score is the mean of its indicators. Flat averaging has well-known problems — a country that scores at ceiling on SDG 8 (economic growth) effectively earns credit that offsets a failure on SDG 13 (climate action), because both enter the mean with equal weight. This is the canonical Sen–Stiglitz critique of composite indices, and the design choice to use arithmetic mean rather than a penalty function like geometric mean (which is more sensitive to low scores) is a methodological decision that shapes every ranking downstream. It is defensible, but it belongs upfront in the dashboard UI, not buried in the methodology paper. A reader looking at a country’s “overall progress” has no way to see, from the map view, whether that score is an even average or an aggregate that hides a failing component.

Country-level aggregation is blind to within-country inequality — which is exactly what “development” as a concept is supposed to track. The US national score on SDG 3 (health) hides a Black–white life-expectancy gap of several years. China’s SDG 1 score hides the material differences between Xinjiang and Shenzhen. India’s SDG 4 score hides the order-of-magnitude gap between Kerala and Bihar education systems. The dashboard displays the who of national averages, while the animating moral question of the SDG framework is the distribution beneath those averages. SDG 10 (Reduce Inequality) exists as a goal, which partly addresses this — but framing inequality as “one of 17 goals” rather than as a cross-cutting dimension of every other goal is itself a framework-level decision that the dashboard inherits from the UN SDG agenda and does not interrogate. A more ambitious dashboard would surface sub-national data where it exists (the Human Development Report does this for some indicators).

Data lag is invisible in the current-status UI. Many SDG 4 indicators in low-income countries have latest available data from DHS/MICS surveys conducted in 2015–2019. The dashboard presents scores as current state without displaying per-indicator data years in the hover tooltip. A reader in 2026 sees a country coloured orange and assumes the colour reflects 2025 reality; it may reflect the most recent available observation from 2018. A per-indicator data-year label in the tooltip, or a transparency channel encoding recency on the map, would be a low-cost honesty fix.

Assessment against SDG targets naturalizes a political artefact. The 2030 deadline and the specific numerical targets (“by 2030, ensure all girls and boys complete free, equitable, and quality primary and secondary education”) are the outputs of a 2015 UN negotiation — political compromises among member states, not independent scientific benchmarks. The dashboard translates distance-from-target into technical-looking assessment language (“on track,” “challenges remain”) that launders the political origin of the targets themselves. A country rated “on track” on SDG 4 is on track relative to a politically negotiated target; whether that target is adequate is a separate question that the dashboard’s framing forecloses. As 2030 approaches and most goals are visibly missing their targets, the dashboard does not have a framework for asking whether the targets themselves need to be revised — it just continues scoring against them.

The grey “Not Available” countries carry political information the UI flattens. North Korea, Somalia, Eritrea, parts of conflict-affected Sub-Saharan Africa — the countries where data is most absent are not randomly distributed. They are the countries where state capacity is weakest, where conflict has disrupted statistical infrastructure, or where governments have chosen not to participate in international reporting regimes. These are frequently also the places where development outcomes are most consequential. Rendering them as grey is an honest acknowledgement of the data gap, but it positions them as absent from the conversation rather than as cases where the absence of data is itself a finding. A dashboard committed to equity framing would name this explicitly: the countries most in need of developmental attention are also the countries least visible in the global measurement apparatus.

The interactive-vs-narrative trade-off has tipped the wrong way for the dashboard’s most common users. The Annual Sustainable Development Report PDF provides narrative context — structural drivers of progress or regression, cross-cutting analysis, country case studies — that the dashboard strips away in favour of numbers and colours. Policy users who treat the dashboard as their primary reference (which increasingly they do — UN agency briefings, government strategy documents, and media reports cite SDG Index rankings directly) are consuming interpretation-free data. The pre-class reading for this ICA (the Medium piece on “the death of interactive infographics”) makes this exact point about interactive journalism: most users don’t actually explore; they want guided interpretation. SDSN has that interpretation in the report. The dashboard doesn’t import enough of it.

Proportionality. SDSN / Bertelsmann produce one of the most methodologically serious composite-index products in the field, and the critique above is not about analytical capacity. It is about editorial choices specific to the dashboard medium — composition opacity, within-country blindness, data-lag invisibility, target naturalization, grey-country invisibility, stripped narrative — that the annual PDF report either addresses or has room to address. The drift the dashboard is under is that it is being cited as an authoritative source in contexts where the caveats that live in the report are invisible. That drift is the institutional problem worth naming.

Reflection — Kyle Walker’s Neighborhood Diversity App

The app’s core layout is a sidebar-main split: sidebar holds user inputs (city dropdown, span slider), main holds three linked outputs — a scatterplot of diversity score versus distance to city hall, a choropleth map of diversity by census tract, and a stacked bar of the city’s overall racial composition. Selecting a different city updates all three plots simultaneously; adjusting the span slider re-smooths the scatterplot without touching the other two.

Is the interactivity necessary? Yes, and for a specific reason: the analytical question here is how diversity varies within and across US metros, and there are ~70 metros of interest. A static panel would need roughly seventy small-multiple scatterplots plus seventy maps plus seventy bars — well over two hundred subplots — which would defeat comparison. The interactive version lets the reader perform the comparison one metro at a time and choose which comparisons matter to them. This is the canonical case where interactivity earns its place: many possible slices, only a few of interest to any given reader, and no single static view can serve them all.

What comparisons would I want that the app doesn’t support? A side-by-side view of two metros would be the first addition — right now the reader switches city and loses the previous view, forcing them to hold the previous metro in working memory. A time dimension (the data_by_year dataset suggests this is in the data but not in this app view) would let the reader see whether a metro’s diversity profile is changing. And a brush-and-link between the map and the scatterplot — clicking a census tract on the map highlights its point on the scatter, or vice versa — would make the distance-to-city-hall story legible per-tract, not just in aggregate.

The Shiny App Structure

A Shiny app has three logical components:

  1. ui — what the user sees. Nested functions for page layout, input controls, and output placeholders.
  2. server — what the app does in response to user input. A function that takes input and output objects and defines render*() functions that tie them together.
  3. shinyApp(ui, server) — launches the app.

The design contract between ui and server is that every inputId in the ui becomes input$that_id in the server, and every outputId in the ui is populated by assignment to output$that_id in the server. Get the IDs right and the data flows; get them wrong and the app runs but shows nothing.

Complete app.R for the neighborhood-diversity app

The following is what would go in neighborhood_diversity/app.R. It is not evaluated in this QMD — Shiny requires a live R process, not a rendered HTML — but running shiny::runApp("neighborhood_diversity/") in the RStudio console will launch it. The two data files (diverse_data_by_dist.rds and diverse_data_by_year.csv) need to sit in neighborhood_diversity/data/.

# neighborhood_diversity/app.R
library(shiny)
library(tidyverse)
library(sf)
library(plotly)

# ---- Data ----
data_by_dist <- read_rds("data/diverse_data_by_dist.rds")
data_by_year <- read_csv("data/diverse_data_by_year.csv")

metro_names <- data_by_dist |> pull(metro_name) |> unique() |> sort()

# ---- UI ----
ui <- fluidPage(
    titlePanel("Neighborhood Diversity Across US Metros"),

    sidebarLayout(
        sidebarPanel(
            selectInput(
                inputId  = "metro",
                label    = "Metropolitan area:",
                choices  = metro_names,
                selected = "Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington, MN-WI"
            ),
            sliderInput(
                inputId = "span",
                label   = "Smoothing span:",
                min     = 0.1,
                max     = 1.0,
                value   = 0.5,
                step    = 0.1
            )
        ),

        mainPanel(
            plotlyOutput("scatter_plot"),
            plotOutput("map_plot"),
            plotlyOutput("bar_plot")
        )
    )
)

# ---- Server ----
server <- function(input, output) {

    # Reactive: city-filtered dataset, re-computed whenever `metro` changes
    metro_data <- reactive({
        data_by_dist |> filter(metro_name == input$metro)
    })

    # Scatterplot — diversity entropy vs. distance to city hall
    output$scatter_plot <- renderPlotly({
        p <- ggplot(metro_data(), aes(x = distmiles, y = entropy)) +
            geom_point(alpha = 0.4) +
            geom_smooth(span = input$span, se = FALSE, colour = "#b6345c") +
            labs(
                x = "Distance to city hall (miles)",
                y = "Diversity entropy",
                title = str_glue("Diversity vs. distance — {input$metro}")
            ) +
            theme_minimal()
        ggplotly(p)
    })

    # Map — choropleth of entropy by census tract
    output$map_plot <- renderPlot({
        ggplot(metro_data()) +
            geom_sf(aes(fill = entropy), colour = NA) +
            scale_fill_viridis_c(option = "magma") +
            labs(fill = "Entropy",
                 title = str_glue("Diversity by tract — {input$metro}")) +
            theme_void()
    })

    # Bar chart — total population by race in the selected metro
    output$bar_plot <- renderPlotly({
        race_totals <- metro_data() |>
            st_drop_geometry() |>
            summarise(across(c(aian, asian, black, hispanic,
                               two_or_more, white), sum, na.rm = TRUE)) |>
            pivot_longer(everything(), names_to = "race", values_to = "n") |>
            arrange(desc(n))

        p <- ggplot(race_totals, aes(x = fct_reorder(race, n), y = n)) +
            geom_col(fill = "#3b7a9b") +
            coord_flip() +
            labs(x = NULL, y = "Population",
                 title = str_glue("Racial composition — {input$metro}")) +
            theme_minimal()
        ggplotly(p)
    })
}

# ---- Launch ----
shinyApp(ui, server)

Annotated design choices

  • reactive({ ... }) for the filtered dataset. The filtering happens once per metro change, and all three plots read from the same reactive. Without it, every plot would re-run the filter independently, and a slow filter step would get executed three times per interaction.
  • plotlyOutput() and renderPlotly() for the scatter and bar, but plotOutput() and renderPlot() for the map. The scatter and bar benefit substantially from hover tooltips and zoom — plotly adds those for free on top of ggplot. The sf map is more finicky under ggplotly() (coordinate reference systems don’t always survive the translation); keeping it as a static ggplot is the safer choice.
  • sort() on the metro names. The dropdown gets long; sorting alphabetically is a user-comfort decision.
  • selected = ... picks a default that makes the app useful on first load (Minneapolis–St. Paul — and if I’m honest, a small nod to the Twin Cities as home to both Macalester and Saint Paul).

Publishing to shinyapps.io

Once the app runs locally, deployment follows three steps:

  1. Install rsconnect, register at shinyapps.io, and paste the connection token into the R console.
  2. From RStudio, open app.R and click the blue “Publish” icon in the top-right of the source pane.
  3. Confirm the files to include (everything in the neighborhood_diversity/ folder, including the data/ subdirectory) and wait for the first-time deploy to complete.

The free tier allows five apps with 25 active hours per month — enough for coursework and portfolio pieces but not for production traffic.

plotly — Static ggplot to Interactive

plotly::ggplotly() is the lowest-effort path from a static ggplot to an interactive widget. The ggplot stays the same; the wrapper does the translation.

Code
bnames <- babynames |>
    filter(name %in% c("Brianna", "Briana", "Breanna"))

p_bnames <- ggplot(bnames, aes(x = year, y = prop, colour = sex, linetype = name)) +
    geom_line() +
    labs(
        x = "Year", y = "Proportion of births",
        colour = "Sex", linetype = "Name",
        title = "Three spellings of Brianna, 1880–2017"
    ) +
    theme_classic()

ggplotly(p_bnames)

Hovering over any line reveals the exact year, proportion, and which spelling/sex combination you are on — information that a static chart could only convey with manual annotation per point.

Animation — Gapminder

Code
p_gap <- ggplot(gapminder,
                aes(x = gdpPercap, y = lifeExp,
                    colour = continent, size = pop)) +
    geom_point(aes(frame = year, ids = country), alpha = 0.7) +
    scale_x_log10() +
    scale_size(range = c(2, 12)) +
    labs(
        x = "GDP per capita (log scale)",
        y = "Life expectancy (years)",
        colour = "Continent", size = "Population",
        title = "Life expectancy vs. GDP per capita, 1952–2007"
    ) +
    theme_minimal()

ggplotly(p_gap)

Two aesthetic arguments carry the animation:

  • frame = year tells plotly that each year is a separate frame in a temporal sequence. A play button appears, and a year slider underneath.
  • ids = country tells plotly that the same point across frames should be treated as the same object. Without ids, points appear and disappear between frames; with ids, they slide smoothly, preserving object constancy and making longitudinal trajectories legible.

Together these make the animation far more informative than a series of snapshots: the reader can see a country move through the state space, which is the analytic quantity of interest in the Gapminder / Hans Rosling tradition.

When to Use Interactive vs. Static

The pre-class reading (the Medium piece on the death of interactive infographics) is overstated in places but correct on its central claim: most readers do not explore. Evidence from page-level analytics at several major publishers shows median interaction time on interactive infographics measured in seconds, not minutes. If the piece’s argument depends on the reader exploring to find the story, most readers will walk away with the default view.

A working rubric I apply when deciding between static and interactive:

  • Static wins when there is one primary comparison, the argument can be made in two or three views, and the readership is general. A static chart forces the designer to choose the one story they are telling; the reader gets that story or none at all, but the choice is honest.
  • Interactive wins when the analytical question genuinely requires the reader to choose their own cross-section — many cities, many industries, many countries — and the reader has a specific interest that the designer cannot anticipate. The neighborhood-diversity app is the canonical good case: no static version could serve a reader interested in Houston, Chicago, and Seattle equally well.
  • Both lose when the designer uses interactivity to avoid making editorial choices (“let the reader decide”). Interactivity as editorial abdication produces products that feel rich and communicate nothing.

The SDG Index Dashboard critiqued at the top of this document is partly in the third category — it defaults to a view with no guided narrative, and most readers leave the dashboard having scanned colours without registering any specific finding. The SDSN’s own annual PDF report handles the same data with far better interpretive lift. The dashboard is not wrong to exist; it is wrong to be treated as a replacement for the report.

Reflection

Shiny’s architectural insight is that ui and server are the same thing described from two angles. The ui says “there is an input called metro” and “there will be a plot called scatter_plot”; the server says “when something reads input$metro, give it the current value” and “when output$scatter_plot is accessed, run this ggplot code.” The IDs are the contract between the two sides. Most of the hard-to-debug Shiny errors are contract violations — a typo in an inputId, a plotOutput paired with a renderPlotly, a reactive expression that depends on an input not actually in the UI. Getting the contract right is more of a design discipline than a coding skill, and it’s the thing that transfers between dashboarding tools (Shiny, Dash, Streamlit) more than any specific syntax does.

plotly is the easy on-ramp; Shiny is where the real architecture lives. ggplotly() on a ggplot takes one line and adds tooltip/zoom/pan for free; for most cases where a reader wants “what’s the exact value of this point” the translation is sufficient. Shiny is a larger commitment — separate files, reactivity, deployment infrastructure — and its payoff is proportional to how much branching interaction the analysis requires. A one-slider-one-plot tool can live in a plotly widget embedded in a static HTML; a many-input, many-linked-view application is where Shiny’s architectural choices start to earn their complexity.

Interactive design is editorial discipline by another name. The temptation with interactive tools is to expose every choice to the reader — every filter, every toggle, every view — under the banner of “letting them explore.” What this actually produces, most of the time, is a reader who hits the default view, doesn’t know where to start, and leaves. The best interactive work is still heavily edited: a curated set of views, a clear default state, a guided narrative path that a reader can follow without any interaction, and then optional depth for the reader who wants it. The SDG Index Dashboard would be a sharper instrument with a tenth of its current interactivity and ten times its current narrative framing. The same applies to most dashboards. Interactivity is not a substitute for editorial thinking — it is an amplifier of the quality of the editorial thinking behind it.

**Data lag is invisible in the current-status UI.** Many SDG 4 indicators in low-income countries have latest available data from DHS/MICS surveys conducted in 2015–2019. The dashboard presents scores as current state without displaying per-indicator data years in the hover tooltip. A reader in 2026 sees a country coloured orange and assumes the colour reflects 2025 reality; it may reflect the most recent available observation from 2018. A per-indicator data-year label in the tooltip, or a transparency channel encoding recency on the map, would be a low-cost honesty fix.

**Assessment against SDG targets naturalizes a political artefact.** The 2030 deadline and the specific numerical targets ("by 2030, ensure all girls and boys complete free, equitable, and quality primary and secondary education") are the outputs of a 2015 UN negotiation — political compromises among member states, not independent scientific benchmarks. The dashboard translates distance-from-target into technical-looking assessment language ("on track," "challenges remain") that launders the political origin of the targets themselves. A country rated "on track" on SDG 4 is on track relative to a politically negotiated target; whether that target is adequate is a separate question that the dashboard's framing forecloses. As 2030 approaches and most goals are visibly missing their targets, the dashboard does not have a framework for asking whether the targets themselves need to be revised — it just continues scoring against them.

**The grey "Not Available" countries carry political information the UI flattens.** North Korea, Somalia, Eritrea, parts of conflict-affected Sub-Saharan Africa — the countries where data is most absent are not randomly distributed. They are the countries where state capacity is weakest, where conflict has disrupted statistical infrastructure, or where governments have chosen not to participate in international reporting regimes. These are frequently also the places where development outcomes are most consequential. Rendering them as grey is an honest acknowledgement of the data gap, but it positions them as absent from the conversation rather than as cases where the absence of data is itself a finding. A dashboard committed to equity framing would name this explicitly: *the countries most in need of developmental attention are also the countries least visible in the global measurement apparatus*.

**The interactive-vs-narrative trade-off has tipped the wrong way for the dashboard's most common users.** The *Annual Sustainable Development Report* PDF provides narrative context — structural drivers of progress or regression, cross-cutting analysis, country case studies — that the dashboard strips away in favour of numbers and colours. Policy users who treat the dashboard as their primary reference (which increasingly they do — UN agency briefings, government strategy documents, and media reports cite SDG Index rankings directly) are consuming interpretation-free data. The pre-class reading for this ICA (the *Medium* piece on "the death of interactive infographics") makes this exact point about interactive journalism: most users don't actually explore; they want guided interpretation. SDSN has that interpretation in the report. The dashboard doesn't import enough of it.

**Proportionality.** SDSN / Bertelsmann produce one of the most methodologically serious composite-index products in the field, and the critique above is not about analytical capacity. It is about editorial choices specific to the dashboard medium — composition opacity, within-country blindness, data-lag invisibility, target naturalization, grey-country invisibility, stripped narrative — that the annual PDF report either addresses or has room to address. The drift the dashboard is under is that it is being cited as an authoritative source in contexts where the caveats that live in the report are invisible. That drift is the institutional problem worth naming.

## Reflection — Kyle Walker's Neighborhood Diversity App

The app's core layout is a sidebar-main split: **sidebar** holds user inputs (city dropdown, span slider), **main** holds three linked outputs — a scatterplot of diversity score versus distance to city hall, a choropleth map of diversity by census tract, and a stacked bar of the city's overall racial composition. Selecting a different city updates all three plots simultaneously; adjusting the span slider re-smooths the scatterplot without touching the other two.

**Is the interactivity necessary?** Yes, and for a specific reason: the analytical question here is *how diversity varies within and across US metros*, and there are ~70 metros of interest. A static panel would need roughly seventy small-multiple scatterplots plus seventy maps plus seventy bars — well over two hundred subplots — which would defeat comparison. The interactive version lets the reader *perform the comparison one metro at a time* and choose which comparisons matter to them. This is the canonical case where interactivity earns its place: many possible slices, only a few of interest to any given reader, and no single static view can serve them all.

**What comparisons would I want that the app doesn't support?** A *side-by-side* view of two metros would be the first addition — right now the reader switches city and loses the previous view, forcing them to hold the previous metro in working memory. A time dimension (the `data_by_year` dataset suggests this is in the data but not in this app view) would let the reader see whether a metro's diversity profile is changing. And a *brush-and-link* between the map and the scatterplot — clicking a census tract on the map highlights its point on the scatter, or vice versa — would make the distance-to-city-hall story legible per-tract, not just in aggregate.

## The Shiny App Structure

A Shiny app has three logical components:

1. **`ui`** — what the user sees. Nested functions for page layout, input controls, and output placeholders.
2. **`server`** — what the app does in response to user input. A function that takes `input` and `output` objects and defines `render*()` functions that tie them together.
3. **`shinyApp(ui, server)`** — launches the app.

The design contract between `ui` and `server` is that every `inputId` in the `ui` becomes `input$that_id` in the `server`, and every `outputId` in the `ui` is populated by assignment to `output$that_id` in the `server`. Get the IDs right and the data flows; get them wrong and the app runs but shows nothing.

### Complete `app.R` for the neighborhood-diversity app

The following is what would go in `neighborhood_diversity/app.R`. It is not evaluated in this QMD — Shiny requires a live R process, not a rendered HTML — but running `shiny::runApp("neighborhood_diversity/")` in the RStudio console will launch it. The two data files (`diverse_data_by_dist.rds` and `diverse_data_by_year.csv`) need to sit in `neighborhood_diversity/data/`.

```r
# neighborhood_diversity/app.R
library(shiny)
library(tidyverse)
library(sf)
library(plotly)

# ---- Data ----
data_by_dist <- read_rds("data/diverse_data_by_dist.rds")
data_by_year <- read_csv("data/diverse_data_by_year.csv")

metro_names <- data_by_dist |> pull(metro_name) |> unique() |> sort()

# ---- UI ----
ui <- fluidPage(
    titlePanel("Neighborhood Diversity Across US Metros"),

    sidebarLayout(
        sidebarPanel(
            selectInput(
                inputId  = "metro",
                label    = "Metropolitan area:",
                choices  = metro_names,
                selected = "Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington, MN-WI"
            ),
            sliderInput(
                inputId = "span",
                label   = "Smoothing span:",
                min     = 0.1,
                max     = 1.0,
                value   = 0.5,
                step    = 0.1
            )
        ),

        mainPanel(
            plotlyOutput("scatter_plot"),
            plotOutput("map_plot"),
            plotlyOutput("bar_plot")
        )
    )
)

# ---- Server ----
server <- function(input, output) {

    # Reactive: city-filtered dataset, re-computed whenever `metro` changes
    metro_data <- reactive({
        data_by_dist |> filter(metro_name == input$metro)
    })

    # Scatterplot — diversity entropy vs. distance to city hall
    output$scatter_plot <- renderPlotly({
        p <- ggplot(metro_data(), aes(x = distmiles, y = entropy)) +
            geom_point(alpha = 0.4) +
            geom_smooth(span = input$span, se = FALSE, colour = "#b6345c") +
            labs(
                x = "Distance to city hall (miles)",
                y = "Diversity entropy",
                title = str_glue("Diversity vs. distance — {input$metro}")
            ) +
            theme_minimal()
        ggplotly(p)
    })

    # Map — choropleth of entropy by census tract
    output$map_plot <- renderPlot({
        ggplot(metro_data()) +
            geom_sf(aes(fill = entropy), colour = NA) +
            scale_fill_viridis_c(option = "magma") +
            labs(fill = "Entropy",
                 title = str_glue("Diversity by tract — {input$metro}")) +
            theme_void()
    })

    # Bar chart — total population by race in the selected metro
    output$bar_plot <- renderPlotly({
        race_totals <- metro_data() |>
            st_drop_geometry() |>
            summarise(across(c(aian, asian, black, hispanic,
                               two_or_more, white), sum, na.rm = TRUE)) |>
            pivot_longer(everything(), names_to = "race", values_to = "n") |>
            arrange(desc(n))

        p <- ggplot(race_totals, aes(x = fct_reorder(race, n), y = n)) +
            geom_col(fill = "#3b7a9b") +
            coord_flip() +
            labs(x = NULL, y = "Population",
                 title = str_glue("Racial composition — {input$metro}")) +
            theme_minimal()
        ggplotly(p)
    })
}

# ---- Launch ----
shinyApp(ui, server)
```

### Annotated design choices

- **`reactive({ ... })` for the filtered dataset.** The filtering happens once per metro change, and all three plots read from the same reactive. Without it, every plot would re-run the filter independently, and a slow filter step would get executed three times per interaction.
- **`plotlyOutput()` and `renderPlotly()` for the scatter and bar, but `plotOutput()` and `renderPlot()` for the map.** The scatter and bar benefit substantially from hover tooltips and zoom — plotly adds those for free on top of ggplot. The `sf` map is more finicky under `ggplotly()` (coordinate reference systems don't always survive the translation); keeping it as a static ggplot is the safer choice.
- **`sort()` on the metro names.** The dropdown gets long; sorting alphabetically is a user-comfort decision.
- **`selected = ...`** picks a default that makes the app useful on first load (Minneapolis–St. Paul — and if I'm honest, a small nod to the Twin Cities as home to both Macalester and Saint Paul).

### Publishing to `shinyapps.io`

Once the app runs locally, deployment follows three steps:

1. Install `rsconnect`, register at [shinyapps.io](https://www.shinyapps.io/), and paste the connection token into the R console.
2. From RStudio, open `app.R` and click the blue "Publish" icon in the top-right of the source pane.

## plotly — Static ggplot to Interactive

`plotly::ggplotly()` is the lowest-effort path from a static ggplot to an interactive widget. The ggplot stays the same; the wrapper does the translation.

### Line chart — variants of "Brianna" over time

```{r}
#| label: plotly-babynames
bnames <- babynames |>
    filter(name %in% c("Brianna", "Briana", "Breanna"))

p_bnames <- ggplot(bnames, aes(x = year, y = prop, colour = sex, linetype = name)) +
    geom_line() +
    labs(
        x = "Year", y = "Proportion of births",
        colour = "Sex", linetype = "Name",
        title = "Three spellings of Brianna, 1880–2017"
    ) +
    theme_classic()

ggplotly(p_bnames)
```

Hovering over any line reveals the exact year, proportion, and which spelling/sex combination you are on — information that a static chart could only convey with manual annotation per point.

### Animation — Gapminder

```{r}
#| label: plotly-gapminder
p_gap <- ggplot(gapminder,
                aes(x = gdpPercap, y = lifeExp,
                    colour = continent, size = pop)) +
    geom_point(aes(frame = year, ids = country), alpha = 0.7) +
    scale_x_log10() +
    scale_size(range = c(2, 12)) +
    labs(
        x = "GDP per capita (log scale)",
        y = "Life expectancy (years)",
        colour = "Continent", size = "Population",
        title = "Life expectancy vs. GDP per capita, 1952–2007"
    ) +
    theme_minimal()

ggplotly(p_gap)
```

Two aesthetic arguments carry the animation:

- **`frame = year`** tells plotly that each year is a separate frame in a temporal sequence. A play button appears, and a year slider underneath.
- **`ids = country`** tells plotly that the same point across frames should be treated as the same object. Without `ids`, points appear and disappear between frames; with `ids`, they slide smoothly, preserving object constancy and making longitudinal trajectories legible.

Together these make the animation far more informative than a series of snapshots: the reader can *see a country move* through the state space, which is the analytic quantity of interest in the Gapminder / Hans Rosling tradition.

## When to Use Interactive vs. Static

The pre-class reading (the *Medium* piece on the death of interactive infographics) is overstated in places but correct on its central claim: **most readers do not explore**. Evidence from page-level analytics at several major publishers shows median interaction time on interactive infographics measured in seconds, not minutes. If the piece's argument depends on the reader exploring to find the story, most readers will walk away with the default view.

A working rubric I apply when deciding between static and interactive:

- **Static wins when** there is one primary comparison, the argument can be made in two or three views, and the readership is general. A static chart forces the designer to choose the one story they are telling; the reader gets that story or none at all, but the choice is honest.
- **Interactive wins when** the analytical question genuinely requires the reader to choose their own cross-section — many cities, many industries, many countries — *and* the reader has a specific interest that the designer cannot anticipate. The neighborhood-diversity app is the canonical good case: no static version could serve a reader interested in Houston, Chicago, and Seattle equally well.
- **Both lose when** the designer uses interactivity to avoid making editorial choices ("let the reader decide"). Interactivity as editorial abdication produces products that feel rich and communicate nothing.

The SDG Index Dashboard critiqued at the top of this document is partly in the third category — it defaults to a view with no guided narrative, and most readers leave the dashboard having scanned colours without registering any specific finding. The SDSN's own annual PDF report handles the same data with far better interpretive lift. The dashboard is not wrong to exist; it is wrong to be treated as a replacement for the report.

## Reflection

**Shiny's architectural insight is that `ui` and `server` are the same thing described from two angles.** The `ui` says "there is an input called `metro`" and "there will be a plot called `scatter_plot`"; the `server` says "when something reads `input$metro`, give it the current value" and "when `output$scatter_plot` is accessed, run this `ggplot` code." The IDs are the contract between the two sides. Most of the hard-to-debug Shiny errors are contract violations — a typo in an `inputId`, a `plotOutput` paired with a `renderPlotly`, a reactive expression that depends on an input not actually in the UI. Getting the contract right is more of a design discipline than a coding skill, and it's the thing that transfers between dashboarding tools (Shiny, Dash, Streamlit) more than any specific syntax does.

**plotly is the easy on-ramp; Shiny is where the real architecture lives.** `ggplotly()` on a ggplot takes one line and adds tooltip/zoom/pan for free; for most cases where a reader wants "what's the exact value of this point" the translation is sufficient. Shiny is a larger commitment — separate files, reactivity, deployment infrastructure — and its payoff is proportional to how much *branching interaction* the analysis requires. A one-slider-one-plot tool can live in a plotly widget embedded in a static HTML; a many-input, many-linked-view application is where Shiny's architectural choices start to earn their complexity.

**Interactive design is editorial discipline by another name.** The temptation with interactive tools is to expose every choice to the reader — every filter, every toggle, every view — under the banner of "letting them explore." What this actually produces, most of the time, is a reader who hits the default view, doesn't know where to start, and leaves. The best interactive work is still heavily edited: a curated set of views, a clear default state, a guided narrative path that a reader can follow without any interaction, and then optional depth for the reader who wants it. The SDG Index Dashboard would be a sharper instrument with a tenth of its current interactivity and ten times its current narrative framing. The same applies to most dashboards. Interactivity is not a substitute for editorial thinking — it is an amplifier of the quality of the editorial thinking behind it.
