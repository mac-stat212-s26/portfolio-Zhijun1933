🔥 Data Story Critique — Cheaper Mortgages and Car Loans (NYT, 18 Sept 2024)

Source: https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2024/09/18/business/economy/mortgages-car-loans-interest-rates.html

Author: Karl Russell (NYT graphics team).

What is the data story?

Published on the day the Federal Reserve announced its first rate cut in over four years — a 50-basis-point reduction that took the federal funds rate from 5.25–5.50% to 4.75–5.00% — the piece is a short explainer aimed at non-specialist readers. It situates the rate cut within a five-year window (2019–2024) using three overlaid time-series charts: (1) the federal funds rate alongside average 30-year mortgage and new-car-loan rates, (2) the federal funds rate alongside inflation, and (3) average credit-card APRs. The narrative takes six short paragraphs to walk the reader through three ideas: borrowing costs are easing; the Fed cut rates after years of fighting inflation; the transmission of rate changes to consumer products is not instantaneous.

What is effective?

The overlaid time-series chart is the right form for the claim. All three rates share a unit (percent interest) and a time axis, so putting them on one y-axis establishes comparability without the interpretive hazards of a secondary axis (which almost always misleads). What the reader sees is the co-movement of Fed funds and consumer borrowing rates across the tightening cycle — which is precisely the claim the piece is making. This is the least-astonishment principle in economic charting.

The 2019 baseline is well chosen for the explainer form. It captures pre-pandemic conditions, the COVID shock, the zero-rate era, the 2022–2023 tightening cycle, and the cut itself. Starting earlier would dilute attention on the recent event; starting later would lose the context that explains why the cut is news.

“Transmission is not instantaneous” is the most important line in the piece. Readers’ intuition is that when the Fed moves rates, consumer products follow immediately. They don’t — mortgage rates are mediated by the 10-year Treasury, credit-card APRs by lenders’ own repricing cycles. By explicitly flagging lag and using the credit-card chart as visual evidence (APRs kept climbing after Fed funds peaked), the piece sets the correct horizon expectation. This is a genuinely important piece of financial literacy that a lot of coverage skips.

Keeping credit-card APR on its own chart is a subtle analytical choice. At ~21%, it is an order of magnitude above mortgage and auto rates; putting all four on one chart would have compressed the mortgage/auto variation into invisibility. The separation also lets the reader notice an unspoken distributional point — that the people most reliant on credit-card borrowing (often lower-income, without home equity to draw on) pay rates that have barely moved while Fed policy swung dramatically.

What could be improved?

The Fed funds → mortgage rate causality is presented as direct, but it isn’t. Mortgage rates track the 10-year Treasury yield, not the federal funds rate — Fed funds drives short-end rates, and long-end rates respond through the term structure and market expectations. This is why the chart shows the mortgage rate already declining before the Fed cut on Wednesday: markets had priced the cut in weeks earlier. The piece doesn’t mention this, and the caption language (“Borrowing costs hit a two-decade high as part of the Fed’s inflation fight”) implies more direct causation than the data shows. A single sentence — “mortgage rates have been easing in anticipation of this cut, because markets set long-term rates based on where they expect Fed funds to go” — would be more honest and is arguably necessary to explain what the reader is looking at.

No distinction between real and nominal rates. The second chart places Fed funds against inflation on the same axis, but the Fed’s actual policy stance is the real rate — nominal minus inflation expectations. In 2022–2023, Fed funds rose steeply, but so did inflation, and the real rate’s trajectory is a different story from the nominal one the chart tells. Real-rate economics is outside an explainer’s scope, but a one-line note — “once you subtract out inflation, the Fed’s tightening was less aggressive than the blue line suggests” — would prevent the wrong inference that nominal rate level equals policy tightness.

The labour market is named in text but not visualized. The piece says “a variety of factors go into the Fed’s decision… including the strength of the labor market,” then describes unemployment rising and wage growth slowing — but no fourth chart supplies the data. In a piece whose default mode is “show, don’t tell,” this asymmetry is visible. A compact unemployment + wage-growth panel would give the claim visual weight.

The “waited too long” line hangs in isolation. “That has raised questions about whether the Fed waited too long to begin lowering rates” — who is raising these questions, with what argument? The Sahm rule trigger, the persistence of yield-curve inversion, the speed of labour-market cooling — these are the actual reasons the “too late” case is being made. The reader is left with a suggestion of debate but no substance. For a neutral explainer, either develop the point briefly or leave it out; in current form it reads as tacit editorial agreement without argument.

Proportionality. This is a same-day news explainer from a graphics reporter with very limited word count. It executes its brief well — correct reference frame, clean overlaid time series, honest horizon-setting. The critiques above are directed at what a slightly longer version of this piece could add, not at what this one got wrong.

JSON Basics

A minimal example

Code
# JSON is just text — here stored as a string in R
json_basic <- '{
  "name": "John",
  "age": 30,
  "city": "New York",
  "hobbies": ["reading", "hiking", "coding"]
}'

# fromJSON() parses it into an R object
person <- fromJSON(json_basic)
person
$name
[1] "John"

$age
[1] 30

$city
[1] "New York"

$hobbies
[1] "reading" "hiking"  "coding"
Code
class(person)
[1] "list"
Code
str(person)
List of 4
 $ name   : chr "John"
 $ age    : int 30
 $ city   : chr "New York"
 $ hobbies: chr [1:3] "reading" "hiking" "coding"

The result is a named list because JSON objects map to named lists in R. The hobbies element inside the list is an unnamed collection that jsonlite simplified into a character vector — this is the “simplify” convention discussed in Ooms (2014) — a homogeneous JSON array becomes an atomic vector rather than a list.

Arrays of objects — the simplifyVector choice

Code
json_mario <- '[
  {"Name": "Mario",  "Age": 32, "Occupation": "Plumber"},
  {"Name": "Peach",  "Age": 21, "Occupation": "Princess"},
  {},
  {"Name": "Bowser",             "Occupation": "Koopa"}
]'

With simplifyVector = FALSE (no simplification): we get a list of four lists.

Code
fromJSON(json_mario, simplifyVector = FALSE)
[[1]]
[[1]]$Name
[1] "Mario"

[[1]]$Age
[1] 32

[[1]]$Occupation
[1] "Plumber"


[[2]]
[[2]]$Name
[1] "Peach"

[[2]]$Age
[1] 21

[[2]]$Occupation
[1] "Princess"


[[3]]
named list()

[[4]]
[[4]]$Name
[1] "Bowser"

[[4]]$Occupation
[1] "Koopa"

With simplifyVector = TRUE (default): jsonlite detects that the records have compatible fields and returns a data frame.

Code
fromJSON(json_mario, simplifyVector = TRUE)
    Name Age Occupation
1  Mario  32    Plumber
2  Peach  21   Princess
3   <NA>  NA       <NA>
4 Bowser  NA      Koopa

Three things worth noticing:

  • Where a record had no Name field (the third row), jsonlite inserted NA. This is the row-based-JSON / column-based-R translation at work — in the JSON, missing fields are simply absent; in a data frame, every row must have a value (or NA) for every column.
  • The third record ({}) maps to an all-NA row. It doesn’t disappear, which is the right behaviour: the presence of an empty record is itself information.
  • Once simplifyVector = TRUE gives us a tibble-shaped object, the result can flow directly into the tidyverse pipeline. This is the main reason the default is on.

When simplification fails — heterogeneous arrays

simplifyVector = TRUE is a best-effort heuristic. When records don’t share a consistent structure, jsonlite falls back:

Code
json_heterogeneous <- '[
  {"id": 1, "tags": ["red", "blue"]},
  {"id": 2, "tags": "red"},
  {"id": 3, "tags": null}
]'

fromJSON(json_heterogeneous, simplifyVector = TRUE)
  id      tags
1  1 red, blue
2  2       red
3  3      NULL

The tags column is a list-column — because in the three rows it’s respectively a length-2 character vector, a length-1 character vector, and NULL. A data frame column can’t be a regular atomic vector here because the lengths don’t match, so jsonlite produces a list-column. This is one of the places where JSON’s row-orientation collides with R’s column-orientation and the fix is not neat; the user is expected to tidyr::unnest() downstream.

Edge Cases from the Ooms Paper

Vectors of length 1

Ooms emphasizes that jsonlite always encodes length-1 vectors as JSON arrays (not as scalars), because inconsistency across lengths is a common source of bugs in dynamic-data pipelines.

Code
# A length-1 vector stays an array in JSON
toJSON(pi)
[3.1416]
Code
# Round-trip preserves the vector
identical(fromJSON(toJSON(pi)), pi)
[1] FALSE

The rival packages rjson and RJSONIO handle this differently — rjson encodes a length-1 vector as a JSON primitive inside a list, which silently changes the type depending on vector length. For pipelines that consume API output of varying sizes, jsonlite’s consistency is a real feature, not a cosmetic one.

The “golden rule” — keys are names, not data

Ooms quotes Mike Dewar’s “golden rule”: JSON keys should be names, not data. A common violation is using timestamps as keys:

Code
# Bad — keys are data (timestamps)
bad_json <- '[
  {"1325344443": 124},
  {"1325344456": 131},
  {"1325344478": 137}
]'

fromJSON(bad_json)
  1325344443 1325344456 1325344478
1        124         NA         NA
2         NA        131         NA
3         NA         NA        137

Every record has a different key, so no downstream code can address the “price” field by name. The fix is to promote the timestamp into its own field:

Code
# Good — keys describe what the field means
good_json <- '[
  {"time": 1325344443, "price": 124},
  {"time": 1325344456, "price": 131},
  {"time": 1325344478, "price": 137}
]'

fromJSON(good_json)
        time price
1 1325344443   124
2 1325344456   131
3 1325344478   137

The “good” version simplifies cleanly to a tidy data frame; the “bad” version produces a list of list of named scalars that needs manual unpacking. The rule generalizes beyond JSON — any data-interchange format, and indeed any tabular data representation, works correctly when the schema (which keys exist, what they mean) is separated from the data (what values those keys take). Wickham’s tidy-data principle is the same insight, arrived at from the R side.

Nested records

Code
nested_json <- '[
  {
    "driver": "Bowser",
    "vehicle": {"model": "Piranha Prowler", "stats": {"speed": 55, "weight": 67}}
  },
  {
    "driver": "Peach",
    "vehicle": {"model": "Royal Racer",     "stats": {"speed": 34, "weight": 24}}
  }
]'

nested <- fromJSON(nested_json, simplifyVector = TRUE)
nested
  driver   vehicle.model vehicle.stats.speed vehicle.stats.weight
1 Bowser Piranha Prowler                  55                   67
2  Peach     Royal Racer                  34                   24
Code
# The `vehicle` column is itself a data frame — this is a nested data frame
str(nested)
'data.frame':   2 obs. of  2 variables:
 $ driver : chr  "Bowser" "Peach"
 $ vehicle:'data.frame':    2 obs. of  2 variables:
  ..$ model: chr  "Piranha Prowler" "Royal Racer"
  ..$ stats:'data.frame':   2 obs. of  2 variables:
  .. ..$ speed : int  55 34
  .. ..$ weight: int  67 24

For one-to-one nesting, tidyr::unnest_wider() flattens the structure cleanly:

Code
nested |>
    tidyr::unnest_wider(vehicle) |>
    tidyr::unnest_wider(stats)
# A tibble: 2 × 4
  driver model           speed weight
  <chr>  <chr>           <int>  <int>
1 Bowser Piranha Prowler    55     67
2 Peach  Royal Racer        34     24

For one-to-many nesting (where a single record has an array of sub-records), unnest_longer() is the corresponding tool. Together, unnest_wider() and unnest_longer() cover most cases of bringing JSON-from-the-web into a tidy-data-frame shape.

A Real API Call

A typical use of fromJSON() is to fetch directly from a URL. GitHub’s public API returns JSON and does not require authentication for modest request volumes. Here we look up the latest releases of the tidyverse package:

The GitHub API returns an array of release objects, which jsonlite simplifies into a data frame. Columns like author, assets, and reactions come through as nested data frames or list-columns (you can check with str(tidy_releases, max.level = 1)), reflecting the one-to-many relationships in the underlying schema — each release has one author but can have multiple assets. The rest of this course’s API material is essentially about this pattern: hit a URL, get JSON back, negotiate the nesting, reshape into a tibble.

Reflection

JSON and R have an impedance mismatch, and jsonlite is the translator. R thinks in columns; JSON thinks in rows. R has vectors as a first-class type; JSON has objects and arrays but no separate notion of a scalar. When you call fromJSON(), you are invoking a set of conventions — described in Ooms (2014) — for how to bridge the two. The single most important convention is simplifyVector = TRUE: when the structure permits, promote JSON arrays of compatible objects into data frames. When it doesn’t permit, fall back to list-columns rather than fail. This is not a mathematical isomorphism — fromJSON(toJSON(x)) is not always exactly x — but it is the practical mapping that makes JSON APIs usable from R.

Type consistency is a runtime contract you have to enforce yourself. API documentation tells you what fields should be returned, but reality will hand you missing fields, null values, length-1 arrays that could-have-been scalars, and the occasional structural change. jsonlite does its best — consistent encoding of length-1 vectors as arrays, NA insertion for missing fields in simplified data frames — but the consumer of the API still needs to validate that the shape of the data matches expectations. The map_*() functions from ICA 12 are relevant here: map_chr() will fail loudly if any element isn’t a character, which is what you want when the upstream JSON has silently started returning numeric IDs instead of string IDs.

The “golden rule” is a data-modeling principle, not a JSON one. Keys-describe-meaning and values-carry-data is the same rule that makes relational schemas normalise-able, that makes tidy data tidy, and that makes APIs versionable. When you design data structures that will cross system boundaries — whether as JSON over HTTP, as Parquet on disk, or as tables in a warehouse — holding the schema steady while the data varies is what makes downstream code possible to write. Everything in the next ICA about URL construction and query parameters is downstream of this principle.

```{r}
# Bad — keys are data (timestamps)
bad_json <- '[
  {"1325344443": 124},
  {"1325344456": 131},
  {"1325344478": 137}
]'

fromJSON(bad_json)
```

Every record has a different key, so no downstream code can address the "price" field by name. The fix is to promote the timestamp into its own field:

```{r}
# Good — keys describe what the field means
good_json <- '[
  {"time": 1325344443, "price": 124},
  {"time": 1325344456, "price": 131},
  {"time": 1325344478, "price": 137}
]'

fromJSON(good_json)
```

The "good" version simplifies cleanly to a tidy data frame; the "bad" version produces a list of list of named scalars that needs manual unpacking. The rule generalizes beyond JSON — any data-interchange format, and indeed any tabular data representation, works correctly when the **schema** (which keys exist, what they mean) is separated from the **data** (what values those keys take). Wickham's tidy-data principle is the same insight, arrived at from the R side.

### Nested records

```{r}
nested_json <- '[
  {
    "driver": "Bowser",
    "vehicle": {"model": "Piranha Prowler", "stats": {"speed": 55, "weight": 67}}
  },
  {
    "driver": "Peach",
    "vehicle": {"model": "Royal Racer",     "stats": {"speed": 34, "weight": 24}}
  }
]'

nested <- fromJSON(nested_json, simplifyVector = TRUE)
nested
```

```{r}
# The `vehicle` column is itself a data frame — this is a nested data frame
str(nested)
```

For one-to-one nesting, `tidyr::unnest_wider()` flattens the structure cleanly:

```{r}
nested |>
    tidyr::unnest_wider(vehicle) |>
    tidyr::unnest_wider(stats)
```

For one-to-many nesting (where a single record has an array of sub-records), `unnest_longer()` is the corresponding tool. Together, `unnest_wider()` and `unnest_longer()` cover most cases of bringing JSON-from-the-web into a tidy-data-frame shape.

## A Real API Call

A typical use of `fromJSON()` is to fetch directly from a URL. GitHub's public API returns JSON and does not require authentication for modest request volumes. Here we look up the latest releases of the `tidyverse` package:

```{r}
#| cache: true
tidy_releases <- fromJSON(
    "https://api.github.com/repos/tidyverse/tidyverse/releases"
)

# What did we get?
class(tidy_releases)
dim(tidy_releases)
names(tidy_releases)
```

```{r}
# Pull out the columns that are actually useful for a release summary
tidy_releases |>
    as_tibble() |>
    transmute(
        tag       = tag_name,
        name      = name,
        published = lubridate::as_date(published_at),
        prerelease = prerelease
    ) |>
    arrange(desc(published))
```

The GitHub API returns an array of release objects, which `jsonlite` simplifies into a data frame. Columns like `author`, `assets`, and `reactions` come through as nested data frames or list-columns (you can check with `str(tidy_releases, max.level = 1)`), reflecting the one-to-many relationships in the underlying schema — each release has one author but can have multiple assets. The rest of this course's API material is essentially about this pattern: hit a URL, get JSON back, negotiate the nesting, reshape into a tibble.

## Reflection

**JSON and R have an impedance mismatch, and `jsonlite` is the translator.** R thinks in columns; JSON thinks in rows. R has vectors as a first-class type; JSON has objects and arrays but no separate notion of a scalar. When you call `fromJSON()`, you are invoking a set of *conventions* — described in Ooms (2014) — for how to bridge the two. The single most important convention is `simplifyVector = TRUE`: when the structure permits, promote JSON arrays of compatible objects into data frames. When it doesn't permit, fall back to list-columns rather than fail. This is not a mathematical isomorphism — `fromJSON(toJSON(x))` is not always exactly `x` — but it is the practical mapping that makes JSON APIs usable from R.

**Type consistency is a runtime contract you have to enforce yourself.** API documentation tells you what fields *should* be returned, but reality will hand you missing fields, null values, length-1 arrays that could-have-been scalars, and the occasional structural change. `jsonlite` does its best — consistent encoding of length-1 vectors as arrays, `NA` insertion for missing fields in simplified data frames — but the consumer of the API still needs to validate that the shape of the data matches expectations. The `map_*()` functions from ICA 12 are relevant here: `map_chr()` will fail loudly if any element isn't a character, which is what you want when the upstream JSON has silently started returning numeric IDs instead of string IDs.

**The "golden rule" is a data-modeling principle, not a JSON one.** Keys-describe-meaning and values-carry-data is the same rule that makes relational schemas normalise-able, that makes tidy data tidy, and that makes APIs versionable. When you design data structures that will cross system boundaries — whether as JSON over HTTP, as Parquet on disk, or as tables in a warehouse — holding the schema steady while the data varies is what makes downstream code possible to write. Everything in the next ICA about URL construction and query parameters is downstream of this principle.
