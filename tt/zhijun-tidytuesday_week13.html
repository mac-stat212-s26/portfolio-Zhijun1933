Background

Birchy Head is a small inlet on the south shore of Nova Scotia, part of Lunenburg County’s coastal water system. Since early 2018, the Centre for Marine Applied Research (CMAR), in partnership with the Nova Scotia Department of Fisheries and Aquaculture, has maintained a sensor string in the water column at this location — seven thermistors stacked from the near-surface at 2 metres down to 40 metres, recording temperature continuously. The resulting dataset, published on the Nova Scotia Open Data Portal, now covers seven years of daily-aggregated temperatures at all seven depths.

The data is scientifically useful because coastal oceans are where atmospheric warming, aquaculture, commercial fisheries, and ecological change all meet. Cold deep water in summer is what keeps Nova Scotia’s farmed salmon and wild lobster alive through the heat; if that deep reservoir warms, or if summer stratification collapses earlier in the year, the downstream consequences are economic as well as ecological. The North Atlantic has been warming rapidly — the Gulf of Maine, just to the southwest, has in some recent years been described as warming faster than 99 percent of the world’s ocean. Whether that warming is visible at this one sensor string, over the seven-year window available, is a question the data is well-posed to answer.

Motivation & Research Question

A coastal water column has a predictable seasonal cycle. In winter, surface cooling and wind-driven mixing homogenise the column — sensors at 2, 10, 20, and 40 metres all read roughly the same temperature. In spring, solar heating warms the surface faster than the deep, and a thermocline builds. By summer, the top few metres can be ten degrees warmer than the bottom. Autumn cooling and storms mix the column back together, and the cycle repeats.

Research question: Over seven years of daily measurements, is the thermal structure at Birchy Head stable — does each year’s annual cycle look like the others — or are there visible year-on-year shifts in when the column stratifies, how strongly it stratifies, and how warm the warmest water gets?

The plot shows the water column at Birchy Head as a cross-section: the top row of each year is the 2-metre sensor, just below the surface, and the bottom row is the 40-metre sensor, sitting on the seabed. Colour is daily mean temperature, from deep winter blues near 0°C to summer reds above 20°C. The repeated seasonal cycle is the dominant feature. Every winter (visible as a vertical blue band spanning all seven depths) the water column cools and mixes until the top and bottom sensors are within a degree of one another. Every summer, a red-orange tongue appears at the top of the figure while the bottom of the figure stays blue — surface waters warm rapidly while deep water, insulated by the thermocline, remains cold. The summer of 2020 shows the most intense and longest-lasting surface warmth in the record, with the red tongue extending both deeper and further into autumn than in most other years. The summer of 2022 is the mildest-looking, with cooler surface peaks and a shorter warm season. Winter mixing, by contrast, looks remarkably consistent: in no year does the deep water fail to reset to near-freezing.

The second view collapses the seven-by-seven grid into a single line per year, tracking the difference between the surface and the 40-metre sensor. When this line sits near zero — most of winter and early spring — the column is mixed and the two depths agree. As spring sun builds a thermocline, the line climbs; it peaks in July or August when surface waters are warmest and deep waters are still cold; it falls again as autumn storms break the thermocline down. The grey ribbon is the middle 50 percent of all years, which makes anomalous years easy to see. 2020 (deep red) climbs visibly higher than the others, reaching surface-to-deep differences above 15°C at its peak — a genuinely extreme stratification event in this record. 2022 and 2025, by contrast, sit below the grey band for much of summer: their columns stratified less strongly than a typical year. The shape of the annual cycle is otherwise remarkably preserved. Spring onset of stratification falls in a narrow two-week window each year, and autumn collapse arrives on schedule. The variance is in amplitude, not timing.

Takeaway

Seven years is a short record for climate questions. What it is long enough to show is the envelope of ordinary variability at a coastal site — the range within which “normal” falls. At Birchy Head, the envelope is well-defined. The column stratifies every summer and mixes every winter; the 40-metre sensor rarely rises above 10°C; the timing of the seasonal transitions is consistent to within about two weeks from year to year. Against that envelope, 2020 stands out as the year with the strongest and longest surface warming in the record, and 2022 as the mildest. Neither year is outside what a longer record might show as natural variability, but both sit clearly at the edges of this one.

What the record cannot do, given its length, is tell us whether the North Atlantic warming that has been documented in offshore data is already penetrating this particular inlet. A monotonic upward drift in peak summer temperature would take at least another decade of monitoring to separate from year-to-year variability. What the record can do is what good environmental monitoring is supposed to do: establish a baseline against which future years will be measurable. Seven years in, Birchy Head has that baseline.

The second view collapses the seven-by-seven grid into a single line per
year, tracking the difference between the surface and the 40-metre sensor.
When this line sits near zero — most of winter and early spring — the
column is mixed and the two depths agree. As spring sun builds a
thermocline, the line climbs; it peaks in July or August when surface
waters are warmest and deep waters are still cold; it falls again as
autumn storms break the thermocline down. The grey ribbon is the middle
50 percent of all years, which makes anomalous years easy to see. 2020
(deep red) climbs visibly higher than the others, reaching surface-to-deep
differences above 15°C at its peak — a genuinely extreme stratification
event in this record. 2022 and 2025, by contrast, sit below the grey
band for much of summer: their columns stratified less strongly than a
typical year. The shape of the annual cycle is otherwise remarkably
preserved. Spring onset of stratification falls in a narrow two-week
window each year, and autumn collapse arrives on schedule. The variance
is in amplitude, not timing.

## Takeaway

Seven years is a short record for climate questions. What it is long
enough to show is the *envelope* of ordinary variability at a coastal
site — the range within which "normal" falls. At Birchy Head, the
envelope is well-defined. The column stratifies every summer and mixes
every winter; the 40-metre sensor rarely rises above 10°C; the timing of
the seasonal transitions is consistent to within about two weeks from
year to year. Against that envelope, 2020 stands out as the year with
the strongest and longest surface warming in the record, and 2022 as the
mildest. Neither year is outside what a longer record might show as
natural variability, but both sit clearly at the edges of this one.

What the record cannot do, given its length, is tell us whether the
North Atlantic warming that has been documented in offshore data is
already penetrating this particular inlet. A monotonic upward drift in
peak summer temperature would take at least another decade of monitoring
to separate from year-to-year variability. What the record can do is
what good environmental monitoring is supposed to do: establish a
baseline against which future years will be measurable. Seven years in,
Birchy Head has that baseline.
