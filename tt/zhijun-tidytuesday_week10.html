Background

In 1964, Sherman Kent, then chair of the CIA’s Board of National Estimates, circulated an internal memo titled “Words of Estimative Probability.” Kent had become alarmed by how wildly his own colleagues disagreed about what standard intelligence vocabulary meant. He had surveyed a group of senior analysts, asking each to put a percentage on phrases like “serious possibility” and “highly unlikely.” For “serious possibility,” one analyst reported 20 percent; another reported 80 percent. Kent’s worry was not that analysts were sloppy — it was that the vocabulary of estimation, which the intelligence community relied on to convey risk to policymakers, was systematically failing at the job.

The response, over decades, was to build calibrated language. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change now pegs “very likely” to 90–100 percent and “likely” to 66–100 percent in its reports. The US National Intelligence Council publishes its probability ladder alongside every major estimate. These institutional fixes assume the problem is real: that untethered probability phrases fail to coordinate interpretation across readers. Adam Kucharski’s online quiz (2024–2026, approximately 5,200 respondents) lets us check that assumption at scale. Each respondent assigned a 0–100 percent value to nineteen phrases in a random order. The question this dataset invites is whether, sixty years after Kent, ordinary usage actually conveys probability — or whether some phrases are so unmoored that saying them communicates essentially nothing.

Motivation & Research Question

There are two distinct ways a probability phrase can fail to communicate. It can be imprecise — people agree on roughly where it sits, but with wide disagreement on exactly how high or low. Or it can be redundant — people place it at a precise location, but several other phrases live in the same spot, so using one rather than another conveys no extra information about probability. A phrase that is both imprecise and redundant is, in Kent’s phrase, “a foot in each camp”: neither specific enough to pin a number to, nor distinct enough to justify its existence in the vocabulary.

Research question: Which probability phrases actually convey distinct information, and which are so imprecise or redundant that a reader learns nothing from them?

The plot orders the nineteen phrases by median rating, so the vertical axis reads as a rough probability ladder from “Almost No Chance” at the bottom to “Will Happen” at the top. Each phrase is represented by its interquartile range (the coloured bar) and its median (the white point), with individual responses shown as the faint grey cloud behind. Two patterns stand out. The ends of the ladder are tight: “Almost No Chance,” “Highly Unlikely,” “Will Happen,” and “Almost Certain” all have narrow interquartile bands concentrated near the edges of the scale, coloured gold. Respondents broadly agree on what these mean. The middle of the ladder is wide: “Could Happen,” “Might Happen,” “May Happen,” and “Realistic Possibility” all have interquartile bands spanning twenty-five to thirty-five percentage points, coloured deep blue. A reader who hears “might happen” cannot recover even a rough probability band from the phrase — its middle 50 percent of interpretations stretches from about 20 percent to about 55 percent.

The second view answers the redundancy question. Within each panel, three phrases that landed at roughly the same position on the ladder are plotted against one another. If the phrases carried distinct information, the density curves would sit side by side with visible separation. They do not. In the low panel, “Improbable,” “Highly Unlikely,” and “Remote Chance” produce densities that peak in essentially the same neighbourhood near 10 percent and overlap almost entirely. In the middle panel, “Realistic Possibility” drifts slightly higher than “About Even” and “Better than Even,” but the three distributions share the bulk of their mass. The high panel is the starkest: “Likely,” “Probable,” and “Very Good Chance” produce densities that are close to identical. Takeaway

Sixty years after Sherman Kent warned that untethered probability vocabulary would fail to communicate, the pattern he documented internally at CIA is visible at scale in the open data. The edges of the scale work: phrases that anchor near certainty or near impossibility produce tight, consistent interpretations. The middle does not, and it fails in two distinct ways. First, the middle phrases are imprecise — “might happen” or “could happen” does not narrow the probability to anything a listener can act on. Second, at several positions on the ladder the phrases are redundant — “likely,” “probable,” and “very good chance” live in the same place and no listener can tell which a speaker meant by choosing one rather than another.

None of this says the words are meaningless. They clearly convey direction (high, medium, low), and some are tight enough to convey a useful band. But the claim implicit in ordinary usage — that picking “likely” rather than “probable,” or “may happen” rather than “might happen,” communicates a meaningful shade of probability — is not supported by how respondents actually interpret them. The institutional response of the IPCC and the National Intelligence Council, which is to bolt numerical ranges onto a small calibrated vocabulary rather than trust the phrases to do the work, looks, on this evidence, like the right response to a real problem rather than an overcautious one.

the claim implicit in ordinary usage — that picking "likely" rather than
"probable," or "may happen" rather than "might happen," communicates a
meaningful shade of probability — is not supported by how respondents actually
interpret them. The institutional response of the IPCC and the National
Intelligence Council, which is to bolt numerical ranges onto a small calibrated
vocabulary rather than trust the phrases to do the work, looks, on this
evidence, like the right response to a real problem rather than an overcautious
one.
