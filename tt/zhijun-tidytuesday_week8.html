Background

Science Foundation Ireland (SFI) was established in 2000 as Ireland’s first dedicated science funding agency, modelled on the US National Science Foundation, and dissolved on 31 July 2024 when it was absorbed into a new, broader research body. In its 24-year lifespan it committed billions of euros to research in STEM fields across Irish higher education. SFI’s founding mandate was explicitly selective: it would back researchers most likely to produce world-class outputs, rather than distributing small grants widely. That competitive philosophy raises a natural empirical question about how funding was actually distributed across the landscape of Irish universities.

Ireland’s higher education system is dominated by a handful of large research-intensive institutions—Trinity College Dublin, University College Dublin, University College Cork, University of Galway, and University of Limerick—alongside specialised institutes such as the Tyndall National Institute. Whether SFI’s commitment to excellence led to increasing concentration of funding in the top institutions, or whether it spread more evenly as more universities built research capacity, is a question the data can answer.

Motivation & Research Question

A funding agency that declares itself selective has an internal tension: it can either track genuine excellence wherever it emerges (dispersing grants over time) or it can reinforce existing capacity advantages at established institutions (concentrating grants). Over a 24-year lifecycle, did SFI’s grant portfolio become more or less concentrated, and which institutions drove the dynamics at the top and bottom of the distribution?

Research question: Did SFI funding become more concentrated in a small number of institutions over its lifespan, and which research bodies anchored the top tier throughout?

Visualization 2: Herfindahl-Style Concentration Over Time

Show code
# Compute an annual share-based concentration measure:
# sum of squared market shares (HHI), then scale 0–1 for readability
hhi_annual <- grants_clean |>
  group_by(year) |>
  mutate(total_year = sum(current_total_commitment)) |>
  group_by(year, institution) |>
  summarise(
    share = sum(current_total_commitment) / first(total_year),
    .groups = "drop"
  ) |>
  group_by(year) |>
  summarise(
    hhi        = sum(share^2),
    n_inst     = n_distinct(institution),
    top2_share = sum(sort(share, decreasing = TRUE)[1:min(2, n())]),
    .groups    = "drop"
  )

# Dual-axis: HHI left, top-2 share right
ggplot(hhi_annual, aes(x = year)) +
  geom_ribbon(
    aes(ymin = 0, ymax = hhi),
    fill = "#d4af37", alpha = 0.25
  ) +
  geom_line(aes(y = hhi, colour = "HHI (left axis)"),
            linewidth = 1.1) +
  geom_line(aes(y = top2_share / 3, colour = "Top-2 share ÷ 3 (right axis)"),
            linewidth = 1.0, linetype = "dashed") +
  scale_colour_manual(
    values = c(
      "HHI (left axis)"            = "#d4af37",
      "Top-2 share ÷ 3 (right axis)" = "#4a90d9"
    ),
    name = NULL
  ) +
  scale_y_continuous(
    name     = "Herfindahl–Hirschman Index (HHI)",
    labels   = number_format(accuracy = 0.01),
    sec.axis = sec_axis(
      ~ . * 3,
      name   = "Top-2 institution share",
      labels = percent_format(accuracy = 1)
    ),
    expand = expansion(mult = c(0, 0.05))
  ) +
  scale_x_continuous(breaks = seq(2000, 2024, 2)) +
  annotate(
    "text", x = 2001.5, y = 0.48,
    label = "Early years: few grants,\nhigh concentration",
    size = 3.2, colour = "grey40", hjust = 0
  ) +
  annotate(
    "text", x = 2014, y = 0.21,
    label = "Maturation: more\ninstitutions compete",
    size = 3.2, colour = "grey40", hjust = 0
  ) +
  labs(
    title    = "Funding concentration fell as SFI matured, but TCD + UCD still capture ~40–50% most years",
    subtitle = "HHI and top-2 institution share of annual grant commitments, 2000–2024",
    x        = NULL,
    caption  = "Source: Ireland Open Data Portal · TidyTuesday 2026 Wk 8 · Zhijun He · 2026-02-25"
  ) +
  theme_minimal(base_size = 13) +
  theme(
    plot.title    = element_text(face = "bold", size = 13),
    plot.subtitle = element_text(color = "grey40", size = 10.5),
    plot.caption  = element_text(color = "grey50", size = 8, hjust = 0),
    legend.position = "top",
    panel.grid.minor = element_blank(),
    axis.text.x = element_text(angle = 45, hjust = 1)
  )

The Herfindahl–Hirschman Index (HHI) is a standard measure of market concentration: a value of 1.0 means one institution receives everything; values below 0.10 indicate broad dispersion. SFI’s early years (2000–2003) show very high HHI values, an artifact of the small number of initial grants and the fact that a handful of large Principal Investigator awards went almost entirely to TCD, UCD, and UCC. As the portfolio grew through the 2000s and 2010s, more institutions entered the picture and concentration fell. The dashed blue line confirms the same story from a different angle: TCD and UCD together captured over 60% of commitments in some early years, declining to around 40–45% in the mature phase. The answer to the research question is therefore nuanced — SFI did become less concentrated over time as a system, but the same two institutions remained structurally dominant throughout, never falling below roughly 35% of annual commitments combined.

Takeaway

Science Foundation Ireland’s 24-year grant history reveals a dual narrative. At the level of the whole system, funding dispersed as the agency matured: more institutions competed successfully and the extreme concentration of the early 2000s gave way to a broader distribution. But at the top of the hierarchy, Trinity College Dublin and University College Dublin proved remarkably durable. Their combined share never collapsed; it moderated. This pattern is consistent with a model in which competitive excellence funding does, over time, allow challengers to enter, but cannot easily displace the compounding advantage of institutions that were already well-resourced at the outset. When SFI closed its doors in 2024, the landscape it left behind was more plural than the one it inherited — but still decisively shaped by the same two institutions that dominated its founding awards.
