Background

Golem Grad is a small rocky island in Lake Prespa, on the border between North Macedonia, Albania, and Greece. It holds one of the densest known populations of Hermann’s tortoises (Testudo hermanni) anywhere in Europe, and over sixteen years of capture–recapture work Ljiljana Tomović and colleagues documented something unusual: the island’s sex ratio is extraordinarily male-biased, and the island’s females are in trouble. The 2026 Ecology Letters paper by the Tomović group reports that island females are smaller, reproduce less frequently, produce smaller clutches, and die younger than females on the neighbouring mainland. The New York Times covered the paper under the headline “Constant Sexual Aggression Drives Female Tortoises to Walk Off Cliffs.” The authors project, from sixteen years of demography, that the last island female will die in 2083.

The mechanism proposed is specific. Island males so vastly outnumber females that every female is repeatedly pursued and mounted; copulatory injuries are severe; harassed females flee onto the plateau’s sheer limestone edges and fall. Under this account, the cost of island life is not evenly distributed between the sexes. It is borne, almost entirely, by females.

TidyTuesday has now released two of the study’s underlying datasets: a body-condition file covering sixteen years of individual measurements, and a clutch-size file covering reproductive output.

Motivation & Research Question

A “harsh-island” story and a “male-harassment” story both predict that island tortoises will be in worse condition than mainland ones. They diverge on one specific point: whether the penalty falls on both sexes, or on females only. If harsh environment is the driver, island males should look about as thin as island females. If male harassment is the driver, island males should look fine and the penalty should concentrate in the female half of the population.

Research question: Is the island condition penalty sex-specific — concentrated in females, as the paper’s harassment mechanism predicts, rather than borne equally by both sexes?

  theme_minimal(base_size = 13) +
  theme(
    plot.title    = element_text(face = "bold", size = 14),
    plot.subtitle = element_text(color = "grey40", size = 11),
    plot.caption  = element_text(color = "grey50", size = 8, hjust = 0),
    strip.text    = element_text(face = "bold", size = 12),
    panel.grid.major.x = element_blank(),
    panel.grid.minor   = element_blank()
  )

The figure shows the distribution of the body condition index (BCI) for each sex–locality combination, with violin shapes giving the shape of the distribution and boxplots summarising the median and interquartile range. The male panel shows condition distributions that sit essentially on top of one another: Beach, Plateau, and (where observed) Konjsko males occupy the same range. Male condition is not noticeably worse on the island than on the mainland. The female panel tells a different story. The island distributions (Beach and Plateau) are shifted visibly downward relative to the Konjsko females, and the lower tail of the Beach distribution in particular extends further down than anything seen in the male panel. The data cannot prove that harassment is the causal mechanism, but the pattern matches the specific prediction of that mechanism: condition loss that is concentrated on one side of the sex divide rather than distributed across the island population as a whole.

The second view takes the question from “what do females look like” to “what do they produce.” Each point is a single observed clutch. Two features emerge. The most obvious is position: Konjsko (mainland) females cluster in the upper right of the plot at 1,400–2,900 grams, producing 4–11 eggs per clutch, while the island females sit in the lower left at 500–1,700 grams, producing 1–5 eggs. The gap is not marginal — island clutches are on the order of half the size of mainland clutches, and island females themselves are roughly half the body mass of mainland females of the same species. The second, subtler feature is that within each locality the body-mass-to-eggs relationship has a similar positive slope. This suggests that the island penalty is not a different reproductive physiology so much as the result of island females being prevented, in the ordinary course of their lives, from ever reaching the body mass at which mainland females are reproducing.

Takeaway

The two panels together describe a single population-level pattern. Island males show condition distributions essentially indistinguishable from mainland males where both are observed; island females do not. Island females are lighter, in poorer condition, and produce clutches roughly half the size of mainland clutches of the same species. The cost of Golem Grad is real and it is falling, overwhelmingly, on one side of the sex divide.

None of this establishes causation on its own — capture-recapture data cannot, by construction, rule out confounders like differences in habitat productivity between the mainland and the island plateau. But a generic “harsh island” hypothesis would predict a penalty shared across both sexes, and that is not what the data show. The pattern in the open data matches the specific footprint of the paper’s proposed mechanism: a condition loss and a fecundity loss that are sex-specific rather than population-wide. Whether or not Tomović and colleagues are right that the last island female dies in 2083, the asymmetry they describe is already visible in the sixteen years of measurements they have left behind.
