Background

The WHO Global Health Expenditure Database, maintained since 2000, is the closest thing the development community has to a universal ledger of how the world pays for health. For 195 countries and territories, it records every dollar of health spending each year, decomposed by source — domestic government, domestic private households and firms, or external donors — and by financing scheme, the mechanism that channels the money through. The framework it encodes is not neutral. Since a landmark 2010 World Health Report, the WHO has argued that countries move, or ought to move, through a predictable fiscal transition in health: as states grow richer and their administrative capacity strengthens, government health spending rises as a share of total health expenditure, households’ out-of-pocket (OOP) burden falls, and foreign aid eventually tapers off. Thailand in the 2000s is the canonical demonstration. Rwanda after 1994 is the case studied most intensely by Indermit Gill and the Economic Growth Center. Ghana, Vietnam, Turkey are the other names people cite.

The empirical question — against which all three of my graduate advisors would push me to hold any optimistic claim — is whether the transition is actually occurring at scale, or whether it is a success story reconstructed from a small number of cases and a mostly-flat global average. For most poor countries, the absence of government capacity to run a tax-funded health system is not a transient problem. The OOP share sits high, donor aid fills a fraction of the gap, and when donors leave (as PEPFAR and the Global Fund increasingly have, under the 2025 US aid suspension) the gap falls back onto households. The data released this week covers exactly the period in which this claim is testable.

Motivation & Research Question

The fiscal transition makes a specific, testable prediction. In any given country, over time, one should see the government share of total health expenditure rising and the household OOP share falling. Countries should sort themselves along this trajectory by income level: richer countries further along it, poorer countries still at the starting point but moving in the same direction. A transition that is not happening looks different. The shares stay roughly constant at the country level; the global picture is dominated by new aid flows that replace, rather than catalyse, domestic spending; and the households of the poorest countries continue carrying a share of health costs that households in the richest countries have not carried for half a century.

Research question: Across 195 countries and 24 years, is the fiscal transition in health financing — the expected shift from out-of-pocket toward pooled government financing — actually visible in the data, or does the out-of-pocket burden on households in low-income countries sit structurally unchanged?

The four panels read, from left to right, as four successive stages of economic development. In the left-most panel, the median low-income country derives roughly 20 percent of its health spending from government, 50 to 60 percent from private sources (the vast majority of which is direct out-of-pocket payment from households), and the rest from external aid that peaks around 2010 and has drifted down since. Across the twenty-four years of the record, the government share rises modestly; the private share barely moves; the external share declines without a corresponding increase in domestic government revenue taking its place. In the right-most panel, high-income countries sit at roughly 70 percent government, 30 percent private, and less than 1 percent external aid — a composition they reached before the period in this record began. The two middle income groups sit between these two states. The upper-middle group has visibly moved toward the high-income profile over the twenty-four years; the lower-middle group’s trajectory is much flatter. The ordering is exactly what the fiscal-transition thesis predicts. The speed of movement, for the countries that most need it, is not.

The second view zooms in on the single most consequential share in the composition — out-of-pocket. Each point is one country, with its OOP share at the start of its observed record on the horizontal axis and at the end on the vertical. Countries on the dashed 45-degree line ended where they started; countries below the line cut their OOP reliance; countries above it pushed it up. The country-label highlights are deliberately chosen. Rwanda (RWA) and Thailand (THA) are the textbook cases of the fiscal transition working: both began with over 30 percent OOP reliance and cut it dramatically through, respectively, community-based health insurance (Mutuelles de Santé) and the 30-Baht universal coverage scheme. Turkey (TUR) and China (CHN) sit in the same family. But Nigeria (NGA), India (IND), Bangladesh (BGD), Afghanistan (AFG) are the other side of the picture: OOP shares in the 60–80 percent range at the start, in the 60–80 percent range at the end. Among the low-income countries in the left-most panel, only Rwanda and a handful of others have moved meaningfully below the dashed line. For most, a quarter-century of growth, donor engagement, and SDG-era rhetoric about universal coverage has left the household’s share of the health bill essentially untouched.

Takeaway

The WHO database tells a story that is, on its face, two stories. At the group level, the fiscal-transition thesis is validated — the ordering of country income groups along the trajectory from high-OOP to high-government-share is exactly as predicted, and the movement over twenty-four years is in the predicted direction. At the country level, inside the lowest-income group, the story is different. The gradient between income groups is real; the gradient within the low-income group, the slope of transition year-over-year, is mostly flat. A small number of countries — Rwanda being, for well-documented institutional reasons, the one most studied — have done the transition. Most have not.

The implication depends on what claim the data is being used to support. A claim that “development raises state capacity, which builds pooled health financing” is consistent with the data once countries graduate into middle-income status. A claim that “the fiscal transition is happening globally” is not consistent with the data for the poorest sixty-odd countries. And a claim that “donor exit will be filled by domestic revenue as countries grow richer” is, at least over a twenty-four-year window, the riskiest of the three. The households in the low-income panel of this figure are where the gap between that assumption and the data actually lives.

The four panels read, from left to right, as four successive stages of
economic development. In the left-most panel, the median low-income country
derives roughly 20 percent of its health spending from government, 50 to 60
percent from private sources (the vast majority of which is direct
out-of-pocket payment from households), and the rest from external aid that
peaks around 2010 and has drifted down since. Across the twenty-four years
of the record, the government share rises modestly; the private share
barely moves; the external share declines without a corresponding increase
in domestic government revenue taking its place. In the right-most panel,
high-income countries sit at roughly 70 percent government, 30 percent
private, and less than 1 percent external aid — a composition they reached
before the period in this record began. The two middle income groups sit
between these two states. The upper-middle group has visibly moved toward
the high-income profile over the twenty-four years; the lower-middle
group's trajectory is much flatter. The ordering is exactly what the
fiscal-transition thesis predicts. The speed of movement, for the countries
that most need it, is not.

and SDG-era rhetoric about universal coverage has left the household's
share of the health bill essentially untouched.

## Takeaway

The WHO database tells a story that is, on its face, two stories. At the
group level, the fiscal-transition thesis is validated — the ordering of
country income groups along the trajectory from high-OOP to
high-government-share is exactly as predicted, and the movement over
twenty-four years is in the predicted direction. At the country level,
inside the lowest-income group, the story is different. The gradient
between income groups is real; the gradient *within* the low-income group,
the slope of transition year-over-year, is mostly flat. A small number of
countries — Rwanda being, for well-documented institutional reasons, the
one most studied — have done the transition. Most have not.

The implication depends on what claim the data is being used to support.
A claim that "development raises state capacity, which builds pooled
health financing" is consistent with the data once countries graduate
into middle-income status. A claim that "the fiscal transition is
happening globally" is not consistent with the data for the poorest
sixty-odd countries. And a claim that "donor exit will be filled by
domestic revenue as countries grow richer" is, at least over a
twenty-four-year window, the riskiest of the three. The households in
the low-income panel of this figure are where the gap between that
assumption and the data actually lives.
