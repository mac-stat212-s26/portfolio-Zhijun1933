Background

Salmon farming is one of Norway’s largest export industries. Roughly 400 million farmed Atlantic salmon are grown in open-net pens along the country’s western and northern coasts at any given time, and the sector’s health and welfare record has become a visible public issue. The Norwegian Veterinary Institute now publishes monthly mortality and loss statistics from every licensed farm through its Laksetap (literally “salmon loss”) portal, and the Institute’s annual Fish Health Report frames the governance question that gives this dataset its stakes: the industry aspires to a 5 percent annual mortality ceiling, a figure repeatedly cited in policy and industry communications, but recent annual averages have sat closer to 15 percent. The transparency regime is serious — every farm, every month, publicly — but the target itself remains aspirational rather than enforced.

Two features of the data make it more than a simple mortality tracker. First, mortality is reported with quartiles across farms within each sea area, not just a mean, which exposes how uneven outcomes are between sites. Second, the separate losses dataset breaks down each month’s total fish disappearance into four categories: dead, discarded, escaped, and other. The headline “mortality rate” tracks only the first of these. The other three are real losses — welfare-relevant, environmentally relevant, commercially relevant — that the headline figure quietly leaves out.

Motivation & Research Question

A well-run regulatory regime delivers two things. It measures the outcome it claims to care about, and it measures it in a way that makes the full cost visible. Norwegian salmon mortality monitoring is a good test case. The industry has a publicly stated target (around 5 percent annual). The surveillance regime is dense and open. We can check, from the data alone, whether the target is being met, and whether the headline metric even captures what the public thinks it captures.

Research question: How close is the Norwegian salmon industry to its 5 percent annual mortality target, and what fraction of losses does the headline mortality figure fail to count?

Data Preparation

Show code
# Focus on salmon, at sea-area level (the finest geographic unit consistent
# across both datasets). Areas numbered 1-13 correspond to production zones
# along the Norwegian coast, running roughly south to north.
mort_salmon <- mortality |>
  filter(species == "salmon",
         geo_group == "area",
         !is.na(median)) |>
  mutate(
    year      = year(date),
    month     = month(date),
    # Put numeric areas in a sensible order; drop any multi-area aggregates
    area_num  = suppressWarnings(as.integer(region))
  ) |>
  filter(!is.na(area_num)) |>
  mutate(area = factor(area_num, levels = 1:13, labels = paste("Area", 1:13)))

# The 5% annual target, expressed as the equivalent monthly rate.
# Monthly equivalent ≈ 1 - (1 - 0.05)^(1/12) ≈ 0.427%
target_monthly <- 1 - (1 - 0.05)^(1/12)

# National losses (all Norway) for the decomposition view
losses_norway <- losses |>
  filter(species == "salmon", region == "Norge") |>
  select(date, dead, discarded, escaped, other) |>
  pivot_longer(c(dead, discarded, escaped, other),
               names_to = "category", values_to = "fish") |>
  mutate(
    category = factor(category,
                      levels = c("dead", "other", "discarded", "escaped"),
                      labels = c("Dead", "Other", "Discarded", "Escaped"))
  )

The heatmap reads each row as a production area, running from Area 1 (the southernmost, outside Agder) at the top down to Area 13 (Finnmark, at the Russian border) at the bottom. Each cell is one area-month; its colour is the median mortality rate across farms that month. Blue cells sit below the monthly equivalent of the 5 percent annual target (≈0.42%); the palette turns through pale yellow at the target and into red for rates above it. Two patterns are immediately visible. First, vertical red bands appear every autumn — August through November — across most of the coast, reflecting the combined stress of warm-water events, de-lousing treatments, and the autumn harvest. Second, the southern and west-central areas (3 through 6, Vestland and Trøndelag) carry darker, more persistent red, while some far-northern areas (Area 13, Finnmark) run cooler more of the year. The dominant impression, however, is red. Of all area-months in the data, roughly four in five exceed the monthly equivalent of a 5 percent annual mortality target. The target is, on the evidence of its own regulator’s data, not close to being met.

The second view reframes the question from “how many fish die?” to “what counts as a loss?” Each month, the Norwegian Veterinary Institute reports four categories of lost fish. Dead is the category the public mortality figure tracks. Discarded refers to fish culled or rejected as unfit for sale — welfare-relevant but not a natural death. Escaped refers to fish that break out of their pens into the wild, a genetic and ecological concern for wild Norwegian salmon populations. Other is a residual category that includes, among other things, mass-casualty events that the Institute’s reporting system has not yet sorted into the above three. The stacked area makes the composition visible. In a typical month, dead accounts for 85 to 95 percent of total losses; the remaining 5 to 15 percent comes from the three other categories. But the share is not stable. Periodic spikes, particularly in the warmer months of 2020 and again in 2023–24, push the non-dead share above 20 percent as other and discarded jump — spikes that would vanish from view if the headline metric were the only one published.

Takeaway

A governance regime can be judged on two things: whether its stated target is being met, and whether its chosen metric measures the right thing. Norway’s salmon mortality monitoring gives a clean test on both counts. The target is not being met — by the Veterinary Institute’s own data, median monthly mortality sits above the 5 percent annual equivalent in roughly four out of every five area-months, and a pronounced autumn peak repeats each year across almost the entire coast. The metric, for its part, is incomplete. It tracks dead fish, not lost fish. The gap is small in an average month but non-negligible, and the months when it matters most — mass-casualty events and welfare cullings — are exactly the months the public most needs to see.

None of this diminishes the transparency of the regime itself, which is unusually open and granular by international standards; the data needed to audit the claim is in this file. The claim itself, about whether the industry is approaching a welfare threshold it has publicly committed to, does not survive that audit. A target that is exceeded 80 percent of the time, and a metric that systematically undercounts a fraction of losses, are together compatible with sincere intent, rising expectations, and a long gap still to close — but they are not compatible with a picture in which the problem is already in hand.
