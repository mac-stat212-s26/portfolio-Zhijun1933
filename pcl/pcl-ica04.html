Three concepts the video assumes you internalize

Datum vs projection are not the same thing. A datum is a model of Earth’s shape (typically an ellipsoid plus a reference origin); a projection is a transformation from that ellipsoid to a flat plane. WGS84 is a datum; Mercator is a projection. Talking about “the WGS84 projection” is technically wrong even though everyone does it. The distinction matters when you’re debugging — st_set_crs(data, 4326) declares the datum/coordinate system; if the underlying coordinates are actually in Web Mercator meters, that declaration silently corrupts the data.

Every projection distorts something. Mercator preserves shape and angle but inflates polar areas to absurd sizes (Greenland looks bigger than Africa even though Africa is ~14× larger). Albers Equal Area preserves area but distorts shape. Lambert Conformal preserves shape locally but distorts area. There is no projection that preserves both — this is a mathematical fact (Gauss’s Theorema Egregium). When choosing a CRS for a map, the question is “which distortion is least harmful for what this map is trying to show?”

st_set_crs() and st_transform() look similar but do opposite things. st_set_crs() labels coordinates with a CRS without changing them — used when the CRS metadata was lost or never set. st_transform() actually reprojects the coordinates from one CRS to another — the numerical values change. Confusing them produces silent wrong answers: st_set_crs() on data already labeled with a CRS overwrites the label without recomputing, putting your polygons in completely wrong locations on a map.

EPSG codes worth memorizing

The five codes that come up constantly in a US-centered spatial workflow:

  • 4326 — WGS84 lon/lat. The default for almost everything that gets shared globally; what GPS uses; what Leaflet expects.
  • 3857 — Web Mercator. The CRS Google Maps and most slippy-tile web maps use for display.
  • 4269 — NAD83 lon/lat. The CRS that most US Census data ships in.
  • 2163 — US National Atlas Equal Area. A reasonable choice for projecting the Lower 48 when you want equal-area distortion.
  • 5070 — NAD83 / Conus Albers. Another equal-area projection optimized for the contiguous US, increasingly the recommended default.

sf in one sentence

A sf object is a regular tibble where one column holds geometry (POINTs, LINESTRINGs, POLYGONs, MULTIPOLYGONs) and the whole tibble carries one CRS attribute — which means every dplyr verb you already know works on it, plus a layer of st_* functions that do the spatial operations (intersect, buffer, distance, area). The Moodle checkpoint will likely test exactly this collapse: spatial data in R is not a special data structure, it is regular data plus one careful piece of metadata.
