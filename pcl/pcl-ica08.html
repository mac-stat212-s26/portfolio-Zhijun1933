Three concepts the chapter quietly hinges on

x == NA doesn’t work; is.na(x) does. This is the most-bitten gotcha in all of R. NA == NA evaluates to NA, not TRUE, because R’s logic is “we don’t know what either is, so we don’t know if they’re equal.” Same for x == NA. The only way to test for missingness is is.na(x), which is a special predicate that returns TRUE/FALSE (never NA). Every wrangling beginner writes filter(df, x == NA) once, gets an empty result, and is confused; this is why.

filter() silently drops rows where the condition is NA. filter(df, x > 0) keeps rows where x > 0 is TRUE, drops rows where it’s FALSE, and also drops rows where it’s NA (because NA > 0 is NA, which filter() treats as “not TRUE”). This means filtering on a column with missing values silently shrinks your dataset in ways you might not notice. The defensive pattern: if you suspect missing values matter, use filter(df, x > 0 | is.na(x)) to keep NA rows explicitly, or check count(df, is.na(x)) first to see how many would be dropped.

Implicit missingness is the invisible kind that bites the hardest. If you have monthly sales data and February’s row is just missing — not NA, but absent — every plot, every summary, every mean() will silently treat the dataset as 11-month instead of 12-month, with no warning. The detection move: build an expected universe (expand(df, year, month) or full_seq() for numeric ranges), then anti_join() your data against it to find what’s absent. Or use complete(df, year, month) to fill in the missing rows with NA so they become explicit and countable. After surfacing, you have to decide why — was Feb skipped? Did the system go down? Was the product not yet launched? — and that decision determines whether to drop, impute, or flag.

A small decision tree

For most missing-data wrangling, this captures the reasoning:

Symptom Move
Want to count missingness in each column colSums(is.na(df)) or naniar::miss_var_summary(df)
Want to keep only complete rows drop_na()
Want to keep rows where specific cols are non-NA drop_na(col1, col2)
Want to replace NA with a constant replace_na(list(col = 0)) inside mutate()
Want to carry a value forward (e.g., last observed) fill(col, .direction = "down")
Want NA to be a visible category in a plot fct_na_value_to_level() on the factor
Want to find rows that should exist but don’t complete() or anti_join() against template
Computing a mean and getting NA back add na.rm = TRUE to the summary

Why this comes after joins

ICA 08 follows ICA 07 (joins) for a reason: the moment you start joining tables, missingness becomes structural rather than incidental. A left_join produces NA in the new columns whenever the lookup fails — and those NAs are diagnostic of mismatches between key sets, not just sensor failures. Knowing how to handle missing data is therefore part of knowing how to debug a join. The two skills compose: surface the implicit missingness, decide its meaning, then drop or impute or flag accordingly.

A note on the deeper question

Ch18 is operational — how to detect and handle missingness. It deliberately doesn’t get into the statistical question of why it’s missing (MCAR vs MAR vs MNAR — missing completely at random, missing at random, missing not at random) or what imputation strategy is justified for each. That’s downstream territory, picked up in mice and other imputation packages. The R4DS chapter is the prerequisite layer: you need to see the missingness clearly before you can reason about its mechanism. ICA 08 builds that layer.
