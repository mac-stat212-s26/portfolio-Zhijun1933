Pre-class learning task

ICA 19’s prep is structurally different from every prior PCL: instead of readings to synthesize, it’s a critique task. Nathan Yau’s Counting Happiness and Where It Comes From (FlowingData, July 2021) is a published data story analyzing the HappyDB corpus — 100,000 happy moments from 10,000 Mechanical Turk participants, parsed into subject-verb-object triples. The job is to read it as a designer, not as a consumer: identify the story, identify what works, identify what could be stronger. The mindmap below organizes the piece itself plus the three critique axes the assignment asks for, ending with what this exercise teaches about quality control as the closing skill of STAT 212.

What is the data story

The piece argues — with structure rather than overt claim — that happiness in daily life splits into two grammatical channels: things people do for themselves (I watched a movie, I had dinner) and things others do that arrive as relational gifts (husband surprised me, son gave hug). The Sankey-style flow diagrams are not the argument; they’re the evidence display. The argument is that when you parse 100,000 self-reports of happy moments by subject-verb-object, the verbs and objects in the relational column trend small and ordinary — meals, gifts, hugs — rather than grand gestures. The closing line — “sometimes you have to refocus your attention to see the moments more clearly” — is the persuasive payload: happiness is structurally available, you just need to see it.

The pets sub-view operates as a third channel that confirms the same logic at finer resolution — when the relational counterpart is a pet, the verbs become specific and physical (snuggled, jumped, curled). Same story, sharper instance.

What is effective

Method transparency at the right granularity. Yau explains the SVO parsing in two sentences with one concrete example before showing any results. The reader can immediately replicate the mental move — “I watched a movie” → S=I, V=watched, O=movie — and trust what follows. Many published data stories skip this; the absence shows up later as confusion about what the chart is actually counting.

Honest sample disclosure. The Mechanical Turk caveat — that participants skew 20–40 years old, which inflates spouse and child mentions and depresses workplace mentions — is not buried in a footnote. It explicitly tells the reader what would change with a different sample. This converts a methodological weakness into a strength: the reader leaves trusting the analyst, not the dataset.

Form fits content. SVO triples have a natural left-to-right flow, and the Sankey-like rendering makes that flow legible. The gray-and-accent palette directs attention without overpowering. The top-5 lists in plain text before the diagrams give readers an anchor — they know what they’re about to see in graphical form.

Restraint in interpretation. Yau narrates the patterns (“Generally speaking, it’s about someone making time for another”) without claiming causal mechanisms. Given that this is observational self-report data, this restraint is appropriate; many less careful data stories would have over-claimed.

What could be improved

Magnitude information is missing from the rankings. The top-5 lists give ordinal information (rank 1, rank 2, …) but no counts or proportions. Without that, the reader can’t tell whether dinner (rank 1) is 5× or 1.05× more frequent than lunch (rank 5). For a story about what makes people happy, the size of the gap between #1 and #5 is itself part of the answer. A small inline bar with each rank — even just visual length — would carry magnitude without disrupting the layout.

The bias caveat appears after the main argument. The note that the MTurk sample skews 20–40 years old, increasing spouse/child mentions and depressing workplace ones, is a real interpretive constraint — but it’s placed below the analysis, after the reader has already formed conclusions. One sentence at the top — “the sample skews 20–40, which shapes what relational categories appear” — would frame the reading without undercutting it. Knaflic’s storytelling principle would put the constraint in the setup, not the resolution.

Long-tail data is invisible. Top-5 lists by definition show 5 of however-many distinct triples exist. With 100k moments and SVO parsing, the long tail almost certainly contains interesting structure — rare but coherent clusters of moments that the top-5 framing erases. A static piece is constrained here; ICA 18’s lessons about when interactivity earns its keep apply: this is exactly the case where a search/filter affordance would add real value, because the reader’s question (“does my kind of happiness appear?”) isn’t answered by aggregate ranks.

No comparative null. What does the same SVO parsing look like on a sadness corpus, or a frustration corpus? Without that comparison, it’s hard to tell what’s specifically about happiness versus what’s about how humans attribute attention to events generally. The piece is strong on its own terms; a comparative lens would have made it stronger.

Time is absent. When were these moments collected? Was it pre- or post-pandemic? Any seasonality? Aggregate-over-time analysis can hide drift, and for an emotion corpus that’s a substantive concern.

Three takeaways for my own work

Put method transparency before findings, not after. Yau does this and it’s the cleanest move in the piece. One paragraph naming what was done and how — concretely, with an example — earns the rest of the reader’s attention.

Frame sample bias in the opening, not the notes. Bias caveats placed after the analysis read as defensive footnotes; placed before, they read as honest framing. The same sentence does very different work depending on where it lives.

Show magnitude, not just rank. Ordinal lists are easy and accessible, but they hide gradient. Even a minimal visual encoding of count or proportion — a tiny inline bar — converts a list into a chart that respects the reader’s intelligence.

Why quality control is the closing skill

ICA 19 is the last ICA of STAT 212 deliberately. By this point you can wrangle, iterate, acquire, visualize statically, and add interaction. The remaining skill — the one that’s hardest to teach because it requires slow reading of finished work — is critique. The course’s earlier ICAs taught what should be in a chart; ICA 19 trains the muscle of asking what shouldn’t be there, what’s missing, what could be clearer. This is the skill that distinguishes a data scientist who produces work from one whose work others trust.

Critique is also reciprocal: every good chart you’ll ever make benefits from someone else applying this same lens before it goes public. ICA 19 is rehearsal for that role. The Yau piece was chosen well — it’s published, it’s by a respected practitioner, and it’s good enough that the criticisms have to be substantive rather than corrective. Critiquing weak work teaches little; critiquing strong work teaches what the next level of strength looks like.

Closing thought

The piece’s own closing line — “sometimes you have to refocus your attention to see the moments more clearly” — applies recursively to data design. The chart that looks finished often has structural choices the maker has stopped seeing. Quality control is the practice of refocusing — looking at the chart as if you’d never seen it, asking whether the magnitudes are visible, whether the framing is honest, whether the reader can answer their own questions from what’s there. STAT 212 has spent a semester teaching how to make charts; ICA 19 closes by teaching how to read them. The two are the same skill seen from opposite sides.
